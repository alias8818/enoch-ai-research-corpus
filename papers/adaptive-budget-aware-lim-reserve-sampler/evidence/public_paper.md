## Abstract

We present an adaptive budget- and confidence-aware reserve sampling strategy for mitigating the Lost-in-the-Middle (LIM) effect in retrieval-augmented generation pipelines. Prior fixed-reserve antidote samplers allocate a static proportion of context budget to middle-positioned evidence, which improves answer quality at moderate budgets but reverses gains under tight context constraints. The proposed method, `pack_adaptive_antidote`, estimates budget capacity from candidate chunk lengths and retrieval confidence from score gaps, falling back to standard top-k packing when capacity is insufficient and enabling a confidence-trimmed middle reserve otherwise. On a public SQuAD extractive QA harness (64 cases, 2 query variants, 3 budget levels, 1152 total model calls on NVIDIA GB10 CUDA), the adaptive sampler yields +3.39 exact-match points and +3.56 F1 points over top-k source-order packing, and +3.13 exact-match points and +3.47 F1 points over the fixed-reserve parent, while eliminating the tight-budget regression observed in the fixed-reserve baseline. Evidence recall improves by +1.56 points over top-k and +5.21 points over the fixed reserve. These results are moderate in strength: the evaluation uses a single extractive QA dataset with a compact model, and external replication on broader RAG workloads has not been performed.

## 1. Introduction

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems assemble context windows from retrieved document chunks before passing them to a language model. A well-documented phenomenon—the "Lost-in-the-Middle" (LIM) effect—is that language models attend less effectively to information positioned in the middle of long contexts, favoring content at the beginning and end. Prior work on LIM antidote samplers addresses this by reserving a fraction of the context budget for middle-positioned evidence, promoting those chunks from their natural retrieval rank to more attended positions.

However, fixed-reserve strategies introduce a budget sensitivity problem. When the context budget is generous, reserving space for middle evidence is beneficial; when the budget is tight, the same reservation displaces higher-ranked evidence and can reduce answer quality. The parent project for this work demonstrated exactly this pattern: a fixed LIM antidote sampler improved F1 at moderate budgets but degraded it at tight budgets relative to simple top-k packing.

This paper reports on an adaptive variant that conditions the reserve on two runtime signals: (1) budget capacity, estimated from the cumulative token lengths of candidate chunks relative to the budget ceiling, and (2) retrieval confidence, estimated from score gaps among retrieved chunks. The method falls back to top-k packing under tight budgets or low confidence, and activates a confidence-trimmed reserve otherwise. We evaluate against both top-k source-order packing and the parent fixed-reserve sampler on a public SQuAD harness and report results with honest treatment of limitations.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Background: Fixed LIM Antidote Sampler

The parent project implements a fixed-reserve antidote sampler that allocates a static fraction of the context window to middle-positioned chunks. Given a ranked list of retrieved chunks, the sampler promotes chunks from the middle rank positions into reserved slots near the beginning or end of the context, where model attention is empirically higher. This approach improves answer quality when the context budget is large enough to absorb the reservation without displacing high-ranked evidence, but it causes regressions when the budget is tight.

### 2.2 Adaptive Budget-Aware LIM Reserve Sampler

The adaptive variant, implemented as `pack_adaptive_antidote`, introduces two conditioning signals:

**Budget capacity estimation.** The sampler computes the cumulative token length of candidate chunks and compares it to the context budget ceiling. If the total candidate length is close to or exceeds the budget, the sampler classifies the setting as "tight" and deactivates the reserve, falling back to top-k source-order packing. This prevents the displacement of high-ranked evidence under constrained budgets.

**Retrieval confidence estimation.** The sampler examines score gaps among retrieved chunks. Large gaps between top-ranked and lower-ranked chunks indicate high retrieval confidence (the top chunks are clearly distinguished), while small gaps indicate low confidence (chunks are nearly interchangeable). When confidence is low, the sampler reduces the reserve strength, on the rationale that promoting ambiguous middle chunks provides less benefit.

**Combined gating.** The reserve is activated only when both budget capacity is sufficient and retrieval confidence exceeds a threshold. When activated, the reserve strength is trimmed by the confidence signal: higher confidence permits a stronger reserve, while lower confidence attenuates it. This two-factor gating is the core mechanism that eliminates the tight-budget regression of the fixed-reserve parent while preserving moderate-budget gains.

### 2.3 Fallback Behavior

Under very tight budgets or very low confidence, the adaptive sampler produces output identical to top-k source-order packing. This fallback is by design: it ensures the adaptive method never performs worse than the baseline in the regimes where the fixed reserve causes regressions.

## 3. Experimental Setup

### 3.1 Harness

The evaluation uses a public SQuAD (Stanford Question Answering Dataset) extractive QA harness inherited from the parent project, extended to evaluate three strategies in a single run: top-k source-order packing, parent fixed LIM antidote, and adaptive budget/confidence LIM reserve.

### 3.2 Configuration

- **Cases:** 64 SQuAD development examples
- **Query variants:** 2 per case
- **Budget levels:** 90, 130, and 170 tokens
- **Strategies:** top-k, fixed LIM antidote, adaptive LIM reserve
- **Total model calls:** 64 × 2 × 3 × 3 = 1152
- **Hardware:** NVIDIA GB10 GPU (CUDA)
- **Software stack:** CUDA PyTorch, Transformers 4.57.6, psutil; environment managed via `uv` in a project-local `.venv`
- **Unit tests:** 8/8 passed prior to benchmark execution

### 3.3 Metrics

- **Exact Match (EM):** binary indicator of whether the predicted answer matches any reference answer exactly
- **F1:** token-level overlap F1 between prediction and best matching reference
- **Evidence recall:** fraction of ground-truth supporting evidence chunks present in the packed context
- **Mean tokens:** average number of tokens in the packed context
- **Total latency:** end-to-end latency per sample (ms)

### 3.4 Kill Condition

A branch-specific kill condition was defined before evaluation: adaptive reserve gating would be abandoned if it failed to beat both baseline top-k ordering and the best fixed-reserve parent setting on SQuAD EM/F1 at tight and moderate budgets, or if gains came only from weakening retrieval or context constraints.

### 4.2 Adaptive vs. Top-k

The adaptive sampler improves over top-k source-order packing by +3.39 EM points, +3.56 F1 points, and +1.56 evidence-recall points, while using 0.78% fewer tokens and reducing mean latency by 0.483 ms.

### 4.3 Adaptive vs. Fixed Reserve

The adaptive sampler improves over the fixed LIM antidote by +3.13 EM points, +3.47 F1 points, and +5.21 evidence-recall points, while using 0.20% fewer tokens and reducing mean latency by 0.031 ms. The fixed reserve's evidence recall (0.9141) is notably below top-k (0.9505), indicating that the fixed reservation displaces relevant evidence. The adaptive method recovers and exceeds top-k's evidence recall.

At budget 90, the adaptive sampler matches top-k performance and substantially outperforms the fixed reserve, which suffers from its tight-budget regression. This budget-dependent behavior is the central empirical finding: the adaptive gating successfully deactivates the reserve when it would hurt and activates it when it would help.

## 5. Limitations

1. **Single-dataset evaluation.** All reported metrics come from a SQuAD extractive QA harness with 64 cases. SQuAD is a short-answer extractive task; the LIM effect and the benefit of reserve sampling may differ substantially on generative, multi-hop, or long-form QA tasks.

2. **Compact model.** The evaluation uses a compact model on a single GPU. Results may not transfer to larger models with different attention patterns or to models specifically trained for long-context handling.

3. **No external replication.** The experiments were executed once, within a single automated pipeline run. No independent replication has been performed.

4. **Limited budget range.** Three budget levels (90, 130, 170 tokens) span tight-to-moderate regimes but do not explore very large budgets where the LIM effect may manifest differently.

5. **Confidence estimation is heuristic.** The retrieval confidence signal is derived from score gaps among retrieved chunks. This heuristic has not been validated against alternative confidence measures, and its behavior under distribution shift in retrieval scores is unknown.

6. **Fixed-reserve parent had mixed results.** The parent project's fixed LIM antidote showed only marginal aggregate improvement over top-k (+0.26 EM, +0.09 F1) with a notable evidence-recall regression (−3.64 points). The adaptive method's gains are measured relative to this weak baseline; the absolute improvement over top-k, while meaningful, rests on a comparison where the fixed reserve itself was nearly neutral.

7. **Real-QA artifacts present but unreported.** The artifact manifest includes `real_qa_benchmark` files and a `real_qa_sweep/` directory, but the run notes do not report metrics from these artifacts. Their status and findings are not incorporated into this paper.

8. **Automated provenance.** This draft and the underlying experiments were produced by an automated research pipeline. No human has independently verified the experimental code, data processing, or metric computation.

## 7. Conclusion

The adaptive budget-aware LIM reserve sampler addresses a concrete failure mode of fixed-reserve antidote samplers—tight-budget regression—by conditioning the middle-evidence reservation on runtime estimates of budget capacity and retrieval confidence. On the tested SQuAD harness, the adaptive method improves over both top-k source-order packing and the fixed-reserve parent across all reported metrics, with the most practically important finding being the elimination of the tight-budget F1 regression while preserving moderate-budget gains.

The evidence strength is moderate. The evaluation is confined to a single extractive QA dataset, a compact model, and one hardware configuration. The adaptive method's confidence estimation is heuristic and unvalidated under distribution shift. The real-QA benchmark artifacts in the project directory have not been analyzed in the run notes. These constraints bound the generality of the findings.

The project decision recommends finalizing this branch as supported, with future work directed toward larger external RAG validation rather than additional fixed-reserve variants. This recommendation is consistent with the evidence: the adaptive method resolves the identified failure mode within the tested setting, but its behavior under broader conditions remains an open question.
