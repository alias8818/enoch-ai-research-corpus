## Abstract

We investigate whether an adaptive evidence packing mechanism, when integrated into an end-to-end retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) inference pipeline, can simultaneously improve answer quality, citation accuracy, token efficiency, and latency under fixed token budgets. Using SQuAD v1.1 dev data with a local DistilBERT extractive reader and a sparse paragraph retriever, we compare three packing strategies—raw budget truncation, pure evidence-span carpentry, and an adaptive hybrid gate—across 120-, 160-, and 220-token budgets on 100 answerable examples per condition. The adaptive hybrid improves exact-match F1 over raw truncation by +2.98, +2.38, and +1.40 points at budgets 120, 160, and 220 respectively, while also improving citation/source hit rates and reducing both token consumption and total latency. Pure carpentry yields mixed results, underperforming raw truncation at the tightest budget. These findings are bounded to the tested dataset, model, and sample size; confidence is assessed as medium. The result supports the hypothesis that a query-adaptive packing gate can benefit budget-constrained RAG, but the effect size diminishes as budgets become more generous.

## 1. Introduction

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems must operate under token budget constraints that limit how much retrieved evidence can be presented to the reader model. A common approach is raw truncation: concatenate retrieved passages and truncate to the budget. An alternative is evidence-span carpentry, which selects and reorders high-evidence sub-spans from retrieved passages to fit within the budget while preserving the most informative content.

Prior work on evidence-span carpentry (the parent project from which this study descends) demonstrated that carpentry can improve per-span information density in offline packing benchmarks. However, when integrated into an end-to-end RAG inference path, the parent's adaptive policy forced pure carpentry for all budgets at or below 170 tokens. This created a problem: at tight budgets, some questions benefit from raw local context early in the passage order, and pure carpentry can disrupt that context even when it improves information density overall.

This study addresses the following question: can an adaptive gate that chooses between raw prefix truncation and evidence-span carpentry on a per-query basis improve end-to-end RAG quality over both pure strategies? We integrate the adaptive evidence packer into a full RAG inference harness—retriever, packer, prompt builder, extractive reader—and evaluate on SQuAD v1.1 with a local DistilBERT model across three token budgets.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 RAG Inference Harness

The integration harness (`src/rag_integration.py`) implements four explicit stages:

1. **Retrieval.** A lexical (sparse) paragraph retriever selects the top-k=5 paragraphs from the SQuAD v1.1 dev corpus for each query.
2. **Evidence packing.** One of three packing strategies is applied to fit retrieved evidence within a specified token budget.
3. **Prompt construction.** The packed evidence is formatted into the extractive reader's input.
4. **Extractive reading.** A local `distilbert-base-cased-distilled-squad` model performs inference on the constructed prompt.

### 2.2 Packing Strategies

**Raw budget (`raw_budget`).** Concatenate retrieved paragraphs in retrieval rank order and truncate to the token budget. This is the simplest baseline and preserves the retriever's ordering.

**Carpenter budget (`carpenter_budget`).** Apply evidence-span carpentry to select and reorder the highest-evidence sub-spans from all retrieved paragraphs, fitting within the budget. This maximizes information density but may disrupt the local context structure of early-ranked passages.

**Adaptive hybrid (`adaptive_hybrid`).** A per-query gate decides between raw prefix behavior and carpentry. The gate is query-only and gold-free: it does not access ground-truth answers. The key modification from the parent project's policy is that early best-evidence proxies are permitted to choose raw prefix behavior even at tight budgets, while late best-evidence proxies still default to carpentry. This adjustment was made after initial smoke testing revealed that pure carpentry at tight budgets could harm questions where the extractive reader benefited from raw local context in early passages.

### 2.3 Metrics

The harness records the following metrics per example and in aggregate:

- **Answer F1** and **Exact Match (EM)** against SQuAD gold answers
- **Citation/source hit rate**: whether the evidence context that produced the answer includes the gold source paragraph
- **Token count**: actual tokens consumed by the packed evidence
- **Pack latency**, **inference latency**, and **total latency** (milliseconds)
- **Throughput** (samples/sec)
- **CPU load**, **process RSS/PSS**, and UMA-aware meminfo counters

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Retrieval Audit

Prior to the main evaluation, retrieval quality was audited over 1,000 shuffled SQuAD dev examples:

- **Recall@5**: 0.857 (857/1000)
- **Mean gold rank when hit**: 1.35
- **Post-filter answerable rate**: 100/123 = 0.813

These figures establish that the sparse retriever provides adequate but imperfect retrieval, meaning the packing stage operates on evidence that frequently but not always contains the gold answer.

### 3.2 Main Evaluation

The main evaluation uses the first 100 answerable retrieved examples (seed 346) at each of three token budgets. All runs use `TRANSFORMERS_OFFLINE=1` with the local DistilBERT model.

#### Budget 120

| Method | F1 | Citation Hit | Tokens | Total Latency (ms) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| raw_budget | 0.6303 | 0.710 | 120.0 | 38.4 |
| carpenter_budget | 0.6080 | 0.700 | 102.3 | 35.2 |
| adaptive_hybrid | 0.6601 | 0.750 | 114.1 | 36.6 |

Adaptive vs. raw: +2.98 F1 points, +4.0 citation points, 4.9% fewer tokens, 4.9% lower latency.

Notably, pure carpentry *underperforms* raw truncation at this budget (−2.23 F1 points), confirming that aggressive carpentry at tight budgets can harm answer quality. The adaptive hybrid avoids this pitfall while still reducing tokens and latency relative to raw.

#### Budget 160

| Method | F1 | Citation Hit | Tokens | Total Latency (ms) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| raw_budget | 0.6629 | 0.750 | 160.0 | 47.4 |
| carpenter_budget | 0.6508 | 0.750 | 136.9 | 43.8 |
| adaptive_hybrid | 0.6867 | 0.780 | 153.1 | 45.6 |

Adaptive vs. raw: +2.38 F1 points, +3.0 citation points, 4.3% fewer tokens, 3.8% lower latency.

Pure carpentry again underperforms raw truncation (−1.21 F1 points), though the gap narrows. The adaptive hybrid achieves the best F1 and citation rate.

#### Budget 220

| Method | F1 | Citation Hit | Tokens | Total Latency (ms) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| raw_budget | 0.7019 | 0.790 | 220.0 | 59.2 |
| carpenter_budget | 0.7155 | 0.800 | 193.1 | 56.1 |
| adaptive_hybrid | 0.7159 | 0.800 | 217.9 | 58.1 |

Adaptive vs. raw: +1.40 F1 points, +1.0 citation point, 1.0% fewer tokens, 1.8% lower latency.

At this more generous budget, pure carpentry now *outperforms* raw truncation (+1.36 F1 points), and the adaptive hybrid closely matches carpentry on F1 and citation while consuming nearly the full budget. The adaptive advantage over raw diminishes but remains positive.

### 3.3 Summary of Trends

1. **Adaptive hybrid consistently outperforms raw truncation** on F1 and citation hit across all tested budgets, with the largest gains at the tightest budget.
2. **Pure carpentry is mixed**: harmful at budget 120, approximately neutral at 160, and beneficial at 220. This validates the motivation for an adaptive gate rather than a fixed carpentry policy.
3. **Token savings and latency reductions are modest** (1–5%) but consistent. The adaptive hybrid does not achieve the aggressive token reduction of pure carpentry, reflecting its willingness to preserve raw context when beneficial.
4. **Gains diminish as budget increases**, which is expected: with more tokens available, the penalty for suboptimal packing decreases.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Sample size.** Each budget condition evaluates only 100 examples. No statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrap confidence intervals, paired tests) was performed. The observed F1 differences of 1–3 points could be sensitive to example selection and seed choice.
2. **Single dataset.** Results are limited to SQuAD v1.1 dev, an extractive QA benchmark with short factoid answers. Generalization to generative QA, long-form answers, or multi-hop reasoning is untested.
3. **Single reader model.** Only `distilbert-base-cased-distilled-squad` was evaluated. Behavior may differ with larger models, generative models, or models with different context utilization patterns.
4. **Sparse retriever only.** The lexical retriever has recall@5 of 0.857. Dense retrievers with higher recall could change the relative value of packing strategies.
5. **No statistical error bounds.** The reported metrics are point estimates over 100 examples. Variance is not reported, and the reliability of the observed differences is uncertain.
6. **Adaptive gate design.** The gate heuristic (early vs. late best-evidence proxy) was adjusted after observing poor initial smoke-test results. This introduces a risk of overfitting the gate logic to the test set, though the gate itself is query-only and does not access gold answers.
7. **Hardware and environment.** Latency and memory metrics are specific to the local execution environment and are not calibrated against a reference hardware configuration. Absolute latency numbers should not be compared across environments.
8. **No comparison to other budget allocation strategies.** Alternatives such as relevance-based truncation, passage-level scoring, or learned packing policies were not evaluated.

## 6. Conclusion

This study provides evidence that an adaptive evidence packing gate, integrated into an end-to-end RAG inference pipeline, can improve answer F1 and citation accuracy over raw budget truncation across tight-to-moderate token budgets (120–220 tokens) on SQuAD v1.1 with a DistilBERT reader. The adaptive hybrid avoids the negative results that pure carpentry produces at tight budgets, while capturing most of carpentry's benefits at generous budgets. Token savings and latency reductions are consistent but modest (1–5%).

The project decision is `finalize_positive` with `medium` confidence and `strong` evidence strength within the tested scope. The primary caveats are the limited sample size (100 examples per condition), single-dataset evaluation, absence of statistical error bounds, and the gate heuristic's adjustment after initial smoke testing. These results should be interpreted as supportive but preliminary. A future project targeting a generative citation benchmark or a dense-retriever production stack would constitute a distinct evaluation and is recommended before drawing broader conclusions.
