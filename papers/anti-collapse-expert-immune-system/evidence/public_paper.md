## Abstract

Recursive training on model-generated data has been shown to cause progressive distributional collapse, with disproportionate loss in rare or tail phenomena. We investigate whether a controller architecture—an "expert immune system"—combining immutable provenance tracking, real-data anchoring, diverse expert verification, and quarantine of suspect outputs can mitigate this collapse. Using a synthetic categorical-distribution simulator across 40 random seeds and 24 generational cycles, we compare four scenarios: unfiltered recursive synthetic replacement, expert quarantine alone, real-data anchoring alone, and the full immune system (anchor plus experts). The immune system reduced final Jensen-Shannon divergence from the true distribution by 61.9% versus the unfiltered baseline and by 8.4% versus anchoring alone. Expert quarantine without real-data anchoring produced negligible improvement over baseline (Jensen-Shannon divergence reduction < 0.1%). Rare-mode retention improved from 1.9% to 11.0% of true rare mass under the immune system, but remained severely degraded relative to the original distribution. These results are limited to a toy categorical simulation and do not constitute validation on production language model pipelines. We report the negative finding that internal expert critique alone, absent external or real-data anchors, is ineffective in this setting, consistent with prior work on the limitations of unaided self-correction.

## 1. Introduction

Large language models increasingly consume synthetic data—outputs from themselves or other models—as training or fine-tuning inputs. Shumailov et al. (Nature, 2024) demonstrated that indiscriminate recursive training on model-generated data leads to progressive distributional narrowing and eventual collapse, with tail phenomena disappearing first and irreversibly. Gerstgrasser et al. (arXiv:2404.01413, 2024) showed theoretically and empirically that accumulating real data alongside synthetic data can avert collapse, but the conditions under which this holds at scale remain incompletely characterized.

Separately, multiagent debate and self-critique methods have been proposed as mechanisms for improving model outputs. Du et al. (arXiv:2305.14325, 2023) reported factuality and reasoning improvements from multiagent debate, while Huang et al. (arXiv:2310.01798, ICLR 2024) found that language models cannot reliably self-correct reasoning without external feedback. Gou et al. (arXiv:2305.11738, ICLR 2024) demonstrated that tool-interactive critiquing outperforms introspective self-correction. These findings suggest that verification mechanisms differ substantially in their effectiveness depending on whether they incorporate external information.

We pose the question: can a controller that combines provenance tracking, real-data anchoring, diverse expert verification, and quarantine of suspect outputs plausibly reduce recursive collapse compared with unfiltered self-consumption? We term this controller an "expert immune system" by analogy to biological immune systems that distinguish self from non-self and quarantine threats while preserving host function.

We make the following contributions:

1. A minimal synthetic simulator for studying recursive collapse under controlled interventions.
2. A four-way ablation comparing baseline, experts-only, anchor-only, and combined immune-system configurations.
3. A negative result: expert critique without external anchoring is ineffective in this setting.
4. A provisional design specification for a next-stage prototype on real language model tasks.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Simulator Design

We implemented a synthetic recursive-data simulator (`scripts/immune_collapse_sim.py`) modeling a true data distribution with 10 categorical modes. The distribution includes a rare tail with total mass of approximately 0.0597 (5.97% of total probability). A finite-capacity generator is repeatedly fit from accepted data and resampled over multiple generational cycles.

The simulator tracks the following metrics per generation:

- **Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence** from the true distribution.
- **Entropy** of the generated distribution.
- **Rare-mode mass**: total probability assigned to rare tail modes.
- **Head-mode mass**: probability concentrated in the most probable modes.

This simulator is a toy model. It abstracts away the complexity of language model token distributions, attention mechanisms, and training dynamics in favor of a controlled setting where collapse interventions can be isolated and measured. Results from this simulator establish plausibility only and should not be extrapolated to production systems without further validation.

### 2.2 Scenarios

Four scenarios were tested:

1. **Baseline recursive synthetic** (`baseline_recursive_synthetic`): No anchors, no expert quarantine. Each generation's training data consists entirely of outputs from the previous generation's model.

2. **Experts only, no anchor** (`experts_only_no_anchor`): A committee of expert critics evaluates and quarantines suspect outputs, but no real-data anchor is injected. Experts share the same training lineage and lack independent external information.

3. **Real anchor only** (`real_anchor_only`): 25% of each generation's training data is drawn from the original true distribution (real-data anchor), with no expert quarantine on the remaining synthetic portion.

4. **Immune anchor + experts** (`immune_anchor_plus_experts`): 25% real-data anchor combined with expert committee quarantine of suspect synthetic outputs.

### 2.3 Experimental Protocol

A smoke test was run with 3 seeds over 8 generations to verify simulator behavior. The full experiment used 40 random seeds over 24 generational cycles. Results were aggregated as means across seeds. No variance estimates (standard deviations or confidence intervals) were computed in the summary metrics; the full per-seed results are available in `results/immune_collapse_sim.json` for independent analysis.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Final Jensen-Shannon Divergence

| Scenario | Final JS Divergence (mean across 40 seeds) |
|---|---:|
| Baseline recursive synthetic | 0.3545 |
| Experts only, no anchor | 0.3543 |
| Real anchor only | 0.1474 |
| Immune anchor + experts | 0.1351 |

The immune system (anchor + experts) reduced final JS divergence by 61.9% relative to the unfiltered baseline and by 8.4% relative to the anchor-only condition.

### 3.2 Expert Quarantine Without Anchors

The experts-only scenario produced a JS divergence of 0.3543, a reduction of less than 0.07% from the baseline value of 0.3545. This difference is negligible and within expected noise for 40 seeds. Expert critique without external anchoring did not materially mitigate collapse.

This constitutes a clear negative result: a committee of critics sharing the same training lineage and lacking independent external information provides no meaningful protection against recursive distributional collapse.

### 3.3 Rare-Mode Retention

| Scenario | Rare Retention (% of True Rare Mass) |
|---|---:|
| Baseline recursive synthetic | 1.9% |
| Immune anchor + experts | 11.0% |

The immune system improved rare-mode retention by a factor of approximately 5.8× relative to baseline. However, even the immune system retained only 11.0% of the original rare-mode probability mass—a severe degradation from the true distribution's 5.97% tail mass. Rare-tail collapse was mitigated but emphatically not solved.

### 3.4 Anchor Contribution Decomposition

Comparing the four scenarios reveals that the real-data anchor accounts for the vast majority of the collapse mitigation. The additional expert quarantine layer provides a modest but measurable improvement (8.4% further JS divergence reduction beyond anchoring alone). The experts-only condition confirms that internal critique, absent external information, is insufficient.

The decomposition suggests a qualitative ordering: real-data anchoring is the primary mechanism, expert quarantine is a secondary refinement, and expert quarantine without anchoring is effectively inert.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Toy simulation scope.** The simulator models a 10-mode categorical distribution with a finite-capacity generator. Real language model distributions are vastly higher-dimensional, and collapse dynamics may differ qualitatively. These results establish plausibility only, not efficacy.

2. **No real LLM validation.** No experiments were conducted on actual language model pipelines, benchmark tasks, or production trace corpora. Scientific closure requires validation on tasks with ground truth (e.g., factual QA, code generation, mathematical reasoning).

3. **Simplified expert model.** The "experts" in this simulator are modeled as committee members sharing the same training lineage. Real expert diversity (different architectures, training corpora, tool access) may yield different results, but this was not tested.

4. **Rare-tail preservation remains poor.** Even the best-performing condition retained only 11% of true rare-mode mass. The immune system mitigates but does not solve tail collapse.

5. **Single anchor fraction.** Only a 25% real-data anchor fraction was tested. The relationship between anchor budget and collapse mitigation was not characterized.

6. **No variance reporting.** Summary metrics report means across 40 seeds but do not include standard deviations or confidence intervals, limiting the statistical interpretability of the reported differences.

7. **No cost/latency analysis.** The computational overhead of provenance tracking, expert verification, and quarantine was not measured, as the simulator does not model these costs realistically.

8. **No false accept/reject characterization.** The simulator does not separately track verifier false positive and false negative rates, which would be critical in a production system.

9. **Notion page inaccessible.** The project's Notion page returned HTTP 404 from the execution environment, so the run relied solely on the mission prompt for intent specification. Any additional context that may have been present on that page was unavailable.

## 6. Conclusion

A controller combining real-data anchoring, provenance tracking, diverse expert verification, and quarantine of suspect outputs plausibly reduces recursive collapse in a synthetic categorical setting, achieving a 61.9% reduction in distributional divergence versus unfiltered recursive replacement. However, this finding is limited to a toy simulation and does not constitute validation for production language model systems.

The central negative finding—that expert critique without external or real-data anchoring is ineffective—aligns with prior work showing that unaided self-correction fails and that tool-interactive or externally-grounded verification is necessary. This result has direct design implications: any "immune system" for AI data pipelines must incorporate immutable real-data anchors and must not rely solely on internal model critique.

Rare-mode preservation remains a critical unsolved problem. Even under the best tested condition, only 11% of true rare-tail mass was retained after 24 generational cycles. This suggests that anchor budgets and verification mechanisms may need to be substantially stronger, or that certain tail phenomena require explicit protection strategies beyond those tested here.

We provisionally designate this approach as viable for a next-stage prototype, subject to validation on real LLM benchmarks with ground-truth evaluation, cost/latency measurement, and explicit characterization of verifier error rates. An experts-only system without provenance, real-data anchors, and tool-backed verification should be considered not viable based on both the literature and the local ablation.
