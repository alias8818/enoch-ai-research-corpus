## Abstract

Conventional curriculum design often defaults to exhaustive, encyclopedia-style topic coverage, which evidence suggests contributes to curriculum overload and shallow learning outcomes. This paper examines the design viability of an "Anti-Encyclopedia Curriculum" (AEC) — a curriculum architecture that rejects exhaustive topic-list coverage as its organizing unit and instead structures learning around generative questions, prerequisite knowledge maps, guided inquiry, worked examples, retrieval practice, spaced review, and transfer tasks. We formalize AEC design principles, construct an evidence-derived 28-point rubric from seven learning-science source categories, and score the architecture against that rubric. Under the interpretation that "anti-encyclopedia" means anti-exhaustive-coverage and anti-passive-reference-traversal, the AEC achieves a design viability score of 27/28, exceeding the pre-specified minimum viable threshold of 21. Under the alternative interpretation — anti-knowledge or minimally-guided discovery — the architecture would score substantially below the viability threshold (estimated 10–14/28). However, the 27/28 score reflects structural alignment with established learning-science principles, not demonstrated learner outcomes. No experimental, quasi-experimental, or pilot learner data were collected. The claim ledger for this artifact was audited as blocked with empty claims, meaning no structured claims passed formal audit. Efficacy closure requires a controlled A/B pilot measuring delayed retention and transfer.

## Introduction

Curriculum design faces a persistent tension between breadth and depth. The OECD has documented curriculum overload across multiple education systems, where "mile-wide, inch-deep" coverage pressure leads to superficial treatment of topics and limited opportunity for the learning strategies most strongly supported by cognitive science. The "Anti-Encyclopedia Curriculum" (AEC) concept responds to this tension by proposing that curriculum should not be organized as an exhaustive reference traversal, but rather around generative problems, prerequisite knowledge structures, and evidence-backed learning mechanisms.

This paper does not report learner outcome data. Instead, it addresses a prerequisite question: is the AEC concept structurally viable as a curriculum architecture — that is, does it coherently incorporate principles with established empirical support, and does it avoid known failure modes? We treat this as a design viability analysis, not an efficacy study.

A critical ambiguity in the AEC concept must be resolved at the outset. "Anti-encyclopedia" could be interpreted in at least two ways:

1. **Anti-exhaustive-coverage**: Rejecting the encyclopedia's organizational logic (alphabetical or topical exhaustiveness) in favor of problem-organized, knowledge-mapped, guided learning sequences.
2. **Anti-knowledge / minimal guidance**: Rejecting structured knowledge transmission in favor of unguided discovery or pure constructivist exploration.

These interpretations lead to opposite viability predictions. The first aligns with evidence on guided inquiry, worked examples, and integrated knowledge structures. The second conflicts with substantial evidence that minimally guided approaches harm novice learners. We test both interpretations against the evidence-derived rubric and report results for each.

The analysis is therefore grounded in the project title ("Anti-Encyclopedia Curriculum") and the mission prompt rather than any private design details that may exist in the Notion document. This is a significant limitation that readers should weigh when assessing the completeness of the interpretation.

### Rubric Construction

From the evidence matrix, we derived a 28-point rubric (`artifacts/aec_rubric.json`) with criteria organized into the following domains:

- **Coverage restraint**: Does the architecture limit topic breadth in favor of depth on generative problems?
- **Knowledge mapping**: Does the architecture make prerequisite structures and integrated knowledge schemas explicit?
- **Guidance adequacy**: Does the architecture provide direct instruction, worked examples, and scaffolding sufficient for novice learners?
- **Retrieval and spacing**: Does the architecture incorporate retrieval practice and spaced review?
- **Transfer orientation**: Does the architecture include transfer tasks and varied practice contexts?
- **Inquiry calibration**: Is any inquiry component adequately guided rather than minimally guided?
- **Overload mitigation**: Does the architecture address curriculum overload through structural means, not merely exhortation?

Each criterion was scored as 0 (absent/contradicted), 0.5 (partially present), or 1 (present and evidence-aligned). The minimum viable score was set at 21/28 (75%), representing a threshold above which the architecture is structurally coherent with established learning-science principles. This threshold was pre-specified in the project decision record.

### Interpretation Testing

The rubric was applied under both interpretations of "anti-encyclopedia":

- **Interpretation A (anti-exhaustive-coverage)**: AEC preserves knowledge maps, direct guidance, worked examples, and structured schemas while rejecting exhaustive topic lists.
- **Interpretation B (anti-knowledge / minimal guidance)**: AEC removes structured knowledge transmission in favor of discovery-based exploration.

## Results

### Design Viability Score

Under Interpretation A (anti-exhaustive-coverage), the AEC architecture scored **27/28**. The single point not awarded was in a sub-criterion concerning explicit assessment of delayed retention at the curriculum level — the architecture design permits this but does not mandate it as a structural requirement in the current template version.

Under Interpretation B (anti-knowledge / minimal guidance), the AEC architecture would score substantially below the viability threshold. Criteria in the guidance adequacy, knowledge mapping, and inquiry calibration domains would receive scores of 0, yielding an estimated score in the range of 10–14/28, well below the 21-point minimum. This estimate is approximate because the scoring script was not re-run under Interpretation B; the range reflects manual assessment of which criteria would change.

### Evidence Alignment Summary

The evidence supports the following design features of the AEC under Interpretation A:

- **Reducing exhaustive coverage** is supported by OECD overload evidence and by the well-established finding that shallow coverage undermines durable learning.
- **Explicit knowledge maps** are supported by research on integrated knowledge structures and schema formation.
- **Guided inquiry** (not pure discovery) is supported by meta-analytic evidence that inquiry benefits learners only when guidance is adequate.
- **Retrieval practice and spacing** are among the most robustly supported learning strategies in the cognitive science literature.
- **Worked examples** are supported for novice learners, with a transition to independent problem-solving as expertise develops.
- **Transfer tasks** are necessary because transfer does not emerge automatically from domain learning.

The evidence does not support:

- Removing direct instruction for novice learners.
- Organizing curriculum as unguided exploration.
- Treating reference-style exhaustive coverage as a learning sequence.

### Negative and Mixed Results

Several findings qualify the positive design viability score:

1. **No learner outcome data.** The 27/28 score reflects structural alignment with evidence-backed principles, not demonstrated effects on learning. A curriculum architecture can be structurally sound and still fail in implementation due to teacher capacity, student motivation, assessment misalignment, or institutional constraints.

## Limitations

- **No experimental or quasi-experimental learner data were collected.** The design viability score is a necessary but insufficient condition for curriculum efficacy. The next step — an A/B learner pilot comparing a conventional topic-list unit with an AEC unit on immediate learning, delayed retention, and transfer — has not been conducted.

- **The evidence synthesis is secondary.** We relied on published reviews, meta-analyses, and practice guides rather than conducting primary meta-analytic computation. Effect sizes were not independently recomputed.

- **Generalizability is untested.** The AEC template was not piloted across different subject domains, age groups, or educational contexts. Its applicability beyond the design stage is unknown.

- **Scoring subjectivity.** Although the rubric specifies binary and partial-credit scoring, judgment is involved in determining whether a design feature is "present and evidence-aligned." A different scoring team might yield a different score.

- **No implementation fidelity data.** Even if the design is viable, implementation fidelity in real classrooms is a separate and typically more difficult challenge.

## Conclusion

The Anti-Encyclopedia Curriculum concept is design-viable under the interpretation that it opposes exhaustive topic-list coverage while preserving explicit knowledge maps, direct guidance, worked examples, retrieval practice, spacing, and transfer-oriented assessment. It scored 27/28 on a locally derived evidence-aligned rubric, exceeding the minimum viability threshold of 21. Under the alternative interpretation — opposing structured knowledge transmission in favor of minimal guidance — the concept is not viable, consistent with substantial evidence that minimally guided approaches harm novice learners.

This result is a design-stage finding, not an efficacy result. The architecture's effect on actual learner outcomes — including delayed retention and far transfer — remains unknown and requires a controlled pilot. The AEC unit template and scoring rubric are provided as reusable artifacts to support such a pilot.

The formal claim ledger for this artifact is in a blocked state with zero extracted claims, meaning the design viability score has not passed structured claim-level audit. This is an honest representation of the current evidence state: the architecture is structurally coherent with learning-science principles, but no claim about its real-world effects has been formally audited.

The central practical implication is that curriculum reform aimed at reducing coverage overload must be carefully distinguished from curriculum reform aimed at reducing guidance. The former has structural support from learning science; the latter does not. The same anti-encyclopedia rhetoric can serve either goal, and the difference is consequential for learners.
