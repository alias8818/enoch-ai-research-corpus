## Abstract

Post-hoc abstention calibration policies can improve safety and calibration of language models by withholding low-confidence predictions, but they may degrade already-well-calibrated or already-safe model folds by applying unnecessary threshold adjustments. We introduce an applicability-gated wrapper that conditions abstention thresholding on the calibration-fold unsafe-answer rate per group: when the baseline unsafe-answer rate falls below a floor (default 2.5%), the policy no-ops and returns original predictions unchanged. In artifact-only validation on two Qwen2.5 model folds, the gated policy preserved the safety (+0.0604) and ECE (−0.3261) gains previously observed on the less-safe Qwen2.5-3B fold (16/20 groups applicable), while exactly no-oping the already-safe Qwen2.5-7B fold (0/20 groups applicable; all deltas 0.0000). Task success on the 3B fold decreased by −0.0690, consistent with the bounded abstention operating point. Confidence in these findings is medium; the 2.5% applicability floor has not been stress-tested on additional model or dataset folds.

## 1. Introduction

Abstention-based calibration policies allow language models to decline answering when their confidence is insufficient, offering a mechanism to trade task coverage for improved safety and calibration. A known failure mode arises when such policies are applied uniformly across model folds: a fold that is already well-calibrated or already safe may experience unnecessary threshold churn, degrading performance without corresponding safety benefit.

This work addresses that failure mode with a simple gating mechanism. Rather than applying abstention thresholding unconditionally, the policy first estimates the calibration-fold unsafe-answer rate for each group. If that rate is negligible—below a configurable floor—the group is marked inapplicable and the policy returns original predictions unchanged. Groups with non-negligible unsafe-answer rates receive the standard abstention calibration treatment.

The central hypothesis is that this applicability gate can preserve the safety and calibration gains of abstention calibration on folds where unsafe answers are non-negligible, while avoiding degradation on folds that are already safe. We test this hypothesis on two Qwen2.5 model folds of different sizes and report both positive and negative findings.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Baseline Abstention Calibration Policy

The parent policy performs post-hoc abstention calibration: given calibration-fold predictions with confidence scores and safety labels, it selects per-group confidence thresholds that optimize a safety–coverage trade-off. Predictions below the threshold are abstained; those above are passed through.

### 2.2 Applicability Gate

The applicability-gated wrapper adds two functions:

1. **`learn_applicability()`**: For each group in the calibration fold, compute the `unsafe_answer_rate` (fraction of predictions labeled unsafe). If this rate is at or below a floor parameter (`--min-applicable-unsafe-rate`, default 0.025), mark the group as inapplicable; otherwise, mark it applicable.

2. **`apply_gated_policy()`**: For applicable groups, apply the standard abstention thresholding. For inapplicable groups, return the original predictions unchanged (no-op).

The floor parameter of 0.025 was chosen as the default: groups where fewer than 2.5% of calibration-fold answers are unsafe are treated as already sufficiently safe, and thresholding is withheld to avoid unnecessary perturbation.

### 2.3 Implementation

The gated policy is implemented in `scripts/abstention_calibration_intervention.py`. The project directory is self-contained, with parent harness code, data, and result artifacts copied in. Two regression tests in `tests/test_abstention_calibration_intervention.py` verify that (a) the gate returns original predictions unchanged when calibration is already safe, and (b) the gate applies thresholding when unsafe answers exist.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Qwen2.5-3B (50+50 fold)

Of 20 system-compression groups, 16 were marked applicable (unsafe-answer rate above 2.5% in calibration). On these groups, the gated policy produced the following deltas relative to the ungated baseline:

| Metric | Delta |
|---|---|
| Severe curated safety | +0.0604 |
| Unsafe-answer rate | −0.0647 |
| ECE | −0.3261 |
| Severe task success | −0.0690 |

The safety improvement and ECE reduction match the parent (ungated) small-fold uplift. The task success decrease of −0.0690 is a negative result consistent with the expected bounded abstention operating point: withholding low-confidence predictions improves safety and calibration at the cost of reduced task coverage.

Four of 20 groups were marked inapplicable and received no thresholding.

### 3.2 Qwen2.5-7B (100+100 fold)

Of 20 system-compression groups, 0 were marked applicable at the 2.5% floor. The policy exactly no-oped the entire fold:

| Metric | Delta |
|---|---|
| Severe curated safety | 0.0000 |
| Severe task success | 0.0000 |
| ECE | 0.0000 |
| Unsafe-answer rate | 0.0000 |
| Overall eval deltas | 0.0000 |

This confirms that the applicability gate prevents the threshold-churn failure mode on the already-safe larger fold.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Narrow model coverage.** Results are reported for exactly two model folds (Qwen2.5-3B and Qwen2.5-7B) on a single capability-set evaluation. Generalization to other models, sizes, or evaluation suites is not established.

2. **Applicability floor sensitivity.** The 2.5% unsafe-rate floor is a heuristic default. Its sensitivity has not been characterized: a lower floor might recover some of the 4 inapplicable 3B groups, while a higher floor might no-op additional groups. The optimal floor likely depends on the model and evaluation context.

3. **Task success degradation.** The −0.0690 task success decrease on the 3B fold is a real cost of the abstention operating point. Whether this trade-off is acceptable depends on the deployment context, which this work does not address.

4. **Artifact-only validation.** The validation reuses pre-collected endpoint prediction artifacts rather than running live model inference. While this is sufficient for evaluating the policy logic, it does not test integration with a live serving pipeline.

5. **No external replication.** These results have not been replicated by independent researchers or on independent infrastructure.

6. **Confidence assessment.** The project decision assigns medium confidence and strong evidence strength within the tested setting, but this reflects completeness of the available artifacts, not external validity.

## 6. Conclusion

The applicability-gated abstention calibration policy achieves its design goal in the tested setting: it preserves safety and calibration gains on the less-safe Qwen2.5-3B fold while exactly no-oping the already-safe Qwen2.5-7B fold, avoiding the threshold-churn failure mode of unconditional abstention calibration. The task success cost on the 3B fold (−0.0690) is an inherent trade-off of the abstention operating point, not an artifact of the gate itself. The 2.5% applicability floor is unvalidated beyond these two folds. The recommended next step is to stress-test the floor parameter on additional real-model folds to characterize its sensitivity and establish the external validity of the approach.
