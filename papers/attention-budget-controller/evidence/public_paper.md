## Abstract

We investigate whether a lightweight online Attention Budget Controller (ABC) can improve fixed-budget token retention in long-context inference by dynamically splitting a constrained KV-cache budget between a recency rail (for local, recently streamed tokens) and a utility rail (for salient, repeatedly attended long-range facts). Using a synthetic cache-retention simulation across three workload types—pure recency, pure landmark, and nonstationary mixed-shift—we compare ABC against pure LRU, pure heavy-hitter, random retention, and static budget splits. ABC achieves the highest hit rate on nonstationary mixed-shift workloads at budgets 64, 128, and 512, and is within 0.003 of the best policy at budget 256, with improvements over LRU ranging from +0.116 to +0.434 absolute hit rate. However, ABC is not a universal replacement for specialized policies: on pure recency workloads, LRU remains superior (ABC underperforms by 0.004–0.078); on pure landmark workloads, heavy-hitter retains a small edge at tight budgets (ABC underperforms by 0.006–0.081). These results are from a synthetic simulation measuring target-token availability, not from a real transformer inference pipeline. We conclude that ABC is a viable workload-adaptive controller for nonstationary access patterns, but its practical value depends on whether cheap utility signals can be exposed in real KV-cache backends.

## 1 Introduction

Long-context transformer inference faces a fundamental memory constraint: the KV cache grows linearly with sequence length, and at context lengths of 128k tokens or more, retaining all key-value states becomes infeasible. Recent work has explored KV-cache compression through adaptive budget allocation, attention-sparsity exploitation, and learned eviction policies. A common thread is that uniform or purely recency-based cache management leaves performance on the table—some tokens merit retention for their long-range utility, while others are only relevant transiently.

The central tension is between two retention strategies:

1. **Recency-based retention** (sliding window / LRU): retains the most recently accessed tokens, which is optimal when queries predominantly target local context but discards potentially important distant facts.

2. **Utility-based retention** (heavy-hitter / salience): retains tokens with high cumulative attention mass, which preserves important long-range information but may evict recently relevant local context under tight budgets.

Neither strategy dominates across all workload types. In practice, workloads are often nonstationary—shifting between recency-heavy and landmark-heavy query patterns over the course of a long inference session.

We propose the **Attention Budget Controller (ABC)**, a lightweight online controller that dynamically splits a fixed KV-cache budget between a recency rail and a utility rail. ABC uses exponential-moving-average (EMA) / Hedge-style reward estimates to adjust the split online, clamped so that both rails always retain at least a minimum allocation.

This paper presents results from a synthetic cache-retention simulation. We do not claim these results generalize to real transformer inference without further validation.

## 2 Method

### 2.1 Problem Formulation

We model fixed-budget KV retention as an online cache selection problem. Given a sequence of $N$ tokens and a budget $B \ll N$, a retention policy must decide which $B$ token slots to retain in the cache at each time step. A query arrives targeting a specific token; the hit rate (fraction of queries where the target token is retained) is the evaluation metric.

### 2.2 Policies

We implement and compare the following policies:

- **`lru_recent`**: Pure sliding-window recency. Retains the $B$ most recently accessed tokens.
- **`salience_heavy_hitter`**: Pure utility retention. Retains the $B$ tokens with the highest cumulative observed attention mass (heavy-hitter salience).
- **`random_keep`**: Random fixed-size retention baseline. Selects $B$ tokens uniformly at random.
- **`static_split_0.25`, `static_split_0.50`, `static_split_0.75`**: Fixed budget splits allocating 25%, 50%, or 75% of the budget to the recency rail, with the remainder to the utility rail.
- **`abc_adaptive_split`**: The proposed controller. Uses EMA/Hedge-style reward estimates computed from recent hit rates on each rail to adjust the recency-vs-utility split online. Both rails are clamped to retain a minimum allocation, ensuring neither rail is fully emptied.

### 2.3 Workloads

Three workload types simulate different query access patterns:

- **`recency`**: Queries predominantly target recently streamed tokens. This workload favors LRU retention.
- **`landmark`**: Queries target sparse, salient long-range facts. This workload favors heavy-hitter retention.
- **`mixed_shift`**: Nonstationary workload with phase shifts—starting recency-heavy, transitioning to landmark-heavy, then mixing both patterns. This workload tests adaptivity.

### 2.4 Experimental Configuration

All experiments were run using a dependency-free Python harness (`scripts/attention_budget_experiment.py`) on an NVIDIA GB10 system (Linux aarch64, 127.5 GB RAM, no swap). The experiment script was verified via `python3 -m py_compile`.

A three-stage execution protocol was followed:

1. **Smoke test**: 1,000 tokens, 2 seeds, budgets 64/128, all three workloads.
2. **Calibration run**: 3,000 tokens, 5 seeds, budgets 64/128/256, all three workloads.
3. **Full run**: 8,000 tokens, 12 seeds, budgets 64/128/256/512, all three workloads.

### 3.2 Mixed-Shift Workload: ABC Advantage

On the nonstationary `mixed_shift` workload, ABC won or tied at budgets 64, 128, and 512. At budget 256, a static split policy narrowly led; ABC was within 0.003 of heavy-hitter and outperformed LRU by +0.381. The adaptive split allowed ABC to shift budget allocation as the workload transitioned between recency-heavy and landmark-heavy phases, which neither pure policy could do.

### 3.3 Recency Workload: LRU Remains Best

On pure recency workloads, LRU was the clear winner at all budgets. ABC's mandatory utility rail guardrail imposed a cost of 0.004–0.078 absolute hit rate, with the gap largest at tight budgets (64 slots) and negligible at generous budgets (512 slots). This is expected: when all queries target recent tokens, any budget diverted to long-range retention is wasted.

### 3.4 Landmark Workload: Heavy-Hitter Remains Best at Tight Budgets

On pure landmark workloads, heavy-hitter retention was superior at budgets 64, 128, and 256, with ABC underperforming by 0.006–0.081. At budget 512, ABC and heavy-hitter tied at 1.000 hit rate. ABC massively outperformed LRU (+0.492 to +0.864), confirming that recency-only retention is catastrophically mismatched to landmark workloads.

### 3.5 Control-Plane Overhead

Mean ABC selection overhead in the Python simulation was approximately 42–90 µs/token depending on budget and workload. This reflects Python interpreter overhead for the adaptive split computation and is not a fused-kernel cost estimate. A production implementation integrated into an attention kernel or KV-cache manager would likely incur substantially lower per-token overhead, but this has not been measured.

## 4 Limitations

1. **Synthetic simulation, not real inference.** This study uses a synthetic cache-retention benchmark that models token retention as an online cache selection problem. It does not run a real transformer, does not compute actual attention scores, and does not measure downstream text quality, perplexity, or task accuracy. The results should be interpreted as evidence about the *structural* properties of adaptive budget splitting, not as claims about real-model performance.

2. **Hit rate ≠ answer quality.** The evaluation metric (target-token availability / hit rate) measures whether the retained KV cache contains the token a query targets. It does not measure whether retaining that token actually improves generated output quality. In a real transformer, attention is distributed across many tokens, and the relationship between retaining a specific token and answer correctness is indirect.

3. **Simplified utility signals.** The simulation uses observable salience plus retained-token attention hits as the utility signal. A real implementation must expose cheap utility signals—such as attention mass, key norms, salience tags, or query-aware scores—from the inference backend. Whether such signals are available at acceptable cost is an open question.

4. **No real-model evaluation.** No LongBench, RULER, or needle-in-haystack evaluation was conducted. These benchmarks would be necessary to validate whether ABC's hit-rate advantages translate to meaningful improvements in downstream task performance.

5. **Workload design.** The three synthetic workloads (recency, landmark, mixed_shift) are simplified models of query access patterns. Real inference workloads may exhibit more complex or different nonstationary dynamics.

6. **Static split baselines.** The static split policies (0.25, 0.50, 0.75) provide useful reference points but are not tuned per-workload. A well-tuned static split for a known workload type would likely outperform the generic static splits tested here.

7. **No variance reporting.** The summary tables report mean hit rates across 12 seeds but do not include standard deviations or confidence intervals. Statistical significance of the observed differences has not been formally tested. The magnitude of some differences (e.g., ABC vs LRU on landmark at budget 64: +0.492) makes them unlikely to be due to chance, but smaller differences (e.g., ABC vs heavy-hitter on mixed_shift at budget 256: −0.003) may not be statistically significant.

8. **Budget and sequence length range.** Experiments cover budgets 64–512 on sequences of 8,000 tokens. Behavior at much larger budgets or much longer sequences (e.g., 128k tokens) has not been tested.

## 6 Conclusion

The Attention Budget Controller demonstrates that a lightweight online adaptive split between recency and utility retention rails can outperform both pure policies on nonstationary mixed workloads, with hit-rate improvements over LRU of +0.116 to +0.434 at budgets 64–512. However, ABC is not a universal replacement for specialized policies: on pure recency workloads, LRU remains superior; on pure landmark workloads, heavy-hitter retention retains a small edge at tight budgets.

These results come from a synthetic cache-retention simulation and do not constitute validation on real transformer inference. The practical viability of ABC depends on two conditions: (1) the target KV-cache backend can expose cheap utility signals (attention mass, key norms, or salience indicators) at inference time, and (2) the adaptive split overhead can be absorbed into the cache management path without introducing unacceptable latency. The recommended next step is to implement ABC as a pluggable policy in a real KV-cache eviction backend and evaluate against sliding-window, heavy-hitter, and adaptive budget allocation baselines on standard long-context benchmarks.
