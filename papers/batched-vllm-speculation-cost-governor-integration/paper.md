## Abstract

Speculative decoding in batched LLM serving can reduce latency but introduces wasted draft computation and KV-cache pressure when acceptance rates are low. We present a speculation-cost governor that adaptively caps per-request speculative-token limits, controls admission, and applies bounded dwell delays under KV pressure, integrated into vLLM's V1 scheduler via an OpenAI-compatible control contract (`vllm_xargs`). In synthetic concurrent serving benchmarks, the governor reduced wasted draft work by 2.5–4.7% and peak KV usage by 0.7–5.2% at the cost of 2.1–15.3% p95 latency regression and 5.8–11.2% throughput loss. In real patched-vLLM benchmarks on an NVIDIA GB10 system with SmolLM2-135M and ngram speculation, the governor achieved a 29.5% p95 latency reduction and 28.5% throughput improvement under KV/concurrency stress, and negligible overhead (+0.94% p95, −1.7% throughput) under dwell stress. A critical dwell-clock bug (epoch vs. monotonic time) was identified and fixed during integration. Results are bounded to the tested hardware, model, and concurrency levels; the governor's benefit is contingent on KV-pressure conditions and speculative acceptance rates.

## 1. Introduction

Speculative decoding accelerates LLM inference by drafting multiple candidate tokens in parallel and verifying them against a target model. In batched serving settings (e.g., vLLM, SGLang), speculative decoding interacts with KV-cache management, request scheduling, and admission control in ways that can degrade rather than improve throughput when acceptance rates are low or KV pressure is high. Wasted draft tokens consume KV-cache slots and compute without producing accepted output, creating a cost that existing serving stacks do not govern per-request.

This work investigates whether a lightweight, per-request speculation-cost governor—adaptively capping speculative-token limits, applying bounded dwell delays under KV pressure, and controlling admission—can reduce wasted draft work and KV pressure in batched vLLM serving without exceeding a 20% p95 latency regression threshold.

The governor emits its decisions through an OpenAI-compatible control contract (`vllm_xargs.spec_governor`) that patched vLLM schedulers consume to enforce per-request speculative caps and dwell deadlines. We evaluate the governor across three tiers of evidence: (1) in-process synthetic benchmarks, (2) HTTP endpoint benchmarks against a local OpenAI-compatible shim, and (3) real patched-vLLM benchmarks with a CUDA-backed model server.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Governor Design

The speculation-cost governor (`src/batched_spec_governor.py`) operates as a serving control-plane component that makes per-request decisions along three axes:

1. **Speculative-token cap.** The governor adaptively limits the number of draft tokens a request may propose, based on current KV pressure, recent acceptance rates (tracked via EWMA feedback), and request-level features (prompt length, expected output length).

2. **Admission control.** Under high KV pressure, the governor may deny admission to new speculative requests, allowing only non-speculative or already-admitted requests to proceed.

3. **Bounded dwell.** The governor may impose a dwell delay (bounded to at most one second) on waiting or running requests when KV pressure exceeds a threshold. Dwell deadlines are computed using `time.monotonic()` to avoid clock-skew bugs under concurrent scheduling.

The governor communicates decisions to the serving backend through an OpenAI-compatible contract. Two transport paths are supported:

- **Header transport:** `x-spec-governor-*` HTTP headers on the request.
- **Body transport:** `extra_body.spec_governor` JSON object, or `vllm_xargs.spec_governor` for vLLM's native extension path.

### 2.2 vLLM Scheduler Patch

A patch installer (`scripts/install_vllm_spec_governor_patch.py`) and unified diff (`patches/vllm_spec_governor_contract.patch`) modify three vLLM V1 source files:

- **`vllm/v1/request.py`:** Parses `vllm_xargs.spec_governor` from `SamplingParams.extra_args` into a `Request.spec_governor` attribute.
- **`vllm/v1/core/sched/scheduler.py`:** Applies per-request speculative-token caps to draft token scheduling lookahead and per-request KV allocation. Malformed caps fall back to the server default.
- **`vllm/v1/core/sched/async_scheduler.py`:** Applies bounded dwell by skipping waiting/running requests until their monotonic dwell deadline expires.

### 2.3 Benchmark Harnesses

Two benchmark harnesses exercise the governor at different integration levels:

**Synthetic harness** (`scripts/benchmark_batched_governor.py`): An in-process, dependency-free concurrent serving simulation that records p50/p95 latency, tokens/s, CPU, RSS, UMA `/proc/meminfo`, KV pressure/peak, waste ratio, dwell, and per-request control reasons. No real model or HTTP server is involved.

**Endpoint harness** (`scripts/benchmark_openai_governor_endpoint.py`): Sends concurrent HTTP requests to an OpenAI-compatible endpoint (either the local shim or a real vLLM server), records latency, throughput, CPU/RSS, UMA memory, waste ratio, dwell, and KV peak. For real vLLM servers, it queries `/metrics` to parse Prometheus speculative counters. Timeout and URL errors become per-request error rows rather than aborting the benchmark.

**Local shim** (`scripts/openai_spec_governor_shim.py`): A dependency-free HTTP server exposing `/models`, `/chat/completions`, and `/metrics`. It consumes the governor control contract from both `extra_body.spec_governor` and `vllm_xargs.spec_governor`, applies live speculative-token caps and bounded dwell in a concurrent KV-pressure scheduler simulation, and returns accepted/proposed/waste/KV metrics on the OpenAI-compatible response.

### 2.4 Contract Regression Test

`tests/test_vllm_spec_governor_patch_contract.py` exercises the patched vLLM code paths without requiring a live GPU engine, verifying:

- `Request` parses JSON `SamplingParams.extra_args["spec_governor"]` from the `vllm_xargs` path.
- Dwell is bounded to one second.
- Scheduler caps per-request speculative lookahead tokens and treats malformed caps as the server default.
- Scheduler dwell predicate expires at the expected deadline using `time.monotonic()`.

### 3.3 Real Patched-vLLM Benchmarks

These benchmarks run against a real patched vLLM V1 OpenAI server with CUDA execution on an NVIDIA GB10 system, using SmolLM2-135M-Instruct with ngram speculative decoding (4 speculative tokens).

**Critical bug fix.** Initial real-vLLM benchmarks with Torch 2.9.1+cu130 experienced client timeouts during generation despite the server remaining responsive. Two issues were identified: (1) Torch 2.9.1+cu130 warns that GB10 compute capability 12.1 exceeds its supported maximum (12.0), and (2) the dwell deadline was computed using epoch time rather than monotonic time, causing dwell predicates to never expire under concurrent scheduling. After switching to `time.monotonic()` and upgrading to Torch 2.11.0+cu130, both KV/concurrency and dwell stress benchmarks completed with zero timeouts.

The governor's adaptive cap reduction appears to reduce wasted draft scheduling work that the real scheduler would otherwise commit, whereas the shim's simulation does not model this cost.

### 3.4 Negative and Mixed Results

Several findings qualify the positive results:

1. **Synthetic/shim throughput cost.** In all synthetic and shim benchmarks, the governor imposed a throughput penalty of 5.8–11.2%. The governor's overhead in these tiers comes from per-request decision computation and the dwell mechanism, which the shim's simulation models as pure scheduling delay rather than as a reduction in wasted compute.

2. **PyPI vLLM incompatibility.** PyPI vLLM 0.19.1 is ABI-incompatible with CUDA Torch 2.11.0+cu130 on the GB10 platform. The patch installer intentionally refuses to apply to the PyPI layout because vLLM 0.19.1's scheduler source no longer matches the patch anchors.

3. **Torch 2.9.1+cu130 generation hang.** Even with the monotonic-clock fix, Torch 2.9.1+cu130 on GB10 (compute capability 12.1, exceeding the supported maximum of 12.0) caused generation timeouts. Upgrading to Torch 2.11.0+cu130 resolved this.

4. **Missing per-request waste/KV in stock vLLM.** The generic benchmark `success_rule_met` flag remained false for real vLLM runs because stock vLLM does not return per-request waste or KV fields in its OpenAI-compatible response. Waste and peak-KV deltas therefore remain at zero in the real-vLLM tier even though Prometheus speculative counters are parsed successfully.

5. **Single model and hardware.** All real-vLLM results use SmolLM2-135M-Instruct on a single NVIDIA GB10 system. Generalization to larger models, different speculation methods (eagle, medusa), or other GPU architectures is not established.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Model and hardware scope.** Real-vLLM evidence is limited to SmolLM2-135M-Instruct with ngram speculation on one NVIDIA GB10 system. Results may not generalize to larger models, different speculative decoding methods, or other GPU architectures.

2. **Synthetic vs. real divergence.** The synthetic and shim benchmarks model KV pressure and waste as simplified scheduling simulations. Their latency/throughput tradeoffs differ qualitatively from the real-vLLM results, where the governor improved rather than degraded performance under KV stress. The shim's waste and KV metrics are simulated, not measured from a real KV cache.

3. **Per-request observability gap.** Stock vLLM does not expose per-request speculative waste or KV consumption through its OpenAI-compatible API. The governor's waste-reduction benefit in real vLLM is inferred from Prometheus aggregate counters (accepted vs. drafted tokens) rather than measured per-request.

4. **Patch maintenance.** The vLLM scheduler patch targets a specific checkout (`ab597c869`). vLLM's scheduler internals may change across versions, requiring patch updates. The patch has not been submitted upstream.

5. **Concurrency range.** Real-vLLM benchmarks tested client concurrency of 8 and 16. Behavior at higher concurrency levels (64+) against a real model server is not established.

6. **Dwell clock bug history.** The initial implementation used epoch time for dwell deadlines, causing a high-concurrency stall that was misattributed to a GB10/Torch/Triton deadlock before being root-caused to a clock mismatch. This bug underscores the sensitivity of concurrent scheduling logic to clock semantics.

7. **Automated artifact provenance.** This draft and all reported results were generated by an automated research pipeline. No independent human verification of the benchmark numbers, patch correctness, or runtime behavior has been performed.

## 6. Conclusion

We presented a speculation-cost governor that adaptively controls per-request speculative-token caps, admission, and bounded dwell under KV pressure in batched vLLM serving. The governor communicates through an OpenAI-compatible `vllm_xargs` contract consumed by a patched vLLM V1 scheduler.

In synthetic and shim benchmarks, the governor reduced wasted draft work by 2.5–4.7% and peak KV usage by 0.7–5.2%, at the cost of 2.1–15.3% p95 latency regression and 5.8–11.2% throughput loss—remaining within the 20% p95 latency budget. In real patched-vLLM benchmarks with SmolLM2-135M on NVIDIA GB10, the governor improved p95 latency by 29.5% and throughput by 28.5% under KV/concurrency stress, with negligible overhead under dwell stress. A critical dwell-clock bug (epoch vs. monotonic time) was identified and fixed during integration.

The divergence between synthetic and real-vLLM results—where the governor improves rather than degrades performance under real KV stress—suggests that the governor's adaptive cap reduction captures scheduling costs that simplified simulations do not model. However, this finding is bounded to one model, one hardware platform, and limited concurrency levels. The current project artifacts support this finding in the tested setting; they do not establish universal applicability.

The recommended next step is to promote the monotonic-dwell vLLM patch and real-vLLM benchmark harness into an upstreamable integration hardening task, extending validation to larger models, alternative speculation methods, and broader hardware coverage.
