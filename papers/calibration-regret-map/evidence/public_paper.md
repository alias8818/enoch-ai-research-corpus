## Abstract

Reliability diagrams and scalar summary statistics such as Expected Calibration Error (ECE) indicate whether a probabilistic forecaster is miscalibrated, but they do not directly answer the decision-relevant question: *where* does miscalibration cause the most excess loss? This paper introduces the **Calibration Regret Map (CRM)**, a diagnostic that decomposes proper-score regret and cost-sensitive decision regret across forecast-probability bins and decision thresholds. We implement CRM in pure NumPy and evaluate it on four controlled synthetic scenarios (oracle, overconfident, underconfident, and S-shaped distortion) with sample sizes up to $n = 300{,}000$ and a five-seed stability check at $n = 100{,}000$ each. CRM correctly attributes near-zero regret to the oracle forecaster (mean log calibration regret $= 1.07 \times 10^{-4}$ across five seeds) while stably localizing the highest-regret bin in all three miscalibration scenarios across all five seeds. However, calibration-regret ranking and threshold-regret ranking diverge across scenarios, indicating that the two views capture different aspects of miscalibration cost. Results are limited to synthetic data with equal-width binning; production applicability, adaptive binning, and end-to-end recalibration intervention remain open.

## Introduction

Calibration of probabilistic forecasts—the agreement between predicted probabilities and empirical event rates—is a well-studied property. Standard diagnostics include reliability diagrams, ECE, and Maximum Calibration Error (MCE). Post-hoc recalibration methods such as temperature scaling can reduce global miscalibration. However, these tools share a limitation: they indicate *that* a model is miscalibrated and *how much* in aggregate, but not *where* the miscalibration is most costly.

This gap matters in practice. A forecaster that is slightly overconfident near a critical decision threshold may cause far more excess loss than a larger miscalibration in a region where no decisions are made. Conversely, a globally well-calibrated model may still harbor localized distortions that are invisible to scalar ECE.

We propose the **Calibration Regret Map (CRM)**, which extends the reliability diagram by assigning each probability bin an excess-loss contribution under proper scoring rules (Brier score, log-loss) and by constructing a threshold-regret surface over cost-sensitive binary decisions. CRM is intended as a diagnostic complement to existing calibration tools, not a replacement.

The concrete claim tested here is: *CRM can turn a reliability diagram into an actionable regret surface that identifies where recalibration would matter most, not just whether a model is globally miscalibrated.* We note upfront that this claim is supported structurally (regret measures excess loss by definition) but not by an end-to-end intervention experiment; see Limitations.

## Method

### Definitions

Let $\hat{p}_i \in [0,1]$ be the forecast probability for instance $i$ and $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ the outcome. Partition the forecast range into $B$ equal-width bins. For bin $b$, let $n_b$ be the count, $\bar{p}_b$ the mean forecast, and $\bar{y}_b$ the empirical event rate.

**Per-bin ECE contribution.** The standard ECE is $\sum_b \frac{n_b}{n} |\bar{y}_b - \bar{p}_b|$. CRM retains each term separately.

**Per-bin Brier calibration regret.** The Brier score decomposes into uncertainty, reliability, and resolution terms. The reliability (calibration) component for bin $b$ is $n_b (\bar{y}_b - \bar{p}_b)^2 / n$. CRM reports this per bin.

**Per-bin log calibration regret.** Under log-loss, the calibration regret for bin $b$ is the KL divergence between the empirical Bernoulli($\bar{y}_b$) and the forecast Bernoulli($\bar{p}_b$), weighted by $n_b/n$:

$$R_b^{\log} = \frac{n_b}{n} \left[ \bar{y}_b \log \frac{\bar{y}_b}{\bar{p}_b} + (1 - \bar{y}_b) \log \frac{1 - \bar{y}_b}{1 - \bar{p}_b} \right]$$

**Threshold decision regret.** For a binary decision with threshold $t$ and asymmetric costs (false-negative cost $c_{FN}$, false-positive cost $c_{FP}$), the regret at threshold $t$ is the excess expected cost incurred by using the miscalibrated forecast probabilities versus the true bin-conditional event rates. CRM evaluates this over a grid of thresholds.

### Implementation

The implementation is a single Python script (`scripts/calibration_regret_map.py`) using only NumPy. It accepts parameters for sample size, bin count, threshold count, random seed, and output directory. For each scenario, it:

1. Generates true probabilities from a Beta(2,5) distribution.
2. Applies a distortion to produce forecast probabilities (identity for oracle, shift for overconfident/underconfident, sigmoidal warp for S-shaped).
3. Samples binary outcomes from the true probabilities.
4. Computes all CRM quantities and writes JSON metrics, CSV bin-regret tables, CSV threshold-regret surfaces, and ASCII heat-map summaries.

### Experimental Design

Four synthetic scenarios were tested:

| Scenario | Distortion | Expected pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Oracle | $\hat{p} = p$ | Near-zero regret everywhere |
| Overconfident shifted | Forecasts pushed toward extremes | High regret in high-probability bins |
| Underconfident shifted | Forecasts pulled toward 0.5 | High regret in mid-probability bins |
| S-shaped distorted | Sigmoidal warp | High regret in low-probability bins |

Three experimental phases were run:

1. **Smoke test**: $n = 10{,}000$, 20 bins.
2. **Full run**: $n = 300{,}000$, 20 bins, 37 thresholds.
3. **Stability check**: Five replicates at $n = 100{,}000$ each (seeds 20260430–20260434), 20 bins, 37 thresholds, with aggregate statistics.

All runs were executed on a single machine with approximately 116 GiB available memory. Swap was disabled. Computation time per scenario was under 0.03 seconds. These are pure NumPy simulations on synthetic data—not GPU benchmarks, CUDA calibrations, or production inference evaluations.

## Results

### Full Run ($n = 300{,}000$)

| Scenario | ECE | MCE | Brier | Brier cal. regret | Log cal. regret | Top regret bin | Top bin log regret |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Oracle | 0.00237 | 0.00980 | 0.1262 | $9.86 \times 10^{-6}$ | $3.17 \times 10^{-5}$ | [0.10, 0.15) | $4.58 \times 10^{-6}$ |
| Overconfident | 0.08893 | 0.18177 | 0.1364 | 0.00962 | 0.07685 | [0.95, 1.00) | 0.04078 |
| Underconfident | 0.11140 | 0.18299 | 0.1415 | 0.01528 | 0.04754 | [0.70, 0.75) | 0.00989 |
| S-shaped | 0.09463 | 0.20082 | 0.1383 | 0.01119 | 0.03329 | [0.15, 0.20) | 0.00433 |

The oracle forecaster exhibits near-zero calibration regret across all metrics, as expected. The top regret bin for the oracle ([0.10, 0.15)) carries a log regret of only $4.58 \times 10^{-6}$, consistent with sampling noise rather than genuine miscalibration signal.

Each miscalibration scenario produces a distinct and interpretable CRM signature:

- **Overconfident shifted**: The dominant regret bin is [0.95, 1.00), where the forecaster assigns high confidence that is not borne out by events. This bin alone accounts for 0.0408 of the total log calibration regret of 0.0769 (approximately 53%).
- **Underconfident shifted**: The dominant regret bin is [0.70, 0.75), where the forecaster is too conservative relative to the true event rate. The direction is labeled "underconfident."
- **S-shaped distorted**: The dominant regret bin is [0.15, 0.20), where the sigmoidal warp causes the forecaster to overstate the probability of rare events. The direction is labeled "overconfident" in this low-probability region.

### Threshold Decision Regret

The threshold-regret surface provides a decision-centric view:

| Scenario | Max threshold regret | Total threshold regret | Top threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oracle | 0.000202 | 0.000735 | 0.475 |
| Overconfident | 0.01995 | 0.17295 | 0.950 |
| Underconfident | 0.01806 | 0.29153 | 0.725 |
| S-shaped | 0.00827 | 0.22011 | 0.525 |

A notable finding is that the underconfident scenario yields the highest total threshold regret (0.292), despite having lower log calibration regret than the overconfident scenario (0.0475 vs. 0.0769). This divergence illustrates that calibration regret and decision regret can rank miscalibration modes differently depending on the cost structure and threshold placement. This is a key motivation for CRM: a scalar calibration metric alone does not determine which miscalibration mode is most costly for a given decision context.

Conversely, the overconfident scenario has the highest per-bin log calibration regret concentrated in a single bin, but its total threshold regret is the lowest among the three miscalibration scenarios. This suggests that concentrated miscalibration in extreme-probability bins, while producing large proper-score regret, may be less costly under the default symmetric cost structure than diffuse mid-range miscalibration that shifts many threshold decisions.

### Five-Seed Stability Check

Aggregate statistics across five replicates ($n = 100{,}000$ each):

| Scenario | Mean log cal. regret | Stdev | Top regret bin (all 5 seeds) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oracle | $1.07 \times 10^{-4}$ | $3.70 \times 10^{-5}$ | Unstable (5 different bins) |
| Overconfident | 0.07742 | $7.84 \times 10^{-4}$ | [0.95, 1.00) ×5 |
| Underconfident | 0.04718 | $3.48 \times 10^{-4}$ | [0.70, 0.75) ×5 |
| S-shaped | 0.03313 | $7.63 \times 10^{-4}$ | [0.15, 0.20) ×5 |

The oracle's top-regret bin is unstable across seeds, which is expected: when regret is at noise level, the argmax bin is arbitrary. In contrast, all three miscalibration scenarios show perfect stability of the top-regret bin across all five seeds, indicating that CRM's localization is robust to sampling variation when genuine miscalibration signal is present.

Total threshold regret is also stable across seeds, with coefficients of variation below 2% for all non-oracle scenarios.

### Mixed and Negative Observations

Several observations temper the positive results:

1. **Oracle regret is not exactly zero.** Even the oracle forecaster yields non-zero calibration regret ($1.07 \times 10^{-4}$ mean across seeds), reflecting finite-sample binning noise. CRM does not include a built-in statistical test to distinguish genuine miscalibration from sampling noise; users must interpret regret magnitudes relative to sample size.

2. **Top-regret-bin instability for well-calibrated forecasters.** The oracle's top-regret bin varies across seeds, meaning that CRM's localization output should not be acted upon when overall regret is negligible. A pre-filter on total regret magnitude would be necessary in practice.

3. **Brier calibration regret and log calibration regret can disagree on which bin is worst.** The two proper scoring rules weight bin discrepancies differently (quadratic vs. logarithmic), and the current implementation reports both but does not reconcile them. Users must choose which scoring rule is relevant to their application.

## Limitations

1. **Synthetic data only.** All results are from controlled synthetic distortions applied to Beta-distributed true probabilities. CRM has not been validated on real model outputs, private inference evaluations, or production systems. Whether CRM provides actionable localization on real miscalibration patterns remains an open question.

2. **Equal-width binning.** The current implementation uses equal-width bins, which can produce high-variance estimates in sparse bins (particularly near 0 and 1). Adaptive or quantile-based binning may improve statistical efficiency and should be compared before standardizing CRM for production use.

3. **Binary classification only.** CRM is defined for binary outcomes. Extension to multiclass settings, structured prediction, or LLM sequence-level confidence is not implemented in this artifact.

4. **No comparison to alternative localization methods.** This study does not compare CRM against other per-bin diagnostics (e.g., per-bin reliability diagrams with confidence intervals, or isotonic regression residuals). The incremental value of CRM's regret framing over simpler per-bin gap inspection is not quantified.

5. **No recalibration intervention test.** CRM identifies where regret is highest, but this study does not close the loop by applying targeted recalibration and measuring regret reduction. The claim that CRM identifies "where recalibration would matter most" is supported only by the structural argument that regret measures excess loss, not by an end-to-end intervention experiment.

6. **Cost structure assumptions.** The threshold-regret surface assumes a specific cost structure for binary decisions. Different cost ratios would shift the surface. The current implementation uses a default symmetric cost; sensitivity to cost asymmetry is not explored.

7. **Claim ledger audit status.** The structured claim ledger for this artifact was flagged as `blocked_empty_claims` at generation time, meaning no formal claims passed the evidence-audit pipeline. The results reported here should be read as prototype evidence rather than audit-approved claims.

## Conclusion

The Calibration Regret Map is viable as a probability calibration diagnostic that localizes excess proper-score loss and cost-sensitive threshold regret by forecast bin. On synthetic data, CRM correctly attributes near-zero regret to a well-calibrated oracle and stably identifies the highest-regret probability bin under three distinct miscalibration modes across five independent seeds. The divergence between calibration-regret ranking and threshold-regret ranking across scenarios demonstrates that CRM's decision-relevant surface can surface information not captured by scalar ECE alone.

However, these results are confined to synthetic binary classification with equal-width binning. Whether CRM provides commensurate value on real model outputs, whether adaptive binning improves its stability in sparse regions, whether the regret framing adds value beyond simpler per-bin gap inspection, and whether targeted recalibration guided by CRM actually reduces regret all remain open questions. We release this artifact as a prototype diagnostic for further evaluation rather than a validated production tool.
