## Abstract

We present Chunk-Margin Pruner, a prompt-packing method for retrieved-document question answering that prunes retrieved chunks whose inclusion margin is unstable across deterministic query rewrites. In offline benchmarks against a baseline top-k concatenation packer, the method consistently reduces prompt token count by 18–37% while maintaining or slightly improving generated answer F1. However, gold paragraph recall decreases by 2.5–5.3 percentage points on larger randomized samples, indicating that the pruner can discard annotated evidence paragraphs even when model-generated answer quality is preserved. Results are drawn from a synthetic minimum viable experiment, a 200-example randomized SQuAD v1.1 validation sample, and a 150-example Spoken-SQuAD test sample, all evaluated with a cached extractive QA model. Confidence in the generalizability of these findings is medium; the extractive QA proxy may reward shorter prompts differently than a generative RAG model would.

## 1. Introduction

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems assemble prompts by concatenating top-k retrieved document chunks before passing them to a language model. A practical difficulty arises when chunks near the rank cutoff have similar retrieval scores: small perturbations to the query—such as paraphrasing or rewriting—can change which chunks are included, introducing prompt instability and wasting token budget on marginally relevant content.

Chunk-Margin Pruner addresses this by identifying chunks whose inclusion is unstable across deterministic query rewrites and pruning them from the prompt. The intuition is that chunks whose presence fluctuates under minor query variation are less likely to be essential for answering the question, and removing them frees token budget for more stable, higher-value content.

This paper reports empirical results from offline benchmarks evaluating whether the pruner achieves its stated goals: reducing prompt token usage by at least 15% at equal or better answer quality, and yielding at least a 5-point quality gain at the same token budget. The evidence is mixed but directionally positive for the first criterion and negative for the second.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Chunk-Margin Stability Score

For a given query $q$, the system generates a set of deterministic rewrites $\{q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_r\}$. Each rewrite is used to retrieve and rank document chunks from the same index. A chunk $c_i$ receives a margin-stability score reflecting how consistently it appears near the retrieval cutoff across rewrites. Chunks that appear in some rewrite results but not others—particularly those near the rank boundary—are assigned low stability scores.

### 2.2 Pruning Decision

Given a token budget $B$, the baseline packer selects the top-k chunks by retrieval score and concatenates them until the budget is exhausted. The Chunk-Margin Pruner modifies this by:

1. Retrieving a candidate pool using the original query.
2. Computing margin-stability scores across query rewrites.
3. Sorting candidates by a composite of retrieval score and stability.
4. Selecting chunks in order, preferring stable chunks over unstable ones near the cutoff, until the token budget is reached.

### 2.3 Implementation

The implementation is dependency-free Python (`src/chunk_margin_pruner.py`). Retrieval is performed with a BM25-style scorer over paragraph-indexed documents. Query rewrites are generated deterministically from the original query. The packer operates as a post-retrieval prompt constructor and does not modify the underlying retrieval index or model.

## 3. Experimental Setup

Experiments were conducted in three phases of increasing external validity.

### 3.1 Synthetic Minimum Viable Experiment

A synthetic multi-task RAG corpus was constructed with deliberately unstable near-cutoff distractors and stable cross-rewrite evidence chunks. Five tasks were evaluated at five budget scales (0.7, 0.85, 1.0, 1.15, 1.3), yielding 50 benchmark rows. Quality was measured by answer-field presence and evidence recall against known ground truth, not by model-generated answer F1.

### 3.2 SQuAD v1.1 Validation Benchmark

Real paragraphs from SQuAD v1.1 validation data were indexed (2,067 paragraphs). BM25 retrieval scores were computed for original and rewritten queries. Two packing methods (baseline top-k concatenation and chunk-margin pruner) were applied, and answers were generated by a cached `distilbert-base-cased-distilled-squad` extractive QA model. Metrics included average prompt tokens, token-level answer F1 against gold answers, and gold paragraph recall (whether the annotated supporting paragraph appeared in the prompt).

An initial run used the first 60 eligible examples at budget 260, followed by budget sensitivity runs at 180 and 340. A subsequent run used a seeded random sample of 200 examples at budget 260.

### 3.3 Spoken-SQuAD Test Benchmark

The public Spoken-SQuAD test set was evaluated with the same BM25 retrieval, deterministic rewrites, packers, and cached extractive QA model. A seeded random sample of 150 examples was evaluated at budget 260.

### 3.4 Metrics

- **Average prompt tokens**: mean token count of the constructed prompt across examples.
- **Generated answer F1**: token-level F1 between the extractive QA model's output and the gold answer.
- **Gold paragraph recall**: fraction of examples where the annotated supporting paragraph was included in the prompt.
- **Pack latency overhead**: additional wall-clock time for prompt construction by the pruner versus baseline, measured in milliseconds.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Synthetic MVE

| Metric | Baseline | Pruner | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Avg prompt tokens | 82.88 | 39.60 | −52.22% |
| Answer-field F1 | 0.402 | 1.000 | +59.80 pts |
| Evidence recall | 0.260 | 1.000 | +74.00 pts |
| Pack latency overhead | — | — | +0.366 ms |

The synthetic MVE strongly favors the pruner, as expected given that the corpus was designed around the target failure mode (unstable near-cutoff distractors). These results establish mechanism plausibility but do not generalize.

### 4.2 SQuAD v1.1 — Initial 60-Example Run

| Metric | Baseline | Pruner | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Avg prompt tokens | 240.10 | 180.72 | −24.73% |
| Generated answer F1 | 0.4607 | 0.4867 | +2.60 pts |
| Gold paragraph recall | 0.6333 | 0.3833 | −25.00 pts |
| Pack latency overhead | — | — | +1.67 ms |

Token reduction and F1 improvement are positive, but gold paragraph recall dropped sharply (−25 percentage points). This was the most concerning result in the study.

### 4.3 SQuAD v1.1 — Budget Sensitivity (60 Examples)

| Budget | Token Reduction | F1 Difference | Gold Recall Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| 180 | −18.26% | +0.56 pts | +1.67 pts |
| 260 | −24.73% | +2.60 pts | −25.00 pts |
| 340 | −24.99% | +3.49 pts | −3.33 pts |

The severe gold recall drop at budget 260 did not replicate at budgets 180 or 340, suggesting interaction between budget level and the pruning boundary on this particular non-randomized sample.

### 4.4 SQuAD v1.1 — Randomized 200-Example Sample

| Metric | Baseline | Pruner | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Avg prompt tokens | 226.24 | 142.24 | −37.13% |
| Generated answer F1 | 0.6083 | 0.6207 | +1.25 pts |
| Gold paragraph recall | 0.7400 | 0.7150 | −2.50 pts |
| Pack latency overhead | — | — | +1.83 ms |

On the larger randomized sample, the severe gold recall drop narrowed to −2.5 percentage points. Token reduction was substantial (37.13%), and generated answer F1 improved modestly (+1.25 points). This result passes the ≥15% token-reduction-at-equal-quality criterion but not the ≥5-point quality-gain criterion.

### 4.5 Spoken-SQuAD — Randomized 150-Example Sample

| Metric | Baseline | Pruner | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Avg prompt tokens | 225.87 | 147.22 | −34.82% |
| Generated answer F1 | 0.4047 | 0.4173 | +1.26 pts |
| Gold paragraph recall | 0.6267 | 0.5733 | −5.33 pts |
| Pack latency overhead | — | — | +1.74 ms |

The pattern replicates on a second dataset: substantial token reduction, modest F1 improvement, and a small but consistent gold recall decrease.

### 4.6 Summary Across Settings

The pruner consistently reduces prompt tokens by 18–37% across all tested settings. Generated answer F1 is equal or better in every case. Gold paragraph recall decreases in all non-synthetic settings, ranging from −2.5 to −25 percentage points, with the extreme value occurring on a small non-randomized sample and not replicating on larger randomized samples. Pack latency overhead is consistently under 2 ms.

## 5. Limitations

1. **Extractive QA proxy, not generative RAG.** All non-synthetic benchmarks use `distilbert-base-cased-distilled-squad`, an extractive QA model that selects answer spans from the prompt. Generative RAG models may respond differently to shorter prompts—potentially benefiting more from reduced distractor content, or potentially suffering more when the exact supporting paragraph is absent. The current evidence does not address this.

2. **Gold recall–F1 divergence.** The pruner can discard the annotated gold paragraph while still improving generated answer F1. This occurs when another retrieved paragraph supports a correct extractive answer, or when the model guesses correctly from partial context. Whether this divergence is acceptable depends on the application: systems that require auditable evidence attribution may find the recall drop unacceptable even when answer quality is preserved.

3. **Synthetic corpus overrepresents the target failure mode.** The synthetic MVE was explicitly designed around unstable near-cutoff distractors, inflating the apparent benefit. Those results should be read as mechanism validation only.

4. **Small and non-diverse benchmark samples.** The largest sample is 200 examples from SQuAD v1.1 and 150 from Spoken-SQuAD. Both are extractive QA datasets in English with short factual answers. Performance on longer-form generative QA, multi-hop reasoning, or non-English corpora is unknown.

5. **Deterministic rewrites only.** Query rewrites are generated deterministically from the original query. The method's behavior under stochastic or model-generated rewrites has not been evaluated.

6. **No live inference path validation.** All experiments are offline. The pruner has not been integrated into a production RAG inference pipeline, and end-to-end latency including retrieval and model inference has not been measured.

7. **BM25 retrieval only.** Results are specific to a BM25-style retriever. Dense retrieval methods (e.g., embedding-based search) may produce different score distributions and margin-stability patterns.

## 7. Conclusion

Chunk-Margin Pruner demonstrates that margin-stability-based pruning of retrieved chunks can reduce prompt token usage by 18–37% in offline retrieved-document QA benchmarks while maintaining or slightly improving extractive answer F1. The evidence supports the core utility-per-token claim at medium confidence across three experimental settings of increasing external validity.

However, the method consistently reduces gold paragraph recall, meaning it can discard the annotated supporting evidence even when answer quality is preserved. The practical acceptability of this tradeoff depends on whether downstream systems require the exact gold paragraph for attribution or only require sufficient context for correct answering.

The second success criterion—≥5-point quality gain at the same budget—was not met in any non-synthetic setting. The pruner's value proposition is therefore best characterized as token efficiency at maintained quality, rather than quality improvement.

Future work should integrate the packer into a live generative RAG inference path with product-specific documents and generative language models, where the interaction between prompt length, distractor content, and answer quality may differ from the extractive QA proxy used here.
