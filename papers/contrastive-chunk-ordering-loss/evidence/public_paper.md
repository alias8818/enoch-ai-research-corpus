## Abstract

We investigate whether a contrastive next-chunk ordering loss can learn a directional adjacency signal from shuffled text chunks, and whether it correctly fails when no such signal exists. On a controlled synthetic benchmark with documents of six chunks drawn from latent topic trajectories, a bilinear scorer trained with an InfoNCE-style cross-entropy objective recovers the exact permutation in 97.4% of held-out documents (mean across five seeds), with directed adjacency accuracy of 99.47% and Kendall's τ of 0.976. A symmetric dot-product baseline achieves only 21.3% exact accuracy and near-zero τ (0.007), confirming that directionality is essential. On a no-signal negative control where chunk order is independent of content, the same model remains at chance on all metrics (exact accuracy 0.13%, adjacency 17.1%), with training loss plateauing near ln(5) ≈ 1.609 (observed 1.585). These results support the viability of contrastive chunk ordering as a learning signal under structured content, but do not constitute evidence of benefit for production retrieval, long-context modeling, or RAG systems. Scaling to real documents and larger chunk counts remains open.

## 1. Introduction

Recovering the original order of shuffled text segments is a task with potential applications in document retrieval, long-context assembly, and reranking. A natural approach is to train a model to predict which chunk follows a given chunk, then decode the full ordering from pairwise adjacency scores. Two questions arise:

1. **Can a contrastive objective learn a *directional* ordering signal?** Symmetric similarity measures (e.g., dot product of bag-of-words vectors) can exploit topical contiguity but cannot reliably orient a sequence, since similarity(x_i, x_j) = similarity(x_j, x_i).

2. **Does the loss correctly fail when no ordering signal exists?** A viable ordering loss must not hallucinate structure in exchangeable data.

We address both questions with a minimal experimental design: a bilinear scorer trained with a contrastive next-chunk loss, evaluated on a synthetic benchmark with a structured condition and a no-signal negative control. We report exact permutation accuracy, directed adjacency accuracy, and Kendall's τ, along with training loss dynamics. We deliberately restrict claims to what the controlled synthetic experiment supports.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Problem Formulation

Each document is a sequence of *L* chunks (x_1, …, x_L). During training, the model receives chunks in their true order and learns to score adjacent pairs higher than non-adjacent pairs from the same document. During inference, chunks are shuffled and the model must recover the original ordering using only pairwise adjacency scores.

### 2.2 Chunk Representation

Chunks are represented as normalized log term-frequency vectors over a vocabulary of size *V* = 96. No explicit position features are provided. This bag-of-words representation ensures that any ordering signal must come from content-based transition structure between adjacent chunks, not from positional encodings.

### 2.3 Bilinear Scorer

The adjacency score between two chunks is:

$$s(x_i, x_j) = x_i^T W x_j$$

where *W* ∈ ℝ^{V×V} is a learned parameter matrix. Unlike a symmetric dot product, the bilinear form can capture directional transitions: *W* need not be symmetric, so s(x_i, x_j) ≠ s(x_j, x_i) in general.

### 2.4 Contrastive Loss

For each true adjacent pair (x_t, x_{t+1}) in a document, the loss treats x_{t+1} as the positive and all other chunks in the same document as negatives. The InfoNCE-style cross-entropy objective is:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{L-1} \sum_{t=1}^{L-1} \log \frac{\exp(s(x_t, x_{t+1}))}{\sum_{j \neq t} \exp(s(x_t, x_j))}$$

With *L* = 6, each positive is classified against 5 negatives, giving a chance loss of ln(5) ≈ 1.609.

### 2.5 Inference via Exact Search

After shuffling each test document's chunks, the full ordering is recovered by exhaustive Hamiltonian-path search maximizing the sum of adjacent bilinear scores. With *L* = 6, the search space is 6! = 720 permutations, making exact search cheap and deterministic.

### 2.6 Data Conditions

**Structured condition.** Documents follow latent topic trajectories around a ring. Adjacent chunks share directional transition structure but have noisy, overlapping vocabularies. The true order is recoverable from content-based transitions.

**No-signal condition.** Chunks are exchangeable random topic samples; the assigned order is independent of content. This serves as the negative control: any model that claims to recover order here is fitting noise.

### 2.7 Baselines

- **Identity dot product:** s(x_i, x_j) = x_i · x_j. Symmetric; can exploit topical contiguity but cannot orient the sequence.
- **Random bilinear:** *W* initialized randomly, not trained. Controls for any inductive bias of the bilinear form itself.
- **Tie baseline (first permutation):** Expected exact accuracy is 1/6! ≈ 0.00139; expected directed adjacency accuracy is 1/6 ≈ 0.1667.

### 2.8 Training Configuration

- Training documents: 1,200 per seed
- Test documents: 300 per seed (held-out)
- Epochs: 35
- Document length: 6 chunks
- Vocabulary size: 96
- Random seeds: {0, 1, 2, 3, 4}
- All results report mean ± sample standard deviation across the five seeds.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Synthetic benchmark only.** The documents are generated from a latent topic model with known transition structure. Whether the contrastive chunk ordering loss provides useful signal on real documents—with naturally ordered paragraphs, varied rhetorical structures, and richer vocabularies—remains entirely untested. No claim about production LLM retrievers, long-context models, or RAG pipelines is warranted by these results.

2. **Exact search does not scale.** Inference used exhaustive Hamiltonian-path search over 6! = 720 permutations. For documents with *L* chunks, the search space is *L*!, which becomes intractable beyond approximately *L* = 10–12. Practical deployment requires beam search, dynamic programming, or graph-based decoding, and the interaction between approximate decoding and the learned scoring function needs separate evaluation.

3. **Bag-of-words features.** Chunk representations are normalized log term-frequency vectors, not contextual embeddings from a pretrained language model. The extent to which the bilinear scorer's success depends on this simple feature space—and whether the loss transfers to richer encoders—is unknown.

4. **Single document length.** Only *L* = 6 was tested. Performance as a function of document length, including potential degradation with longer sequences, was not characterized.

5. **No downstream task evaluation.** Ordering accuracy is a proxy; whether improved ordering translates to better retrieval, reranking, or generation quality was not measured.

6. **Small vocabulary and topic space.** The vocabulary of 96 terms and the ring-structured topic model are highly constrained. Performance may differ substantially with larger, more diverse vocabularies and less regular topic structures.

## 6. Conclusion

A bilinear contrastive next-chunk loss can learn a directional ordering signal from shuffled chunks when such a signal exists in the data, and it correctly fails when no signal is present. On a controlled synthetic benchmark with six-chunk documents, the trained model recovers the exact permutation in 97.4% of test cases (vs. 0.14% chance), while a symmetric dot-product baseline achieves only 21.3% exact accuracy and near-zero Kendall τ (0.007), demonstrating that directionality is essential. On the no-signal negative control, all metrics remain at chance and training loss plateaus near the theoretical maximum (ln(5) ≈ 1.609), confirming that the model does not hallucinate order.

These results validate the contrastive chunk ordering loss as a viable learning signal in a controlled synthetic setting. They do not establish that the loss improves any production system. The necessary next steps are: (1) evaluation on real documents with naturally ordered paragraphs or sentences, (2) scalable approximate decoding for longer documents, and (3) measurement of downstream effects on retrieval, reranking, or long-context assembly tasks.
