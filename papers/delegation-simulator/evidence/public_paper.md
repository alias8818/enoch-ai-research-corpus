## Abstract

Delegation—assigning subtasks to specialist workers rather than completing all work solo—is a foundational strategy in both human and multi-agent project execution, yet it incurs coordination overhead: startup cost, context transfer, review effort, and defect repair. We present a self-contained Monte Carlo simulator that models project execution as an acyclic task graph with heterogeneous workers and explicit coordination costs, and we evaluate four delegation policies across a sweep of coordination overhead scales. Across 250 random project seeds per condition, we find that delegation yields substantial speedups (up to 2.3×) when coordination overhead is low, but that a naive delegate-all policy becomes counterproductive (0.54×–0.90×) when overhead is high. An adaptive policy that gates delegation on expected net benefit preserves near-solo performance at high overhead (1.00×–1.04×) while capturing most gains at low overhead (1.95×–2.31×). These results are simulation-only and depend on assumed parameter values; they do not constitute empirical validation against live agent or human delegation data. ## Introduction

When a project can be decomposed into subtasks with heterogeneous skill requirements, delegating each subtask to a specialist offers the prospect of faster completion. This intuition underpins multi-agent orchestration in LLM-based systems as well as conventional team management. Yet delegation is not free: every handoff incurs startup latency, context transfer cost, review effort, and the risk of defects introduced at the boundary.

The central question is not whether delegation is beneficial in principle, but *when* the net benefit turns negative. If coordination overhead is sufficiently high relative to specialist speedup, naive delegation can slow a project below solo performance. An adaptive policy that delegates only when the expected specialist advantage exceeds estimated coordination and risk cost should, in theory, preserve gains where delegation helps and avoid harm where it does not.

We investigate this question with a parameterized Monte Carlo simulation. We do not claim that the simulator's parameter values are calibrated to any particular real-world system; rather, we treat it as a proof-of-concept research harness that can produce concrete, reproducible threshold findings and serve as a scaffold for future empirical calibration.

## Method

### Simulator Design

The simulator (`src/delegation_simulator.py`) is a single-file Python program with no third-party dependencies. It models project execution as follows:

**Task graph.** Each project is a generated acyclic task graph with a configurable number of tasks and edge probability. Task types are drawn from {research, implementation, testing, review}.

**Workers.** Four worker archetypes are defined: generalist, researcher, executor, and tester. Each worker has type-specific speed multipliers for each task type, reflecting specialist advantage.

**Delegation costs.** When a task is delegated (assigned to a worker other than the primary), the simulator charges:

- *Startup cost:* fixed overhead per delegation event.
- *Context transfer cost:* proportional to task complexity.
- *Review cost:* effort for the primary to verify the delegated output.
- *Defect repair cost:* expected cost of rework when a delegated task introduces a defect, modeled probabilistically.

These costs are scaled by a single `coordination_scale` parameter, which is the primary independent variable in the calibration sweep.

### Policies

Four delegation policies are evaluated:

1. **solo:** No delegation. The primary worker completes all tasks sequentially.
2. **delegate_all:** Every task is delegated to the best specialist, regardless of overhead.
3. **adaptive:** A task is delegated only when the expected specialist speedup exceeds the estimated sum of coordination and risk costs.
4. **oracle:** An idealized policy with access to ground-truth task outcomes, delegating when it is truly beneficial. This serves as an upper-bound reference.

### Experimental Design

The primary independent variable is `coordination_scale`, swept over {0.25, 0.50, 1.00, 2.00, 4.00}. For each scale, 250 Monte Carlo seeds are run with the following fixed parameters:

- Number of tasks: 36
- Edge probability: 0.35
- All other simulator parameters at default values as encoded in `src/delegation_simulator.py`

The dependent variable is *speedup relative to solo*, defined as the ratio of solo completion time to policy completion time for the same seed. A speedup greater than 1.0 indicates the policy finished faster than solo; less than 1.0 indicates it was slower.

### Environment

All runs were executed on a Linux `aarch64` host (kernel recorded in `logs/environment.log`) with Python 3.12.3, approximately 128 GB total memory, and no swap (intentionally). The simulator is CPU-only; peak RSS during the smoke test was approximately 15.6 MB. No GPU or long-running workload was involved.

### Verification

- Unit tests: 3 tests passed (`logs/unit_tests.log`).
- Smoke test: 3 seeds, 12 tasks, completed successfully with results written to `results/smoke_rows.csv` and `results/smoke_summary.json`.
- Calibration sweep: 5 scales × 250 seeds × 4 policies completed; per-scale logs in `logs/scale_*.log`.

## Results

### Aggregate Speedup by Coordination Scale

| Coordination Scale | delegate_all | adaptive | oracle | Key interpretation |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 0.25 | 2.107× | 2.307× | 2.226× | Low overhead: broad delegation is strongly beneficial; adaptive slightly exceeds delegate_all and oracle |
| 0.50 | 1.769× | 1.953× | 2.043× | Moderate-low overhead: adaptive and oracle beat delegate_all by avoiding marginal handoffs |
| 1.00 | 1.339× | 1.271× | 1.317× | Baseline overhead: delegation still net-positive but margins thin; adaptive delegates sparingly (3.7 tasks/project) |
| 2.00 | 0.899× | 1.038× | 1.040× | High overhead: naive delegation is harmful (21% win rate); adaptive gating preserves slight gain (62% win rate) |

### Key Observations

**Delegation is not monotonically beneficial.** The delegate_all policy shows a monotonic decline from 2.107× to 0.542× as coordination scale increases, crossing below 1.0 between scales 1.0 and 2.0.

**Adaptive gating is robust.** The adaptive policy never falls below 1.0× mean speedup across all tested scales. At low overhead it captures most of the available gain (2.307× at scale 0.25); at high overhead it safely reverts to near-solo behavior (1.001× at scale 4.0).

**Oracle is not strictly dominant over adaptive at all scales.** At scale 0.25, adaptive (2.307×) slightly exceeds oracle (2.226×). This is a simulation artifact: the oracle optimizes per-task delegation decisions with ground-truth knowledge, while the adaptive policy uses expected-value heuristics that can, by chance, yield higher sample means over a finite number of seeds. The difference is small and not statistically resolved at this sample size.

**Adaptive delegation volume decreases with overhead.** At coordination scale 1.0, the adaptive policy delegated an average of 3.7 tasks per 36-task project, compared to 36 under delegate_all. This confirms that the adaptive gate is actively filtering marginal delegations.

### Negative and Mixed Results

- At coordination scale 1.0, adaptive (1.271×) slightly underperformed delegate_all (1.339×). This suggests the adaptive gate is slightly too conservative at moderate overhead, declining some delegations that would have been net-positive. Tuning the gate threshold could improve this, but risks over-delegation at higher scales.
- The oracle policy did not consistently outperform adaptive, indicating that even perfect per-task information does not guarantee superior project-level outcomes under finite-sample stochastic conditions.
- At scale 4.0, oracle (0.999×) fell marginally below solo, suggesting that even the oracle occasionally delegates when the stochastic realization of coordination costs exceeds expectations.

## Limitations

1. **Simulation, not empirical measurement.** All results depend on the assumed parameter values encoded in the simulator. Worker speed multipliers, defect probabilities, and coordination cost formulas are explicit assumptions, not fitted to real data. These findings demonstrate that the *structure* of the threshold effect exists under the model, not that it holds for any particular real-world system.

2. **Parameter space is narrow.** Only coordination scale was swept. Other parameters—task count, edge probability, worker speed ratios, defect rates—were held at single values. The threshold locations would shift under different parameterizations.

3. **No economic objective.** The simulator optimizes for completion time only. Real delegation decisions also involve token cost, monetary cost, quality penalties, and wall-clock SLA constraints, none of which are modeled.

4. **No live-agent or human validation.** The simulator has not been compared against real multi-agent LLM runs, human delegation logs, or production traces. External validation would require fitting the model's parameters to such data and testing predictive accuracy.

5. **Small unit test suite.** Only 3 unit tests were verified. While the simulator is small and self-contained, the test coverage may be insufficient to rule out subtle bugs in edge-case graph topologies or policy logic.

6. **Oracle imperfection.** The oracle policy uses ground-truth expected values but still operates under stochastic realizations. It is not a true global optimum over the full project schedule.

7. **No statistical uncertainty quantification.** Mean speedups are reported; confidence intervals and standard errors were not computed and are not present in the artifacts. The reported differences between policies at some scales (e.g., adaptive vs. oracle at scale 0.25) may not be statistically significant.

8. **Claim audit blocked.** The structured claim ledger contains no claims and was audited as blocked. No finding in this paper has passed formal claim/evidence audit.

## Conclusion

A self-contained Monte Carlo simulator of delegation in acyclic task graphs produces a concrete and reproducible threshold finding: delegation yields large speedups when coordination overhead is low, remains modestly beneficial at moderate overhead, and becomes actively harmful under a naive delegate-all policy when overhead is high. An adaptive gating policy that delegates only when expected specialist speedup exceeds estimated coordination and risk cost is robust across all tested overhead scales—capturing most available gains when delegation is beneficial and safely reverting to solo performance when it is not.

These results establish the *structural plausibility* of an adaptive delegation threshold but do not empirically validate it. The simulator's parameters are assumptions, not measurements. The claim ledger remains empty and blocked from audit. Closing the gap between this simulation and actionable engineering guidance would require fitting the model to real delegation traces—whether from multi-agent LLM systems, human project logs, or controlled benchmark runs—and testing whether the predicted thresholds hold under empirical conditions.
