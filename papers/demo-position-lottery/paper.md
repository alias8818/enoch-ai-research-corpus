## Abstract

We investigate whether optimizing the position of retrieved evidence chunks within a fixed-token prompt budget can improve answer quality in citation-style QA, beyond what standard top-k similarity packing achieves. We implement an adaptive "searched position lottery" packer that selects candidate-pool search under tight token budgets and falls back to kept-set-preserving reordering at higher budgets, using a lost-in-middle position objective without access to ground-truth labels. In a deterministic synthetic benchmark (120 cases, 3 budgets, 5 repeats) and a small-model QA/citation evaluation (HuggingFaceTB/SmolLM2-135M-Instruct, 12 cases, 3 phrasings), the adaptive packer improves answer F1 by +8.3 to +22.2 percentage points over top-k similarity packing at all tested budgets, and improves citation F1 by +5.6 to +7.2 points at the two tighter budgets. However, the method does not achieve token savings: tight-budget candidate-pool search uses 5–7% more prompt tokens than the baseline, and citation F1 regresses slightly (−1.1 points) at the highest budget. Pure random shuffling and isolated exact-top-k reordering both underperform the baseline. These results support the hypothesis that searched prompt layout improves quality-at-budget in the tested setting, but the token-savings component remains unsupported. All results are limited to a single 135M-parameter model, synthetic and small-scale benchmarks, and deterministic seeds; external replication is needed.

## 1. Introduction

Retrieval-augmented generation systems typically pack evidence into prompts by ranking candidate chunks by similarity to the query and including the top-k that fit within a token budget. This ordering places the most similar evidence first, but a growing body of work on the "lost in the middle" phenomenon suggests that language models attend disproportionately to content at the beginning and end of long contexts, with degraded utilization of middle-positioned evidence.

If position matters, then the default similarity-ranked ordering may be suboptimal: the most relevant evidence chunk might be more effective if placed at a position the model attends to more strongly, even if that means a less-similar chunk occupies the lead position. The question is whether a search over candidate selections and orderings—constrained by a token budget and guided by a position-sensitivity objective—can yield better downstream answer quality than the standard top-k similarity baseline.

We term this approach "demo position lottery": rather than committing to a single deterministic packing order, we search over feasible prompt layouts under budget, using a position-weighted quality proxy that does not require ground-truth answer labels. The core hypothesis is that searched prompt layout can improve answer and citation quality at a given token budget compared to top-k similarity packing.

This paper reports results from a self-contained experimental project that implemented three packing strategies (top-k similarity baseline, random-order lottery, and searched position lottery), evaluated them on both a synthetic offline proxy and a small-model QA/citation harness, and iteratively refined the searched strategy to address a regression observed at higher token budgets. We report positive, negative, and inconclusive findings with equal specificity.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Packing Strategies

All strategies share the same inputs: a query, a pool of candidate evidence chunks with retrieval similarity scores, and a token budget. They differ in how they select and order chunks.

**Top-k similarity packing (baseline).** Candidates are sorted by descending similarity score. Chunks are appended in order until the token budget is exhausted. This is the standard retrieval-augmented prompt construction approach.

**Random-order lottery packing.** Candidates are shuffled in a random order (controlled by a seed), then appended until the budget is filled. This strategy tests whether any reordering helps, without a position objective.

**Searched position lottery packing.** This strategy uses retrieval scores and a lost-in-middle position weighting function to evaluate candidate prompt layouts. The position objective assigns higher weight to evidence placed at the beginning and end of the prompt, reflecting the lost-in-middle attention pattern. The search explores different subsets and orderings of candidates within the token budget, selecting the layout that maximizes the position-weighted quality proxy. Crucially, the objective does not use ground-truth answer labels; it relies only on retrieval scores and position weights.

**Exact-top-k reorder lottery packing (diagnostic).** This variant preserves the exact same set of chunks and token count as the top-k baseline, searching only over their ordering. It isolates pure position effects from retrieval/kept-set effects.

### 2.2 Adaptive Strategy

After initial experiments revealed a regression at the 300-token budget (where the candidate-pool search changed the kept set in ways that hurt answer quality), we refined the searched position lottery to operate adaptively:

- **Tight budgets** (180, 240 tokens): Use the original candidate-pool search, which may select a different kept set than the baseline.
- **High budgets** (300 tokens): Switch to exact-top-k reordering, preserving the baseline's kept set and token count while searching only over chunk order.

This adaptive design prevents the searched strategy from introducing harmful kept-set changes when the budget is already generous enough for the baseline to capture most relevant evidence.

### 2.3 Implementation

All packers are implemented as dependency-free Python functions in `src/position_lottery.py`. The synthetic benchmark harness is in `experiments/run_synthetic_benchmark.py`, and the model-facing QA/citation harness is in `experiments/run_model_qa_benchmark.py`. Unit tests reside in `tests/test_position_lottery.py`.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Synthetic Offline Proxy Benchmark

The synthetic benchmark generates 120 deterministic citation/repo-QA cases with evidence chunks and distractors designed to expose lost-in-middle sensitivity. Each case is evaluated at three token budgets (180, 240, 300) with 5 repeats per strategy, yielding 1,800 rows per strategy family across budgets.

**Table 1.** Synthetic benchmark: searched position lottery vs. top-k similarity baseline.

| Budget | Strategy | Quality Proxy Δ vs. Top-k | Kept-Evidence Recall | Packer Tokens Δ vs. Top-k |
|--------|----------|---------------------------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| 180 | Searched lottery | +6.07 points | 0.442 vs. 0.375 | +3.25% |
| 240 | Searched lottery | +8.49 points | 0.602 vs. 0.514 | +3.16% |
| 300 | Exact top-k reorder | −0.31 points | Matches top-k (0.686) | +0.0% |

At the two tighter budgets, the searched lottery improves both the quality proxy and kept-evidence recall, at the cost of 3–4% more packer tokens. At the 300-token budget, the exact-top-k reorder variant yields a negligible quality proxy decrease (−0.31 points) with identical recall and token usage to the baseline.

Pure random-order lottery underperformed top-k similarity across all budgets, indicating that unsupervised random shuffling is not a useful mechanism in this setting.

### 3.2 Small-Model QA/Citation Evaluation

The model-facing harness uses HuggingFaceTB/SmolLM2-135M-Instruct (a 135M-parameter instruction-tuned model) to generate answers over 12 cases, 3 budgets (180, 240, 300), and 3 query phrasing variants (direct, citation-first, terse), with `max_new_tokens=48`. Metrics include answer exact match, answer F1, citation precision/recall/F1, kept-evidence recall, tokenizer prompt tokens, packer tokens, construction latency, generation latency, and total latency.

**Table 2.** Small-model QA/citation results: searched position lottery vs. top-k similarity, aggregated across all three phrasings.

| Budget | Strategy | Answer F1 | Answer F1 Δ | Citation F1 | Citation F1 Δ | Prompt Tokens Δ | Kept-Evidence Recall |
|--------|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| 180 | Top-k | 0.250 | — | 0.239 | — | — | 0.333 |
| 180 | Searched lottery | 0.417 | +16.7 pp | 0.294 | +5.6 pp | +5.1% | 0.472 |
| 240 | Top-k | 0.250 | — | 0.211 | — | — | 0.500 |
| 240 | Searched lottery | 0.333 | +8.3 pp | 0.283 | +7.2 pp | +7.2% | 0.639 |
| 300 | Top-k | 0.250 | — | 0.272 | — | — | 0.722 |
| 300 | Searched lottery (exact top-k reorder) | 0.472 | +22.2 pp | 0.261 | −1.1 pp | +0.0% | 0.722 |

The searched position lottery improves answer F1 at all three budgets, with the largest gain at the 300-token budget (+22.2 pp). Citation F1 improves at the two tighter budgets but regresses slightly at 300 tokens (−1.1 pp). The tight-budget candidate-pool search incurs a 5–7% prompt token overhead; the 300-token exact-top-k reorder adds no token overhead.

**Diagnostic result.** The exact-top-k reorder strategy alone (without the adaptive switch) is harmful at 240 tokens, producing an answer F1 decrease of −16.7 pp relative to top-k. This confirms that the useful mechanism is genuinely adaptive: candidate-pool search under tight budgets and kept-set-preserving reorder at higher budgets.

**Latency.** Construction latency for the searched lottery was approximately 0.21–0.27 ms in the synthetic benchmark. In the model evaluation, total latency was roughly flat or slightly lower for the searched lottery compared to top-k (e.g., 340 ms vs. 344 ms at budget 180; 325 ms vs. 331 ms at budget 300), though these differences are within noise for a 12-case evaluation.

### 3.3 Negative and Mixed Findings

Several findings qualify the positive results:

1. **No token savings achieved.** The original hypothesis included a token-savings component (≥15% fewer tokens for equivalent quality). The searched lottery does not achieve this: tight-budget search uses 5–7% *more* tokens, and the 300-token variant uses the same number. The token-savings hypothesis is unsupported.

2. **Citation F1 regression at 300 tokens.** Despite the large answer F1 gain at 300 tokens, citation F1 decreases by 1.1 pp. The model generates better answers but slightly less accurate citations at this budget.

3. **Pure random shuffling is harmful.** The random-order lottery consistently underperforms top-k similarity, confirming that position optimization requires a principled objective, not arbitrary reordering.

4. **Isolated exact-top-k reordering is harmful at tight budgets.** At 240 tokens, reordering the top-k set without changing its composition reduces answer F1 by 16.7 pp. The benefit at tight budgets comes from changing *which* evidence is kept, not merely from reordering.

5. **Earlier 300-token regression.** Before the adaptive refinement, the candidate-pool search at 300 tokens produced an answer F1 of 0.222 vs. 0.250 (−2.8 pp) and a citation F1 of 0.197 vs. 0.272 (−7.5 pp). The adaptive switch to exact-top-k reorder eliminated this regression for answer F1 but did not fully recover citation F1.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Small model scale.** All model evaluations use SmolLM2-135M-Instruct, a 135M-parameter model. Whether the observed position sensitivity and the effectiveness of searched layout transfer to larger models (1B+, 7B+, 70B+) is unknown.

2. **Small and synthetic benchmarks.** The synthetic benchmark uses 120 deterministic cases with constructed evidence and distractors. The model evaluation uses only 12 cases. Both are designed to expose lost-in-middle effects but may not represent the distribution of real-world retrieval-augmented QA tasks.

3. **Limited phrasing coverage.** Three query phrasing variants (direct, citation-first, terse) are tested. Natural user queries exhibit far greater diversity.

4. **No token savings.** The method improves quality at a given budget but does not reduce the token budget required for equivalent quality. The original ≥15% token-savings criterion is not met.

5. **Deterministic seeds only.** All experiments use fixed seeds. Variance across random seeds is not characterized with sufficient sample sizes to report confidence intervals.

6. **Single model and hardware configuration.** Results are from one model on one local machine. No cross-model or cross-hardware replication is present.

7. **Position objective is heuristic.** The lost-in-middle position weighting is a hand-specified function, not learned from data. Its optimal form may differ across models and tasks.

8. **Citation F1 inconsistency.** The 300-token budget shows a slight citation F1 regression despite improved answer F1, suggesting the position optimization may trade off citation accuracy for answer quality at some operating points.

## 6. Conclusion

We presented an adaptive searched position lottery method for prompt packing in retrieval-augmented QA. The method improves answer F1 by +8.3 to +22.2 percentage points over top-k similarity packing across three token budgets in a small-model evaluation, and improves citation F1 by +5.6 to +7.2 points at the two tighter budgets. The adaptive design—candidate-pool search at tight budgets and kept-set-preserving reordering at higher budgets—is necessary: pure random shuffling, isolated exact-top-k reordering, and non-adaptive candidate-pool search all produce regressions at some budgets.

However, the method does not achieve token savings, and citation F1 regresses slightly at the highest budget. The results are confined to a single 135M-parameter model, synthetic and small-scale benchmarks, and deterministic seeds. The current project artifacts support the finding that searched prompt layout improves quality-at-budget in the tested setting, but do not support claims of universal effectiveness or token efficiency. Follow-up work should test the adaptive packer on larger, less synthetic QA/citation benchmarks with a stricter no-extra-token variant and across a broader range of model scales.
