## Abstract

We present a self-contained, NumPy-only dense pruning harness designed to produce actionable, evidence-backed pruning-policy comparisons before committing computational budget to real-model or hardware-benchmark runs. The harness trains a two-hidden-layer ReLU MLP on a synthetic four-class classification task, applies five no-finetune pruning policies across five sparsity levels, and reports accuracy, loss, and timing metrics over five random seeds (125 total pruned evaluations). Global magnitude weight pruning retained 95.7% of dense baseline accuracy at 50% sparsity while degrading sharply at 85% sparsity (−31.2 accuracy points) and 95% sparsity (−48.0 points). Informed pruning policies clearly separated from random baselines: at 70% sparsity, global magnitude pruning outperformed random weight masks by 32.7 percentage points, and activation saliency unit pruning outperformed random unit masks by 21.4 percentage points. However, dense masked execution did not demonstrate a deployment speedup—weight-mask timing was indistinguishable from dense timing within noise, and unit masking was slower. These results establish the harness as a viable fast calibration gate for pruning policy selection on this synthetic task, but they do not constitute transformer-scale pruning evidence or production acceleration proof. The structured claim ledger for this artifact remains in a blocked-empty state; no claims have passed formal audit.

## Introduction

Neural network pruning research faces a recurring methodological tension: rigorous policy comparison requires controlled experiments, yet realistic evaluation demands large models, long training runs, and hardware-specific sparse kernels. This tension often forces practitioners to either commit expensive resources to unvalidated pruning strategies or to proceed without empirical calibration.

We investigate whether a minimal, self-contained benchmark can serve as a fast calibration gate—producing evidence sufficient to decide whether a pruning idea deserves real-model budget before that budget is spent. We operationalize this as a "dense prune harness": a synthetic task, a small dense network, multiple no-finetune pruning policies, and multi-seed evaluation with accuracy, loss, and timing metrics.

The research question is deliberately narrow: can such a harness separate useful pruning policies from random masks and detect an unsafe sparsity cliff? We do not ask whether the harness proves production speedup or directly transfers to transformer architectures. The answer, on this synthetic task, is conditionally positive: the harness separates informed policies from random baselines and identifies a sparsity cliff, but the evidence is bounded by the simplicity of the task and architecture.

## Method

### Task and Model

The harness generates a synthetic dense nonlinear four-class classification task from a fixed teacher network with interaction terms. The pruned model is a two-hidden-layer dense ReLU MLP, trained from scratch using a manual NumPy implementation of the Adam optimizer and backpropagation. No deep learning framework dependencies exist beyond NumPy 2.4.4.

### Pruning Policies

Five no-finetune pruning policies are evaluated:

1. **Global magnitude weight pruning** (`global_magnitude_weight`): Weights are ranked globally across all layers by absolute magnitude; the lowest-magnitude weights are zeroed.
2. **Layerwise magnitude weight pruning** (`layerwise_magnitude_weight`): Weights are ranked independently per layer; each layer has the same fraction pruned.
3. **Random weight pruning** (`random_weight`): Weights are selected uniformly at random for zeroing, serving as the weight-level baseline.
4. **Activation saliency unit pruning** (`activation_saliency_unit`): Units are ranked by first-order saliency $|a_j \cdot \partial L / \partial a_j|$ computed on validation data, where $a_j$ is the activation of unit $j$. The lowest-saliency units are zeroed.
5. **Random unit pruning** (`random_unit`): Units are selected uniformly at random for zeroing, serving as the unit-level baseline.

No fine-tuning is performed after pruning; all metrics reflect immediate post-pruning damage rather than recoverable accuracy.

### Sparsity Levels

Each policy is evaluated at five sparsity levels: 0.30, 0.50, 0.70, 0.85, and 0.95, yielding 25 policy–sparsity combinations per seed.

### Evaluation Protocol

Five independent runs with different random seeds are executed. Each run produces 25 pruned evaluations, for a total of 125 observations. Metrics collected include:

- Dense baseline test accuracy and loss
- Pruned test accuracy and loss
- Accuracy delta (pruned − dense)
- Retained accuracy fraction (pruned accuracy / dense accuracy)
- Dense-vs-pruned forward pass timing ratio
- Training throughput (examples per second)
- Memory snapshots from `/proc/meminfo` and max RSS via `/usr/bin/time -v`

### Execution Modes

The harness supports three modes:

- **Smoke**: A fast sanity check with reduced configuration.
- **Calibration**: An intermediate run to verify metric ranges.
- **Main**: The full five-seed benchmark producing the reported results.

### Environment

All runs executed on a Linux aarch64 host (NVIDIA GB10 visible but unused). Python 3.12.3, NumPy 2.4.4. No PyTorch installation was present; the harness ran CPU/NumPy only. Swap was disabled (SwapTotal: 0 kB). Peak RSS was 46,116 kB; available memory after the main run was 122,668,768 kB.

### Safe Region and Sparsity Cliff

At 50% sparsity, global magnitude weight pruning retained 95.7% of dense accuracy (Δacc = −0.0364), suggesting a plausible safe operating region for moderate pruning on this task. At 85% sparsity, accuracy dropped by 31.2 points; at 95% sparsity, by 48.0 points. This sharp degradation identifies an aggressive-pruning cliff between 50% and 85% sparsity.

Whether this cliff location generalizes beyond this synthetic task and architecture is unknown.

### Policy Separation from Random Baselines

Informed pruning policies consistently outperformed their random counterparts. The separation widened at intermediate sparsity (70%: +32.7 pp for global magnitude vs. random weight; +21.4 pp for activation saliency vs. random unit) and narrowed at both extremes—near-zero sparsity (where all policies perform similarly) and near-total sparsity (where all policies collapse toward chance).

At 95% sparsity, the advantage of activation saliency over random unit pruning collapsed to +0.7 pp, indicating that at extreme sparsity the saliency signal provides negligible guidance for this architecture.

### Activation Saliency Underperforms Weight Magnitude

Activation saliency unit pruning underperformed global magnitude weight pruning at every sparsity level. At 50% sparsity, activation saliency accuracy was 0.7368 versus 0.8193 for global magnitude—a gap of 8.3 pp. This suggests that first-order activation saliency without fine-tuning is a weaker pruning criterion than weight magnitude for this architecture, though the comparison is confounded by the difference between weight-level and unit-level pruning granularity.

### Timing

Dense masked forward-pass timing ratios were near noise around 1.0 for weight-level pruning policies. Unit masking was slower than dense execution. No production acceleration was observed or expected: the harness evaluates pruned models via dense matrix multiplications with zero entries, not through sparse kernels or structured compaction. Timing results from this harness must not be interpreted as deployment-speed evidence.

### Resource Usage

The main five-seed benchmark completed in 2.54 seconds. Mean training throughput was 1,438,828 examples per second. Peak RSS was 46,116 kB. No swap activity occurred (SwapTotal: 0 kB confirmed).

## Limitations

1. **Synthetic task and architecture.** The benchmark uses a synthetic four-class task and a two-hidden-layer MLP. Results do not directly transfer to transformer or LLM architectures, where attention patterns, residual connections, and embedding structure may alter pruning dynamics substantially. This is a toy-scale simulation, not a production validation.

2. **No fine-tuning after pruning.** All metrics reflect immediate post-pruning damage. Real deployment typically involves fine-tuning or iterative pruning with retraining, which can recover substantial accuracy. The harness measures damage, not recoverable damage. Policies that appear catastrophic under immediate evaluation may be recoverable with fine-tuning.

3. **Dense execution is not sparse execution.** Pruned weights are zeroed but matrices remain dense. Timing ratios reflect overhead from masking logic, not sparse-kernel acceleration. No production speedup claim is warranted from these timing numbers. This is a fundamental limitation of the harness design: it can rank policies by accuracy damage but cannot predict real speedup.

4. **Unit vs. weight pruning confound.** The comparison between activation saliency (unit-level) and global magnitude (weight-level) is confounded by granularity. The appropriate comparison is activation saliency vs. random unit pruning, and global magnitude vs. random weight pruning. Cross-granularity comparisons should be interpreted cautiously.

5. **Single task complexity.** The synthetic task, while nonlinear with interaction terms, may not capture the difficulty structure of real-world tasks. A policy that performs well here may fail on tasks with different loss landscapes, and vice versa.

6. **No cross-architecture validation.** The harness tests one architecture. Whether the safe-region and cliff patterns generalize to deeper, wider, or architecturally different networks is unknown.

## Conclusion

The dense prune harness demonstrates that a lightweight, NumPy-only benchmark can produce actionable pruning-policy evidence in under three seconds on commodity hardware. It successfully separates informed pruning policies from random baselines (up to 32.7 pp at 70% sparsity) and identifies a sparsity cliff between 50% and 85% where accuracy degrades sharply. These findings support the harness's intended role as a fast calibration gate: a tool for deciding whether a pruning idea merits real-model or hardware-benchmark investment.

However, the results carry clear and substantial boundaries. The synthetic MLP task does not represent transformer-scale pruning behavior. The absence of fine-tuning means the harness measures immediate damage rather than recoverable accuracy. Dense masked execution provides no evidence for production acceleration. The claim ledger remains in a blocked-empty state, meaning no structured claims have been formally audited. Any claim about LLM pruning quality or deployment speedup derived from this harness alone would be unsupported.

The appropriate use of this harness is as a first-pass filter: if a pruning policy cannot beat random masks on this synthetic task, or if it collapses at moderate sparsity, it is unlikely to improve on more complex architectures without significant modification. If it passes, the policy warrants—but does not guarantee—further investment in real-model evaluation with sparse kernels and fine-tuning. Passing this gate is necessary but not sufficient for pruning-policy viability.
