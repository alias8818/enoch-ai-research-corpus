## Abstract

We investigate whether dense-to-sparse training schedules improve the trainability of hard top-k sparse attention on a synthetic associative retrieval task. A model that receives dense attention during early training and is later constrained to sparse top-k selection is compared against sparse-from-start training, gradual density annealing, and hard cutover schedules. Under moderate sparsity (top-4 of 16, density 0.25), all schedules converge to perfect accuracy, rendering the task insufficiently discriminative. Under extreme sparsity (top-1 of 16, density 0.0625) with a limited training budget (120 steps), sparse-from-start training fails near chance level (mean accuracy 0.105 ± 0.022), while every dense-warm-start variant—linear curriculum, cubic curriculum, and hard 50% cutover—achieves perfect target-sparse accuracy (1.000 ± 0.000). However, gradual density annealing did not outperform hard cutover or dense-only training evaluated sparsely. These results support the narrowed claim that dense warm-start before hard sparse attention is viable and substantially more sample-efficient than sparse-from-start under extreme top-k sparsity on this controlled task, but do not demonstrate superiority of gradual curriculum schedules over simpler alternatives, nor do they constitute evidence for real LLM training improvements, sparse-kernel efficiency, or perplexity retention.

## Introduction

The quadratic cost of dense attention motivates sparse attention mechanisms that select only a subset of key positions per query. Prior work has established that large pretrained models can tolerate substantial post-training sparsity (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023, arXiv:2301.00774), that gradual pruning during training can preserve accuracy at high sparsity ratios (Zhu & Gupta, 2017, arXiv:1710.01878), and that learned sparse attention patterns can be distilled from dense attention behavior (Gao et al., 2024, arXiv:2410.13276).

A natural hypothesis is that training with a dense-to-sparse curriculum—beginning with full attention and gradually restricting to sparse top-k selection—should yield better sparse attention models than training with hard sparsity from initialization. The intuition is that early dense training allows the model to form useful attention patterns before sparsity constraints restrict gradient flow to non-selected positions. If correct, this would suggest that gradual annealing schedules offer a training advantage beyond what is achieved by simply pretraining densely and evaluating sparsely.

We test this hypothesis on a controlled synthetic associative retrieval task using hard top-k attention masking. We compare five training schedules: dense-only, sparse-from-start, linear density annealing, cubic density annealing, and hard cutover (dense for half of training, then sparse). Our results confirm that dense warm-start dramatically improves trainability under extreme sparsity, but we find no evidence that gradual annealing outperforms simpler alternatives. The positive finding is specific to a toy retrieval task and does not generalize to claims about real LLM training or sparse-kernel efficiency.

## Method

### Task

We use a synthetic associative retrieval task. Each sample contains 16 key/value item pairs and one query key. The model must perform one-hop attention to retrieve the value paired with the query key. This is a classification task over 16 possible values, with chance accuracy of 1/16 ≈ 0.0625.

### Model

A single-layer attention model computes query-key dot-product attention over the 16 item positions. In sparse mode, hard top-k masking is applied to attention logits before softmax: only the top-k logits are retained, and all others are set to negative infinity. The model then outputs a weighted sum of item values according to the (sparse) attention distribution.

### Training Schedules

Five schedules are compared:

1. **dense**: Full attention throughout training and evaluation.
2. **sparse_static**: Hard top-k attention at the target sparsity from step 0.
3. **linear_curriculum**: Density linearly annealed from 1.0 to the target density over the first 70% of training steps, then held at target.
4. **cubic_curriculum**: Density cubically annealed from 1.0 to the target density over the first 70% of training steps, then held at target.
5. **hard_cutover**: Dense attention for the first 50% of training steps, then hard switch to target sparsity.

### Experimental Configurations

Two regimes were tested:

**Main run.** Target density 0.25 (top-4 of 16), 800 training steps, 5 random seeds (0–4), batch size 128, evaluation batch size 256, 30 evaluation batches.

**Stress run.** Target density 0.0625 (top-1 of 16), 120 training steps, 10 random seeds (0–9), batch size 128, evaluation batch size 256, 40 evaluation batches.

### Environment

All experiments ran on CPU using PyTorch 2.11.0+cpu with NumPy 2.4.4, Python 3.12.3. Swap was disabled and confirmed via system telemetry. An NVIDIA GB10 GPU was present on the machine but was not used by the CPU-only PyTorch wheel. Training time per trial was approximately 4 seconds. These are toy simulation results on a single-layer attention model, not production-scale validation.

### Key Observations

1. **Dense warm-start is critical under extreme sparsity.** The gap between sparse_static (0.105) and every dense-warm-start variant (1.000) is large and consistent across 10 seeds.

2. **Gradual curriculum does not outperform hard cutover.** Linear curriculum, cubic curriculum, and hard cutover all achieve identical perfect accuracy. The gradual annealing schedules provide no measurable advantage over simply switching from dense to sparse at a fixed step.

3. **Dense-only training with sparse evaluation also succeeds.** The dense schedule, when evaluated under top-1 sparse attention, achieves perfect accuracy, suggesting that the attention patterns learned under dense training are already compatible with extreme sparsity at inference time on this task.

4. **The failure mode of sparse-from-start is consistent with gradient starvation.** Hard top-k masking at initialization prevents the model from attending to (and receiving gradient signal from) positions that are not in the top-k, creating a chicken-and-egg problem where the model cannot learn to attend to the correct position because it cannot initially select it.

## Limitations

This study has several important limitations that constrain the generality of its conclusions:

1. **Toy task only.** The synthetic associative retrieval task with 16 items is far simpler than real language modeling or long-context reasoning. The task saturates at moderate sparsity, providing no discriminative signal between schedules. Whether these findings transfer to transformer language models remains unknown.

2. **No real LLM or language modeling.** Results are from a single-layer attention model on a controlled retrieval task. No claims can be made about perplexity, downstream benchmark quality, or long-context performance in transformer language models.

3. **No sparse-kernel latency measurements.** Hard top-k masking on unstructured attention logits does not correspond to an efficient sparse attention kernel. No actual wall-clock speedup was measured or claimed. Block-sparse or structured sparse attention implementations may exhibit different training dynamics and realized efficiency.

4. **No FLOP-equivalent comparison.** Dense-warm-start schedules consume more FLOPs during the dense phase than sparse-from-start. An equal-compute comparison (more sparse-from-start steps versus fewer dense-then-sparse steps) was not conducted, so the sample-efficiency advantage of warm-start may not translate to a compute-efficiency advantage.

5. **Gradual curriculum not differentiated.** The experiment does not support the claim that gradual dense-to-sparse annealing is superior to hard cutover or dense pretraining with sparse inference. All dense-warm-start variants performed identically, which is a negative result for the curriculum hypothesis.

6. **Single sparsity structure.** Only hard top-k item-level masking was tested. Block-sparse patterns, sliding-window-plus-sink structures, and other sparsity regimes used in practice may exhibit different training dynamics.

7. **CPU-only execution.** All runs used CPU PyTorch. No GPU-specific behavior, kernel fusion effects, or memory hierarchy interactions were tested.

8. **Limited task difficulty range.** Only two sparsity levels were tested. The moderate setting was too easy; the extreme setting produced a binary success/failure outcome. Intermediate regimes with more nuanced performance differences were not explored.

## Conclusion

Dense warm-start before hard sparse top-k attention is a viable training strategy and can be dramatically more sample-efficient than sparse-from-start under extreme top-k sparsity on a controlled associative retrieval task. Under top-1-of-16 sparsity with 120 training steps, sparse-from-start training fails near chance level (accuracy 0.105), while all dense-warm-start variants achieve perfect accuracy. However, gradual density annealing (linear or cubic) did not outperform a simple hard cutover or dense-only training with sparse evaluation in this setting. This is a negative result for the hypothesis that gradual curriculum schedules provide a training advantage beyond what dense pretraining alone provides.

The positive result is specific to a toy retrieval task with unstructured top-k masking and does not constitute evidence for real LLM training improvements, sparse-kernel efficiency, perplexity retention, or the superiority of gradual curriculum schedules. Future work should test block-sparse causal attention in tiny transformer language models under equalized FLOP budgets, with both quality metrics (perplexity, retrieval accuracy) and realized latency from actual sparse kernels, to determine whether the warm-start advantage observed here transfers to settings where sparsity yields measurable computational savings.
