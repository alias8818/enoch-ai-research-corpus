## Abstract

Speculative decoding accelerates autoregressive inference by drafting token sequences with a cheap model and verifying them against a target. We investigate whether a shallow prefix of the target model itself—augmented with a lightweight auxiliary exit head—can serve as the drafter, eliminating the need for a separate draft model. In a controlled synthetic benchmark with a 12-layer deterministic autoregressive target, we train multinomial linear heads at early layers to imitate the full target's greedy next-token decisions and simulate speculative decoding under an explicit verifier-cost model. The auxiliary-head condition at layer 3 (25% depth) with draft length 4 yields an estimated speedup of 1.33×, with 76.5% per-token draft acceptance and a mean accepted prefix of 3.06 tokens. The naive baseline—applying the target's final LM head directly to early hidden states—achieves near-zero acceptance and no speedup, despite the target having only 2.27 bits of label entropy. These results are cost-model estimates on a synthetic target, not wall-clock measurements on a real transformer. We conclude that target-internal early-exit drafting is conditionally viable but requires trained auxiliary heads; reusing the final LM head on early representations is ineffective. Real-model validation remains necessary for scientific closure.

## Introduction

Speculative decoding reduces the latency of autoregressive generation by proposing multiple candidate tokens in parallel and verifying them against a target model in a single forward pass. Conventional approaches use a separate, smaller draft model. An alternative design—self-speculative decoding—uses the target model itself at reduced depth, avoiding the engineering and memory cost of maintaining a second model.

A natural question arises: can one simply exit the target model early, apply the final output projection to the early hidden state, and use the resulting distribution as a draft? If early layers already encode sufficient information about the final output, this would require no additional training. If not, a lightweight auxiliary head trained at the early exit point might recover enough signal to draft effectively.

This study tests both strategies in a controlled setting. We construct a synthetic 12-layer autoregressive target with accessible internal states, train auxiliary linear heads at selected early layers to mimic the full target's greedy decisions, and compare against the naive strategy of reusing the final LM head on early states. We simulate greedy speculative decoding and estimate speedup under an explicit cost model that accounts for verification overhead and the fractional cost of the early exit.

The central findings are mixed:

1. **Auxiliary heads work conditionally.** A trained linear head at layer 3 achieves 86.6% accuracy against the full target's greedy labels and produces drafts with 76.5% per-token acceptance, yielding an estimated 1.33× speedup.
2. **The naive approach fails decisively.** Applying the final LM head to layer-3 hidden states achieves only 1.4% accuracy—far below the 44.0% majority-class baseline—and produces no accepted drafts.
3. **Deeper exits trade cost for accuracy.** A layer-2 exit in preliminary runs showed higher acceptance (~86%) but the calibration and final runs converged on layer 3 as the best speedup point, as deeper exits consume more of the target's compute budget.

These results are confined to a synthetic target and a parametric cost model. They do not constitute a production throughput claim.

## Method

### Synthetic Target

We implement a deterministic 12-layer autoregressive target in NumPy. Each layer applies a fixed affine transformation followed by a nonlinearity. The model maps a one-hot token input through all 12 layers to produce a softmax distribution over a finite vocabulary. Internal hidden states at every layer are accessible for probing.

The target's label distribution has an entropy of 2.270 bits, indicating moderate predictability. The majority-class baseline (always predicting the most frequent token) achieves 44.0% accuracy, establishing a non-trivial but structured prediction problem.

### Auxiliary Exit Heads

For each candidate early layer $l \in \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$, we train a multinomial logistic regression head (via scikit-learn) on a fixed training set of hidden-state / greedy-label pairs drawn from the full target. The auxiliary head at layer $l$ receives the hidden state $\mathbf{h}_l$ as input and produces a distribution over the vocabulary. Training uses the full target's greedy next-token label as the supervision target.

We also evaluate the **raw shared-LM-head** condition: applying the target's final output projection (the LM head from layer 12) directly to $\mathbf{h}_l$, with no auxiliary training.

### Speculative Decoding Simulation

We simulate greedy speculative decoding with draft length $K = 4$. At each step:

1. The early-exit drafter (auxiliary head or raw LM head at layer $l$) proposes $K$ tokens greedily.
2. The full target verifies the draft by computing its own greedy continuations for each prefix position.
3. The longest matching prefix is accepted; the first mismatching token is replaced by the target's token; remaining draft tokens are discarded.

This is a deterministic simulation: no sampling stochasticity, no temperature. Acceptance rates reflect exact greedy agreement between drafter and target.

### Cost Model

Estimated speedup is computed as:

$$\text{verifier\_cost}(K) = 1 + \alpha \cdot (K - 1)$$

$$\text{block\_cost} = \text{verifier\_cost}(K) + K \cdot \frac{l}{L}$$

where $L = 12$ is the total depth, $l$ is the exit layer, and $\alpha$ is the per-token verification overhead beyond the first token. The speedup ratio is the baseline cost (one full forward pass per token) divided by the expected cost per emitted token under the speculative regime.

This is an analytical cost model, not a measured kernel throughput. The parameter $\alpha$ was calibrated from the smoke run and applied consistently to calibration and final runs.

### Runs

Three sequential runs were performed:

| Run | Purpose | Notes |
|-----|---------|-------|
| Smoke | Initial feasibility check | Small-scale; identified layer 2 as promising |
| Calibration | Refined verifier-cost parameter | Used smoke-derived $\alpha$; layer 2 showed 1.70× estimated speedup |
| Final | Larger-scale evaluation | 84,460 emitted tokens; best result at layer 3 |

The final run is the primary result. The calibration run's higher speedup (1.70×) reflects a different cost-model parameterization; the final run used a more conservative setting.

## Limitations

1. **Synthetic target.** The 12-layer autoregressive model is a deterministic NumPy construct, not a trained transformer. Its internal representations, attention patterns, and residual stream dynamics do not reflect those of production language models. The 2.27-bit label entropy and 44% majority-class baseline indicate a problem that is structured but simpler than natural language.

2. **Cost-model speedup, not wall-clock speedup.** The 1.33× figure is an analytical estimate under a parametric verifier-cost model. Actual speedup on a real transformer depends on KV-cache layout, memory bandwidth, kernel fusion, batch scheduling, and verification implementation—none of which are modeled here. The sensitivity of the estimate to the cost-model parameter $\alpha$ is demonstrated by the gap between the calibration run (1.70×) and the final run (1.33×).

3. **Greedy-only evaluation.** Both drafting and verification use greedy decoding. Sampling-based speculative decoding—where the target's distribution, not its argmax, defines acceptance—may yield different acceptance rates and speedup trade-offs.

4. **Linear auxiliary heads only.** The exit heads are multinomial logistic regressors. More expressive heads (small MLPs, low-rank adapters) might improve accuracy at shallower layers, but this was not tested.

5. **Single target configuration.** Only one synthetic target (12 layers, fixed vocabulary, deterministic weights) was evaluated. The results do not generalize across model sizes, architectures, or domains without further evidence.

6. **No real-model validation.** This is the most significant limitation. The viability of target-internal early-exit drafting on an actual pretrained language model—where representation geometry, layer specialization, and training dynamics differ substantially from the synthetic setting—remains untested.

7. **Draft length fixed at 4.** Longer drafts (e.g., $K = 8$ or $K = 16$) may change the speedup/accuracy trade-off but were not evaluated.

## Conclusion

This study provides a constrained positive signal for target-internal early-exit drafting in speculative decoding, alongside a clear negative result for the naive alternative.

**Positive finding.** A trained auxiliary linear head at 25% depth of a synthetic 12-layer target can recover 86.6% of the full target's greedy next-token decisions, yielding drafts with 76.5% per-token acceptance and an estimated 1.33× speedup under a parametric cost model. The median draft is fully accepted (4/4 tokens), indicating that the auxiliary head frequently captures the target's greedy trajectory over multi-token spans.

**Negative finding.** Applying the target's final LM head directly to early hidden states is ineffective: 1.4% accuracy, 0% draft acceptance, no speedup. The final-layer projection is misaligned with early-layer representations, and this misalignment cannot be resolved without retraining.

**Constraint.** These results are confined to a synthetic target and an analytical cost model. They demonstrate that the structural idea—using a shallow prefix with a trained exit head as a self-speculative drafter—is not ruled out by representation-alignment failures, but they do not establish that the approach works on real transformers under real inference workloads. The sensitivity of the speedup estimate to the cost-model parameter (1.70× under one parameterization, 1.33× under another) further cautions against treating these figures as robust predictions.

The recommended next step is to train auxiliary early-exit heads on a small open pretrained language model (at 25%, 33%, and 50% depth) and benchmark self-speculative decoding in an inference engine that can verify drafted tokens without redundant KV-cache computation. Until such a real-model validation is completed, the 1.33× figure should be treated as a cost-model estimate on a toy benchmark, not a production speedup claim.
