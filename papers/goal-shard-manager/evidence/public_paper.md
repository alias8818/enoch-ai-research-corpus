## Abstract

Long-running autonomous agents are susceptible to autonomous drift: work that appears complete but lacks required evidence, merges with unverified dependencies, or silently abandons lifecycle stages. We propose a Goal Shard Manager that decomposes long goals into durable shards, each carrying an owner, typed evidence requirements, expiry and reassignment rules, and merge gates. To evaluate this design, we implemented a deterministic toy-model simulator comparing a naive flat task-queue policy against the shard policy across 30 simulated experiments (180 lifecycle shards per trial) over 500 random seeds. The shard policy achieved a mean clean completion rate of 1.000 versus 0.189 for the naive policy (paired Δ = 0.811, 95% CI [0.804, 0.818]), eliminated bad merges entirely (0 vs. 44.088 per run), and reduced mean ticks to completion from 66.1 to 54.8. However, these results derive from a toy simulation whose assumptions encode the hypothesized benefit mechanisms; they do not constitute production validation. Budget-market behavior was not tested. We identify four failure modes for subsequent prototype evaluation and recommend proceeding to a minimal real implementation tested against matched historical control-plane runs.

## Introduction

Autonomous research agents operating over extended horizons face a governance problem: without explicit lifecycle constraints, work products can drift from their original specifications while still appearing complete. A flat task queue that accepts work without evidence verification may report high completion rates while admitting substantial integrity failures—bad merges, missing evidence, and stale or orphaned tasks.

The Goal Shard Manager design addresses this by imposing structure on the lifecycle of each goal. Rather than treating a goal as a single atomic task, it is decomposed into durable shards, each representing a distinct lifecycle stage. Each shard carries:

- **An owner** responsible for its completion.
- **Typed evidence requirements** that must be satisfied before the shard can be closed.
- **Expiry and reassignment rules** that prevent indefinite stalling.
- **Merge gates** that block aggregation of work with unsatisfied dependencies.

The central hypothesis is that these mechanisms reduce autonomous drift—measured as the gap between apparent completion and clean completion—while maintaining or improving throughput.

This paper reports results from a deterministic toy-model simulation of this design, comparing it against a naive flat task-queue policy. We emphasize at the outset that this is a simulation study whose assumptions encode the hypothesized benefit mechanisms; the results provide a measurable signal within the model but do not demonstrate that the approach works in production workflows.

## Method

### Simulator Design

We implemented a deterministic toy-model simulator in pure Python 3.12.3 (`src/goal_shard_sim.py`) using only the standard library. The simulator models a control plane managing a set of experiments, each decomposed into six lifecycle shards: `plan`, `smoke`, `calibrate`, `execute`, `verify`, and `report`. For 30 experiments, this yields 180 lifecycle shards per trial.

Simulated agents have bounded capacity and stochastic work quality. Each tick, agents select available work, attempt to complete it, and may succeed or fail according to the configured quality distribution. The simulator is fully deterministic given a random seed.

### Policies Compared

Two control-plane policies were evaluated:

1. **Naive policy**: A flat multi-agent task queue that accepts incomplete or drifted work with probabilistic review leakage. No explicit evidence gates, ownership, or expiry mechanisms.

2. **Shard policy**: Each lifecycle stage is a durable shard with an assigned owner, typed evidence requirements that must be met before closure, expiry timers that trigger reassignment on stall, and merge gates that prevent aggregation of work with unsatisfied upstream dependencies.

### Experimental Configuration

Three simulation scales were run:

- **Smoke test**: 2 experiments, 3 seeds, max 40 ticks. Used to verify simulator correctness.
- **Calibration**: 30 experiments, 20 seeds, max 160 ticks. Used to estimate throughput and memory requirements.
- **Full run**: 30 experiments, 500 seeds, max 160 ticks. Primary result set.

All runs used paired mode: each seed produces one naive run and one shard run under identical random conditions, enabling paired difference analysis.

### Metrics

- **Accepted completion rate**: Fraction of experiments reported complete (regardless of evidence integrity).
- **Clean completion rate**: Fraction of experiments where all six lifecycle shards have verified evidence and no bad merges.
- **Bad merges per run**: Count of merge events where upstream evidence was missing or invalid.
- **Evidence failures per run**: Count of lifecycle shards closed without satisfying their typed evidence requirements.
- **Ticks to finish**: Simulation ticks until all experiments are reported complete.
- **Throughput**: Merged tasks per tick.
- **Composite score**: A weighted aggregate combining the above metrics (higher is better).

### Environment

All runs executed on a Linux aarch64 host (kernel 6.17.0-1014-nvidia) with approximately 122.6 GB available memory. Swap is disabled. The `earlyoom` daemon is active with a 4% memory threshold. No memory pressure was observed during any run. Calibration throughput was 312.7 paired-mode runs/sec; the full 500-seed run completed in 3.123 seconds at 320.2 runs/sec.

### Interpretation

The accepted completion rate is uninformative: both policies eventually report all 30 experiments complete. The useful discriminative signal is the clean completion rate. Under the naive policy, only 18.9% of experiments finish with all lifecycle evidence verified and no bad merges; the remaining 81.1% contain at least one integrity violation. The naive policy admits a mean of 44.1 bad merges per run.

Under the shard policy, clean completion reaches 100.0% with zero bad merges by construction—the evidence gates and merge criteria prevent closure without verified evidence. The shard policy also finishes faster (mean 54.8 ticks vs. 66.1) and achieves higher throughput (3.30 vs. 2.75 tasks/tick), suggesting that the governance overhead is more than compensated by reduced rework from bad merges.

Evidence failures are not eliminated entirely under the shard policy (mean 40.9 per run), but they are reduced by approximately 21 per run relative to naive. The residual failures reflect shards that fail evidence checks on first attempt but are subsequently corrected through reassignment and retry, as opposed to being silently accepted.

### Smoke Test Consistency

The smoke test (2 experiments, 3 seeds) showed the same directional pattern: naive clean completion rate 0.50, shard clean completion rate 1.00; naive bad merges 2.0, shard bad merges 0.0. The small sample precludes statistical inference but confirms simulator consistency across scales.

## Limitations

This study has several important limitations that constrain the strength of its conclusions:

1. **Toy-model design encodes hypothesized benefits.** The simulator represents evidence gates, expiry, and merge criteria as effective mechanisms by construction. The result demonstrates that these mechanisms produce measurable improvements within the model's assumptions, but does not independently validate that they work in real workflows. The simulation should not be over-read as proof of real-world efficacy.

2. **Budget-market component not implemented.** The original hypothesis mentioned budget markets as a governance mechanism. This component was not included in the simulation; only ownership, typed evidence, expiry/reassignment, and merge gates were tested.

3. **No production validation.** These results are from a deterministic simulation, not from a real Goal Shard Manager operating on actual agent teams. Real agents may exhibit behaviors not captured by the stochastic quality model.

4. **Potential failure modes remain untested:**
   - Shard overhead may dominate when real agents have high context-switch costs.
   - Evidence schemas may become bureaucratic or gameable in practice.
   - Expiry and reassignment may lose critical context in real code or research tasks.
   - The interaction between shard governance and budget markets is unknown.

5. **Stochastic model fidelity.** The simulator uses a simple stochastic model for agent work quality. Real agent failure modes are likely more correlated and path-dependent than this model captures.

6. **Composite score weighting.** The composite score combines multiple metrics with fixed weights. Different weightings could change the relative ranking of policies on specific dimensions.

7. **Missing readiness audit signal.** The review pipeline flagged a missing `readiness_audit` signal, indicating that not all automated quality gates were satisfied prior to finalization.

## Conclusion

A deterministic toy-model simulation of 30 experiments across 500 random seeds demonstrates that a Goal Shard Manager policy—comprising durable shard ownership, typed evidence requirements, expiry and reassignment, and merge gates—substantially reduces autonomous drift relative to a naive flat task queue. The clean completion rate improves from 0.189 to 1.000 (paired Δ = 0.811, 95% CI [0.804, 0.818]), bad merges are eliminated, and throughput increases by 0.55 tasks/tick. These results are consistent and statistically clear within the simulation.

However, the simulation encodes the hypothesized benefit mechanisms as effective by construction, and the budget-market component was not tested. The results provide a measurable signal and a concrete target for prototype development, but they do not constitute production validation. We recommend proceeding to a minimal real Goal Shard Manager implementation, tested on actual queued research projects against matched historical or randomized control-plane baselines, with particular attention to the four identified failure modes: shard overhead, bureaucratic evidence schemas, context loss on reassignment, and budget-market interactions.
