## Abstract

Speculative decoding with multiple training-free candidate sources can exhibit substantial heterogeneity in per-token acceptance rates. The Goose framework (arXiv:2604.02047) proposes that fixed-budget speculation trees should be anisotropic: reliable context-match tokens form a deep spine while lower-acceptance transition candidates are allocated as wide fallback branches concentrated near the root. This paper reports a bounded mechanism study that reproduces and stress-tests the anisotropic-tree yield claim using a dependency-free Python simulator with analytic expected-value computation and Monte Carlo validation (200,000 trials per condition). At the paper-reported median acceptance heterogeneity ratio of approximately 6×, the optimized anisotropic tree achieves a +12.08% expected-yield gain over a balanced mixed-probability baseline under a 63-node budget, with Monte Carlo error below 0.002 tokens. Gains are positive and substantial at ratios 8× (+21.78%) and 12× (+18.62%), but mixed or negative at lower ratios (−15.47% at 1×, −6.36% at 4×), confirming that anisotropy is not beneficial when source quality is homogeneous. These results land at the low end of the original paper's reported 12–33% gain range, which is consistent with the simplified model used here. This study does not constitute a full systems reproduction: no LLM inference, tree-attention kernel, or real tokens-per-second measurement was performed. The result supports the anisotropic-tree mechanism as viable under the paper's acceptance model and recommends a distinct implementation-level replication as next work.

## 1. Introduction

Speculative decoding accelerates autoregressive language model inference by verifying draft tokens against a target model in parallel. When multiple training-free candidate sources are available—such as a context-matching retriever and a transition-predicting smaller model—their acceptance rates can differ substantially. The Goose paper (arXiv:2604.02047, submitted 2026-04-02) reports acceptance heterogeneity ratios $p_s / p_t$ ranging from 2× to 18×, with a median of approximately 6×, and claims equal-budget expected-yield gains of 12–33% for anisotropic trees over isotropic (balanced) baselines.

The core mechanism claim is intuitive: when one candidate source is much more reliable, the speculation tree should invest depth along the reliable spine (since each additional accepted spine token compounds expected yield) while allocating breadth to unreliable fallback branches near the root (where the probability of reaching them is highest). The paper provides a Proposition-1-style expected-yield bound and demonstrates gains in a full serving system.

However, the mechanism claim can be evaluated independently of the serving system. This study asks: does the anisotropic allocation principle hold under a simplified but paper-grounded acceptance model, and what magnitude of gain does it produce? Answering this requires only an analytic bound computation and Monte Carlo validation—not a full LLM serving stack.

We report results from such a study. Section 2 describes the simulation method. Section 3 presents results across seven heterogeneity ratios. Section 4 discusses limitations. Section 5 provides a reproducibility checklist. Section 6 concludes.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Paper-Grounded Bound Model

The simulator implements the variables described in the Goose paper:

- **Reliable spine probability** $p_s$: the acceptance probability for context-match candidates. Set to 0.42 throughout, consistent with the paper's reported range for draft-model acceptance.
- **Transition probability** $p_t = p_s / r$: the acceptance probability for transition candidates, where $r$ is the heterogeneity ratio.
- **Node budget** $B = 63$: the total number of speculation tree nodes available.
- **Spine length** $m$: the number of sequential reliable-token nodes forming the deep spine.
- **Branch widths** $w_i$: the number of low-quality fallback children at spine depth $i$.
- **Branch extension depth** $D = 6$: the maximum depth of branch subtrees.

The allocation constraint is:

$$m + \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} w_i \leq B$$

The simulator performs integer optimization over spine length $m$ and branch widths $w_i$ to maximize the Proposition-1-style expected yield. Branch widths are constrained to be nonincreasing with depth, reflecting the paper's observation that optimal branch allocation decreases with depth.

### 2.2 Simplified Exact Tree DP

A secondary sanity-check model was implemented with a stricter abstraction: at most one high-quality child and low-quality leaf fallbacks at each accepted node. This model provides an independent lower bound but is not the primary basis for the reported results.

### 2.3 Monte Carlo Validation

For each heterogeneity ratio, 200,000 Monte Carlo trials simulate the token acceptance process on the optimized tree structure. Each trial walks the tree according to the acceptance probabilities, recording the number of accepted tokens. The empirical mean is compared to the analytic expected value.

### 2.4 Baselines

Two baselines are computed for each ratio:

1. **Balanced tree**: A mixed-probability tree where all nodes use the average acceptance probability $(p_s + p_t)/2$, with equal branching. This represents an isotropic allocation that ignores source heterogeneity.
2. **Pure spine**: A tree consisting entirely of reliable spine nodes (no transition branches). This represents the opposite extreme of ignoring the lower-quality source entirely.

### 2.5 Environment

The simulation was executed on an aarch64 Linux host with an NVIDIA GB10 GPU (idle, 0% utilization during the run). The run was CPU-only. Available memory was 121,129,512 kB with swap intentionally disabled (SwapTotal: 0 kB). Wall-clock runtime was approximately 1.23 seconds with maximum RSS of approximately 15 MB.

### 2.6 Ratios Tested

Heterogeneity ratios $r \in \{1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, 18\}$, spanning the range reported in the Goose paper.

### 3.2 Monte Carlo Agreement

The maximum absolute error between the analytic paper-bound expected value and the Monte Carlo estimate across all ratios is 0.0072 tokens (at ratio 1). All other ratios show errors below 0.004 tokens. This confirms that the analytic computation is correctly implemented and that the Monte Carlo trials are sufficient for the precision required.

### 3.3 Anisotropic Structure

The optimized allocations exhibit the expected anisotropic pattern. At ratio 6×, the spine has length 6 with 57 branch nodes distributed as [31, 19, 7, 0, 0, 0]—heavily concentrated at the root. As heterogeneity increases, branch width concentrates further: at ratio 18×, the allocation is [47, 10, 0, 0, 0, 0], essentially a single wide layer of fallbacks at the root.

### 3.4 Gain Pattern

The gain versus the balanced baseline is not monotonic in the heterogeneity ratio. It is negative at ratio 1 (−15.47%), slightly positive at ratio 2 (+4.31%), negative again at ratio 4 (−6.36%), then positive and substantial at ratios 6, 8, and 12, before declining at ratio 18 (+9.75%). The non-monotonicity at low ratios reflects the fact that when sources are nearly equal in quality, the anisotropic allocation wastes budget on an artificially deep spine for one source while starving the other, whereas the balanced tree uses both sources more evenly.

The peak gain in this study is +21.78% at ratio 8×, which falls within but at the low end of the Goose paper's reported 12–33% range. This is consistent with the simplified model: the simulator uses a single fixed $p_s$ and derives $p_t$ from the ratio, whereas the real system has per-token acceptance probabilities that vary by context.

### 3.5 Negative and Mixed Results

Two findings deserve emphasis as constraints on the mechanism's applicability:

1. **At ratio 1 (homogeneous sources), anisotropic allocation is harmful.** The −15.47% gain versus balanced confirms that the anisotropic principle should not be applied when source quality is similar. The mechanism is not universally beneficial.

2. **At ratio 4, the gain is slightly negative (−6.36%).** This suggests a transition zone between ratios 2 and 6 where the benefit is marginal or absent. Practitioners should verify that acceptance heterogeneity is sufficiently large before deploying anisotropic trees.

## 4. Limitations

This study has several important limitations that constrain the generality of its conclusions:

1. **No full LLM serving reproduction.** The simulator operates on abstract acceptance probabilities, not on real model outputs. No tree-attention kernel, vLLM/SGLang integration, or actual draft-target verification was performed. Real tokens-per-second, time-to-first-token (TTFT), inter-token latency (ITL), and GPU utilization were not measured.

2. **Simplified acceptance model.** The simulator uses a single scalar $p_s$ and derives $p_t$ from the heterogeneity ratio. Real acceptance rates vary per token and per context. The paper's Proposition-1 bound may be tighter or looser under realistic per-token distributions.

3. **Balanced baseline is a simplified abstraction.** The balanced baseline uses the average acceptance probability for all nodes, which is not identical to the paper's exact isotropic baseline implementation. The gain percentages may differ slightly against the paper's own baseline.

4. **Single budget and spine probability.** Only $B = 63$ and $p_s = 0.42$ were tested. The gain magnitudes and optimal allocations may differ substantially at other budgets or base acceptance rates.

5. **No implementation harness existed locally.** The project directory initially contained no code or benchmark harness. All code was written for this study. No public project-specific repository was accessible.

## 6. Conclusion

This bounded mechanism study provides analytic and Monte Carlo evidence supporting the core claim of anisotropic speculation trees: when candidate sources have substantially different acceptance rates, a fixed-budget tree should allocate depth to the reliable spine and breadth to low-quality fallback branches near the root. At the paper-reported median heterogeneity of approximately 6×, the optimized anisotropic tree yields a +12.08% expected-yield gain over a balanced baseline, with Monte Carlo validation error below 0.002 tokens. The gain magnitude is at the low end of the original paper's 12–33% range, consistent with the simplified single-probability model used here.

The results also delineate the mechanism's boundaries. At homogeneous source quality (ratio 1×), anisotropic allocation is harmful (−15.47%). At moderate heterogeneity (ratio 4×), the benefit is marginal or negative (−6.36%). The mechanism becomes reliably beneficial only at ratios of approximately 6× and above under the tested conditions.

These findings constitute a bounded positive mechanism result, not a full systems reproduction. The recommended next step is a distinct implementation-level replication: integrating GOOSE-style candidate tree construction into a serving framework such as vLLM or SGLang, and measuring accepted tokens per verify step, tokens per second, TTFT, ITL, GPU utilization, and memory behavior under realistic workloads on the GB10 platform.
