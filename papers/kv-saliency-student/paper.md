## Abstract

We investigate whether a lightweight student model, given only insertion-time token and key features, can predict the future saliency of key-value (KV) cache entries well enough to inform cache eviction decisions. In a controlled synthetic attention simulator, a histogram-gradient-boosting student policy retained a mean of 46.6% (SD 0.21%) of future attention mass at a 20% KV budget across three random seeds, compared to 5.4% for recency eviction and 20.1% for random eviction; the oracle upper bound was 47.2%. Top-20 saliency classification ROC-AUC averaged 0.991 and Spearman rank correlation averaged 0.966 across seeds. A supplementary smoke test on a tiny GPT-2 model showed near-perfect student recovery of oracle saliency, but this result is not scientifically decisive because the model's attention patterns appear to be almost fully determined by positional features. No end-to-end perplexity or generation-quality evaluation with actual cache eviction was performed. These findings establish that future KV saliency is learnable from insertion-time features in a controlled setting, but do not yet demonstrate production cache-eviction gains.

## Introduction

Autoregressive language models maintain a growing KV cache during inference, creating memory pressure at long sequence lengths. Practical systems must decide which cache entries to retain and which to evict. Common heuristics—such as recency-based eviction—ignore the fact that attention distributions are highly non-uniform: some earlier tokens receive disproportionate future attention mass relative to their recency.

If one could predict, at the time a KV entry is inserted, how much total future attention it will receive, a policy could retain the most salient entries and evict the rest. We call this quantity *future saliency*. The oracle policy sorts entries by their true future saliency and retains the top fraction; it provides an upper bound on retention for any budget.

This work asks: can a lightweight student model, trained on insertion-time features (token embeddings, key vectors, positional information), predict future saliency accurately enough to approximate oracle retention? We evaluate this question in two settings:

1. A controlled synthetic attention simulator with designed saliency structure.
2. A tiny GPT-2 model from HuggingFace Transformers, as a smoke test.

We report retention of future attention mass, classification AUC, and rank correlation for three student architectures (Ridge regression, Random Forest, Histogram Gradient Boosting) against oracle, recency, and random baselines. We are careful to distinguish the synthetic simulator results—which demonstrate learnability under designed structure—from the tiny-GPT2 smoke test, and from what would be required to validate the approach as a production cache policy.

## Method

### Problem Formulation

For a sequence of length $T$, each position $t$ has a KV cache entry. Let $s_t = \sum_{u=t+1}^{T} \alpha_{u,t}$ denote the *future saliency* of position $t$, where $\alpha_{u,t}$ is the attention weight from query position $u$ to key position $t$. Given a budget $b$ (fraction of entries to retain), the oracle policy keeps the top-$b$ fraction of entries ranked by $s_t$.

A student policy receives features available at insertion time—token embedding, key vector, and positional index—and predicts $\hat{s}_t$. The policy then retains entries with the highest $\hat{s}_t$ values up to the budget.

### Primary Metric

The primary metric is *retention*: the fraction of total future attention mass captured by the retained entries. Formally, if $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of retained positions:

$$\text{retention} = \frac{\sum_{t \in \mathcal{R}} s_t}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} s_t}$$

A student is useful if it materially outperforms recency and random baselines and approaches oracle retention. Retention is a proxy for downstream quality; it does not directly measure perplexity or generation degradation, which limits the strength of conclusions that can be drawn from it.

### Student Architectures

Three student models were evaluated:

- **Ridge regression**: Linear model with L2 regularization, fit on insertion-time features. Serves as a simple baseline for the student family.
- **Random Forest (rf\_student)**: Ensemble of decision trees with default scikit-learn hyperparameters.
- **Histogram Gradient Boosting (hgb\_student)**: Gradient-boosted decision trees with histogram-based splitting (scikit-learn `HistGradientBoostingRegressor`).

All models were trained on a per-sequence basis: for each sequence, features at insertion time were collected, true future saliency was computed from the full attention matrix, and the student was fit on a training split and evaluated on a held-out split. This protocol assumes access to the full attention matrix for the training portion of each sequence, which is a simplification relative to a deployed system that would need to apply a pre-trained student zero-shot to new sequences.

### Backends

**Synthetic simulator.** A controlled attention simulator generates sequences with designed saliency structure. Parameters for the full run: sequence length 160, model dimension 48, vocabulary size 768, 900 sequences (split into training and held-out evaluation). The simulator produces attention patterns where saliency is partially predictable from insertion-time features but not trivially so. This is a toy simulation: it demonstrates learnability under designed structure, not under the attention distributions of real language models.

**HuggingFace tiny-GPT2.** A small causal language model (GPT-2 architecture) was used with 48 sequences of length 96 (15 held-out). This serves as a smoke test on real model attention. The model is very small, and as discussed below, its attention patterns appear to be almost fully determined by position, making saliency trivially predictable. This result should not be treated as evidence for larger models.

### Experimental Protocol

1. **Environment smoke test**: Hardware and dependency verification.
2. **Synthetic smoke test**: 8 sequences, synthetic backend, to verify pipeline correctness.
3. **Full controlled run**: 900 sequences, synthetic backend, seed 11, with held-out evaluation (270 test sequences).
4. **Replication**: 3 random seeds (3, 11, 29), full controlled runs, to assess variance.
5. **HF tiny-GPT2 run**: 48 sequences, length 96, seed 13.

Budgets tested: 10%, 20%, 30% of KV entries retained. The 20% budget is the primary reporting point, consistent with the project's stated goal.

### Environment

Experiments ran on an aarch64 Linux system (kernel 6.17.0-1014-nvidia) with 121 GB RAM and an NVIDIA GB10 GPU (CUDA 13.0, driver 580.142). Software: Python 3, PyTorch 2.11.0+cu130, Transformers 5.7.0, scikit-learn 1.8.0.

## Limitations

1. **Synthetic setting only for the primary result.** The controlled simulator has designed saliency structure. While this demonstrates that saliency is learnable when structured signal exists, it does not establish that real LLM attention patterns contain the same degree of predictability from insertion-time features. The simulator result is a toy simulation, not a production validation.

2. **Tiny-GPT2 result is not scientifically decisive.** The near-perfect student performance on the tiny model likely reflects position-dominated attention rather than a general property of transformer attention. This run serves as a pipeline smoke test, not as evidence for larger models.

3. **No end-to-end eviction evaluation.** Retention of attention mass is a proxy for downstream quality. No experiment measured the effect of actual KV eviction on perplexity, generation quality, or downstream task performance. High attention mass retention does not guarantee low quality degradation, as the relationship between attention mass and output quality may be nonlinear—some low-mass entries may be critical for specific tokens or reasoning steps.

4. **Per-sequence training protocol.** Students were fit per-sequence on training splits, which assumes access to the full attention matrix for the training portion of each sequence. A deployed system would need a student trained on prior sequences and applied zero-shot to new sequences, which was not evaluated. The per-sequence protocol inflates the apparent predictability of saliency relative to what a deployed system would achieve.

5. **Small number of replication seeds.** Three seeds provide a preliminary variance estimate but are insufficient for strong distributional claims about the stability of the result.

6. **No comparison to established eviction policies.** Beyond recency and random, no existing cache eviction heuristics (e.g., frequency-based policies, attention-score-threshold policies, streaming policies from the systems literature) were evaluated. The comparison set is limited.

7. **Single model scale for real-model evidence.** The only real-model experiment used a tiny GPT-2. No medium or large model was tested, so no claim can be made about how saliency predictability scales with model size.

## Conclusion

A lightweight student model can predict future KV-cache saliency from insertion-time features in a controlled synthetic setting, retaining a mean of 46.6% (SD 0.21%) of future attention mass at a 20% KV budget—compared to 5.4% for recency and 47.2% for oracle—across three random seeds. Classification AUC exceeds 0.99 and Spearman rank correlation exceeds 0.96. These results establish that future saliency is learnable when structured signal exists in the attention distribution.

However, this work stops short of demonstrating practical cache-eviction gains. The primary positive result is from a toy simulation with designed saliency structure; the only real-model experiment used a tiny GPT-2 whose attention is trivially predictable from position; and no end-to-end perplexity or generation-quality evaluation with actual cache eviction was performed. The viability of KV-saliency students as a production cache policy remains an open question.

The recommended next step is to collect student training data from a real small-to-medium LLM on diverse corpus prompts, train a student on prior sequences for zero-shot application to new sequences, and evaluate actual cache eviction by dropping predicted-low-saliency KV entries while measuring perplexity and generation degradation against recency and oracle baselines.
