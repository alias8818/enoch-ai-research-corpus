## Abstract

When inference-time memory pressure requires reducing per-token runtime state, one strategy is to skip entire transformer layers. We investigate whether a calibrated policy—skipping layers with the lowest observed residual-delta norm during a brief calibration phase—preserves full-model output better than static heuristics (skip-tail, skip-front, uniform, random) at matched skip counts. In a dependency-light NumPy simulation of a 24-layer residual MLP stack across four random seeds and four pressure levels (12.5%–50% layers skipped), the calibrated policy achieved the lowest relative mean squared error in all 16 of 16 comparisons. At 12.5% skip fraction, calibrated relative MSE was 0.000204 versus 0.00378 for the best baseline (a 14.3× improvement ratio); at 50% skip fraction, calibrated relative MSE was 0.00953 versus 0.0239 (a 2.5× improvement ratio). Class-agreement proxies remained above 0.91 even at 50% skip fraction. Modeled speedups scaled with skip count (1.18×–2.04×). These results support the viability of a calibration-driven layer-skip controller as a memory-pressure response mechanism. However, all experiments use a synthetic residual stack rather than a pretrained transformer, and memory pressure is modeled as a skip-fraction parameter rather than induced via actual memory exhaustion. The findings motivate but do not constitute production validation.

## 1. Introduction

Inference on large language models under memory-constrained conditions—such as edge devices with no swap, or shared servers approaching `MemAvailable` thresholds—creates pressure to reduce per-token runtime state. Common mitigation strategies include KV-cache eviction, KV quantization, and early exit. An alternative is to skip entire layers at inference time, reducing both activation memory and compute.

The key question is *which* layers to skip. Naive policies—always skipping the final layers, the earliest layers, uniformly spaced layers, or a random subset—ignore the fact that layers in residual networks contribute unevenly to the output. Some layers produce small residual deltas and thus have limited effect on the final representation; others are critical.

We hypothesize that a brief calibration phase can identify low-contribution layers, and that skipping those layers preserves full-model output fidelity substantially better than any static policy at the same skip count. We test this hypothesis in a controlled simulation and report results with honest accounting of what the simulation can and cannot establish.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Residual-Stack Model

We implement a synthetic residual layer stack in NumPy. Each layer applies a fixed random linear transformation followed by ReLU, composed via residual connections:

$$\mathbf{x}_{l+1} = \mathbf{x}_l + \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_l \mathbf{x}_l + \mathbf{b}_l)$$

Weights $\mathbf{W}_l \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ and biases $\mathbf{b}_l \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are drawn once per seed and frozen. The stack is not trained; it serves as a reproducible testbed with heterogeneous per-layer contributions.

### 2.2 Calibration Phase

Before evaluation, a calibration batch of $C$ samples is forwarded through the full stack. For each layer $l$, we compute the residual-delta norm:

$$\delta_l = \|\mathbf{x}_{l+1} - \mathbf{x}_l\|_2$$

averaged over calibration samples. Layers are ranked by ascending $\delta_l$; the lowest-delta layers are candidates for skipping.

### 2.3 Skip Policies

At a given pressure level $p$ (fraction of layers to skip), we compare five policies:

| Policy | Selection criterion |
|---|---|
| `calibrated_low_delta` | Skip the $k = \lfloor p \cdot L \rfloor$ layers with lowest mean $\delta_l$ from calibration |
| `tail` | Skip the last $k$ layers |
| `front` | Skip the first $k$ layers |
| `uniform` | Skip every $L/k$-th layer, evenly spaced |
| `random` | Skip a seeded random subset of $k$ layers |

All policies skip the same number of layers at each pressure level, ensuring matched compute and activation-memory reduction.

### 2.4 Evaluation Metrics

- **Relative MSE**: $\text{MSE}(\mathbf{y}_{\text{skipped}}, \mathbf{y}_{\text{full}}) / \text{MSE}(\mathbf{y}_{\text{full}}, \mathbf{0})$, where $\mathbf{y}$ is the final stack output.
- **Class agreement**: Fraction of samples where the argmax of the skipped-stack output matches the argmax of the full-stack output.
- **Mean cosine similarity**: Cosine similarity between skipped and full outputs, averaged over samples.
- **Speedup vs. full**: Ratio of full-stack runtime to skipped-stack runtime.
- **Activation bytes avoided**: Modeled as $k \times d \times 4$ bytes (float32), representing per-layer activation storage that need not be retained when a layer is skipped.

### 2.5 Experimental Configuration

**Smoke test:** 8 layers, width 128, 256 samples, 64 calibration samples, pressure levels {0.125, 0.25}, seed 7.

**Main runs:** 24 layers, width 512, 4096 samples, 1024 calibration samples, pressure levels {0.125, 0.25, 0.375, 0.5}, seeds {7, 11, 17, 23}.

**Host environment:** Linux 6.17.0 on NVIDIA GB10 (aarch64), Python 3.12.3, NumPy 2.4.4. Swap disabled (SwapTotal: 0 kB). earlyoom active with threshold `-m 4`. GPU was visible but unused (CPU/NumPy harness). MemAvailable remained above 122 GB throughout; no real memory pressure was induced.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Smoke Test (8-layer, width 128)

The smoke test confirmed correct harness operation. Calibration identified layers 4 and 6 as having the lowest residual-delta norms (0.123 and 0.200, respectively, versus a stack range of 0.123–0.834). At pressure 0.125 (1 layer skipped), the calibrated policy selected layer 4, achieving relative MSE of 0.000118 and class agreement of 0.984. The best baseline (tail, skipping layer 7) achieved relative MSE of 0.000748. At pressure 0.25 (2 layers skipped), calibrated selected layers {4, 6} with relative MSE 0.000458 versus the tail baseline's 0.00106. The calibrated policy won both pressure levels in the smoke test.

### 3.2 Main Runs (24-layer, width 512)

Across all 4 seeds × 4 pressure levels (16 comparisons), the calibrated policy achieved the lowest relative MSE in every comparison.

| Skip fraction | Calibrated rel. MSE | Best baseline rel. MSE | MSE ratio (cal/best) | Class agreement | Speedup vs. full |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0.125 | 0.000204 | 0.00378 | 0.070 | 0.987 | 1.18× |
| 0.250 | 0.00152 | 0.00970 | 0.157 | 0.962 | 1.30× |
| 0.375 | 0.00443 | 0.01558 | 0.290 | 0.935 | 1.63× |
| 0.500 | 0.00953 | 0.02391 | 0.398 | 0.910 | 2.04× |

**Table 1.** Mean metrics across seeds 7, 11, 17, 23. "Best baseline" is the strongest non-calibrated policy at each pressure level. The MSE ratio column shows calibrated relative MSE divided by best-baseline relative MSE; values below 1.0 indicate calibrated superiority.

Key observations:

1. **Calibrated dominates consistently.** The improvement ratio is largest at low skip fractions (14.3× at 12.5%) and smallest at high skip fractions (2.5× at 50%), but the calibrated policy is never worse than any baseline in any seed or pressure level.

2. **Class agreement degrades gracefully.** Even when half the layers are skipped, the argmax of the output matches the full stack's argmax on 91.0% of samples.

3. **Speedups are real but modest.** The measured speedups (1.18×–2.04×) roughly track the fraction of compute avoided, as expected for a sequential residual stack where skipped layers simply reduce the forward-pass length.

4. **Baselines vary in which is "best."** The tail policy is typically the strongest baseline at low skip fractions; front and uniform are consistently worse. Random policy performance is intermediate and seed-dependent.

### 3.3 Calibration Cost

Calibration requires one forward pass through the full stack on $C$ samples. In the main configuration (24 layers, width 512, $C = 1024$), calibration completed in under 1 second. This overhead is amortized across all subsequent inference steps under pressure.

### 3.4 Memory Posture

No real memory pressure was induced during experiments. The system had ~122 GB MemAvailable with zero swap and earlyoom active. Pressure was modeled purely as a skip-fraction parameter. The activation-bytes-avoided metric is a model ($k \times d \times 4$ bytes) rather than a measured reduction in resident memory. This design was deliberate: inducing actual memory exhaustion on a system with active earlyoom and no swap risks killing unrelated processes.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Synthetic architecture.** The residual stack uses random, untrained weights. Real pretrained transformers have structured, learned residual contributions that may differ systematically from random matrices. The calibration mechanism's effectiveness on trained models remains untested.

2. **Simulated, not induced, memory pressure.** Pressure is represented as a skip-fraction parameter, not as an actual `MemAvailable` threshold crossing or OOM event. The controller's behavior under genuine memory pressure—including latency of detection, interaction with earlyoom, and cgroup memory limits—has not been validated.

3. **Activation memory only.** The modeled memory savings apply to per-layer activation and KV-like runtime state. Model weights remain resident regardless of skip count. For models where weights dominate memory, layer skipping provides no weight-memory benefit.

4. **No task-level evaluation.** Fidelity is measured as MSE and class agreement against the full-stack output, not as performance on language modeling, question answering, or retrieval tasks. A low MSE to the full stack does not guarantee preservation of downstream task quality, especially for tasks sensitive to subtle representation changes.

5. **Single architecture scale.** All main runs use 24 layers at width 512. Whether the relative advantage of calibration holds at different depths, widths, or architectural variants (attention layers, mixture-of-experts) is unknown.

6. **No comparison to alternative memory strategies.** KV-cache eviction, KV quantization, and early-exit mechanisms were not evaluated. The calibrated layer-skip approach may be complementary to or dominated by these alternatives in practice.

7. **Calibration sensitivity.** The calibration phase uses a fixed sample count and assumes calibration data is representative of inference data. Distribution shift between calibration and deployment could degrade layer rankings.

8. **Mixed result at high skip fractions.** While the calibrated policy remains dominant at 50% skip fraction, the improvement ratio shrinks to 2.5× and class agreement drops to 0.91. This suggests diminishing returns as pressure increases, and the practical utility of any layer-skip strategy at extreme skip fractions is questionable.

## 6. Conclusion

A calibrated layer-skip policy that selects layers by lowest residual-delta norm consistently outperforms static and random skip policies at matched skip counts in a synthetic 24-layer residual stack. The advantage ranges from approximately 14× lower relative MSE at 12.5% skip fraction to 2.5× at 50% skip fraction, with class agreement remaining above 0.91 even at the highest pressure level. These results support the viability of a calibration-driven layer-skip controller as a memory-pressure response mechanism.

However, this is a simulation result, not production validation. The synthetic residual stack, simulated pressure, absence of task-level evaluation, and lack of comparison to alternative memory strategies all limit the strength of the conclusion. The result motivates three concrete next steps: (1) replication on a small pretrained transformer, (2) integration with live `MemAvailable` or cgroup memory thresholds under controlled memory ceilings, and (3) comparison against KV eviction, KV quantization, and early-exit baselines on long-context retrieval tasks.
