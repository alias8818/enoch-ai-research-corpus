## Abstract

Large-language-model agents operating over long contexts routinely ingest transcripts containing high volumes of structurally obvious waste—duplicate logs, stack traces, telemetry pings, and boilerplate—alongside a small number of answer-bearing facts. We evaluate a cheap, deterministic "trash compactor" that scores lines by fact-likeness, penalizes trash and duplicates, and applies a weak recency prior, all without query awareness. On synthetic benchmarks with 1,000 cases and a trash ratio of 120:1, the compactor preserves 100% of marked facts at retention budgets as low as 3%, where naive tail retention preserves only 16.4%. Under a natural-fact ablation (no explicit fact markers), recall remains 97.2% at 3% retention and 100% at 5% and above. However, in dense, low-trash contexts (ratio 10:1) with natural facts, recall drops to 23.5% at 3% retention and 54.3% at 10%, still exceeding baselines but far from complete preservation. These results establish deterministic trash compaction as a viable pre-LLM hygiene layer for high-trash contexts such as agent transcripts and logs, but not as a standalone solution for dense, all-relevant contexts. Downstream LLM answer quality after compaction was not measured and remains an open question.

## 1. Introduction

Long-context language models can ingest tens of thousands of tokens, but the effective information density of real agent transcripts is often very low. A typical autonomous-agent session may accumulate duplicate log lines, repeated stack traces, periodic telemetry pings, boilerplate headers, and conversational chatter, while the facts necessary to answer a downstream query may number in the dozens. Naive context truncation—keeping the most recent tokens, or splitting the budget between head and tail—discards facts stochastically, with recall determined by the accidental position of answer-bearing lines relative to the truncation point.

We investigate whether a simple, deterministic, query-agnostic scoring pass can serve as a "trash compactor": a pre-LLM hygiene layer that removes obvious waste before more expensive semantic processing. The approach is intentionally cheap—no embedding calls, no LLM inference, no query—and relies on structural signals (fact-like syntax, duplicate detection, boilerplate pattern matching, recency) to decide which lines to retain under a token budget.

The central question is scoped: can deterministic trash rejection preserve answer-bearing facts better than naive retention when the context is dominated by obvious waste? And where does this approach break down?

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Synthetic Context Generation

The benchmark script (`scripts/benchmark_trash_compactor.py`) generates synthetic long contexts with two components:

- **Answer-bearing facts**: lines containing decisions, constraints, results, or other content relevant to downstream queries. In the default ("marked") configuration, these lines carry explicit structural markers (e.g., `FACT`, `keep_for_answer`). In the "natural-fact" ablation, markers are removed and facts appear as ordinary natural-language statements indistinguishable by marker alone.
- **Trash**: high-volume filler including duplicate log lines, stack traces, telemetry pings, boilerplate headers, and lorem/chatter.

Each case is generated with a configurable number of facts and a trash ratio (trash lines per fact line). Exact ground-truth fact IDs are embedded for evaluator-free recall scoring.

### 2.2 Compaction Methods

Five methods are compared:

1. **tail**: Retain the most recent lines until the token budget is exhausted.
2. **head_tail**: Split the budget evenly between the beginning and end of the context.
3. **dedup_tail**: Remove exact duplicate lines, then retain the tail within the budget.
4. **trash_compactor**: Query-agnostic scoring that rewards fact-like lines, penalizes trash/boilerplate/duplicates, and applies a weak recency prior. No access to the query or any embedding model.
5. **query_aware_oracle**: A needle-matching baseline that uses query keywords. In ID-marked cases this behaves as an oracle (the query needle is unique); in natural-fact ablations it degrades to a weak keyword baseline because query terms also appear in trash.

### 2.3 Metrics

- **Recall**: Fraction of answer-bearing facts exactly preserved in the compacted context. A fact is counted as preserved only if its line survives compaction intact.
- **Actual retained ratio**: The fraction of input tokens retained after compaction, which may differ slightly from the target due to line-boundary alignment.
- **Latency**: Wall-clock milliseconds per case.

Token counts are approximate lexical token counts, not tokenizer-specific BPE counts. This approximation is a known limitation (Section 5).

### 2.4 Experimental Configurations

Four benchmark runs were executed:

| Run | Cases | Facts/case | Trash ratio | Fact markers | Retention budgets |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smoke test | 5 | 6 | 20 | Marked | 10%, 20% |
| Full high-trash | 1,000 | 12 | 120 | Marked | 3%, 5%, 10%, 20% |
| Natural high-trash ablation | 200 | 12 | 120 | Natural | 3%, 5%, 10%, 20% |
| Dense low-trash ablation | 200 | 12 | 10 | Natural | 3%, 5%, 10%, 20% |

The smoke test (5 cases, 30 total facts) served as a sanity check before larger runs. The full high-trash run is the primary result. The two ablations test robustness to marker removal and to dense contexts respectively.

### 2.5 Environment

All runs executed on an NVIDIA GB10 system (`gx10-efe8`), Linux aarch64, kernel 6.17.0-1014-nvidia. Total system memory: 121 GiB; available before runs: 116 GiB. Swap disabled by design (SwapTotal: 0 kB). The benchmark is CPU-only; no GPU utilization occurred. Maximum RSS across the full run: 421,612 KiB (~412 MiB); zero swap events.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Smoke Test

The smoke test (5 cases, 6 facts/case, trash ratio 20, seed 20260430) confirmed basic functionality. At 10% retention, the trash compactor achieved 100% recall (30/30 facts preserved) versus 6.7% for tail (2/30) and 10.0% for dedup_tail (3/30). At 20% retention, the compactor again achieved 100% recall versus 26.7% for both tail and dedup_tail (8/30 each). These results are consistent with the larger runs but too small (N=5) to draw general conclusions.

### 3.2 High-Trash, Marked Facts

On 1,000 cases with 12 facts each (12,000 total facts) and a trash ratio of 120:1, the trash compactor preserved all 12,000 facts at every tested retention budget. Baseline methods lost 25–84% of facts at the same budgets.

| Target retention | Tail recall | Head+tail recall | Dedup+tail recall | Trash-compactor recall | Compactor ms/case |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 3% | 0.164 | 0.164 | 0.167 | **1.000** | 4.200 |
| 5% | 0.250 | 0.260 | 0.257 | **1.000** | 4.181 |
| 10% | 0.443 | 0.441 | 0.466 | **1.000** | 4.195 |
| 20% | 0.687 | 0.691 | 0.746 | **1.000** | 4.202 |

The baselines improve monotonically with budget but remain far below the compactor even at 20% retention. Dedup+tail modestly outperforms plain tail and head+tail, confirming that duplicate removal helps but is insufficient alone. The compactor's latency is stable across budgets at approximately 4.2 ms/case.

### 3.3 High-Trash, Natural Facts (No Markers)

Removing explicit fact markers degrades compactor recall slightly at the most aggressive budget:

| Target retention | Tail recall | Dedup+tail recall | Trash-compactor recall |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 3% | 0.157 | 0.158 | **0.972** |
| 5% | 0.250 | 0.259 | **1.000** |
| 10% | 0.440 | 0.466 | **1.000** |
| 20% | 0.704 | 0.758 | **1.000** |

At 3% retention, the compactor preserves 97.2% of natural facts—losing approximately 0.28 facts per case on average—compared to 15.7% for tail retention. At 5% retention and above, natural-fact recall matches the marked-fact condition at 100%. This suggests that when the trash ratio is high, structural trash rejection alone is sufficient to preserve nearly all facts even without explicit markers.

### 3.4 Dense, Low-Trash Contexts

This ablation represents the regime where most of the context is potentially relevant and the trash ratio is low (10:1). All methods struggle:

| Target retention | Tail recall | Dedup+tail recall | Trash-compactor recall |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 3% | 0.031 | 0.031 | **0.235** |
| 5% | 0.058 | 0.060 | **0.346** |
| 10% | 0.122 | 0.127 | **0.543** |
| 20% | 0.247 | 0.262 | **0.936** |

The compactor consistently outperforms baselines, but absolute recall is poor at low budgets. At 3% retention, only 23.5% of facts survive; at 10%, 54.3%. Even at 20% retention, 6.4% of facts are lost. This is the primary negative result: when the context is dense with potentially relevant natural-language content and the budget cannot accommodate all facts, a query-agnostic compactor cannot guarantee preservation.

### 3.5 Throughput and Resource Usage

The trash compactor processed high-trash cases at approximately 4.2 ms/case (1,000 cases, 4 budgets, 5 methods, total elapsed 51.0 s including all methods). The dense low-trash ablation ran faster at approximately 0.38 ms/case, likely due to shorter input contexts. The full run consumed a maximum of 412 MiB RSS with zero swap on a system with 116 GiB available, indicating no memory pressure concerns at this benchmark scale. These are prototype/simulation benchmark results on synthetic data, not production throughput measurements.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Synthetic data only.** The benchmark generates contexts with recognizable trash patterns (stack traces, telemetry, boilerplate) and clearly delineated facts. Real agent transcripts may exhibit more ambiguous boundaries between signal and noise. These results do not constitute production validation.

2. **Lexical token approximation.** Token counts are approximate lexical counts, not model-specific BPE tokenizations. Actual token budgets under real tokenizers may differ, potentially shifting the effective retention ratios reported here.

3. **No downstream LLM quality measurement.** Recall measures whether fact lines are preserved, not whether a downstream LLM can successfully answer questions using the compacted context. It is possible that preserved facts are insufficiently contextualized after compaction, or that removed "trash" lines provided useful framing. This experiment does not address end-to-end answer quality or latency.

4. **Dense-context failure mode.** The low-trash ablation reveals that the compactor's advantage collapses when the context is mostly relevant. At 3% retention in dense contexts, 76.5% of facts are lost. This approach is not sufficient for dense, all-relevant contexts without additional mechanisms (query-aware retrieval, user/agent pinning, or semantic summarization).

5. **No real-world agent traces.** Validation was performed exclusively on synthetic data. Performance on real, private agent transcripts with known answer probes remains untested.

6. **Query-agnostic design.** The compactor makes no use of the downstream query. In scenarios where the query is known at compaction time, query-aware retrieval would likely outperform the compactor, particularly in dense contexts.

7. **Line-level granularity.** Compaction operates at line granularity. Facts spanning multiple lines or embedded within longer blocks may not be preserved correctly.

8. **Favorable trash structure.** The synthetic benchmark assumes trash is structurally recognizable (log formats, stack traces, duplicate patterns). Real-world noise may be less distinguishable from signal, which would erode the compactor's advantage.

## 6. Conclusion

A deterministic, query-agnostic trash compactor can preserve 100% of marked facts and 97–100% of natural facts in high-trash long contexts at retention budgets as low as 3–5%, where naive baselines preserve only 16–26%. The cost is modest (~4 ms/case on CPU) and the implementation requires no LLM inference, embedding calls, or query access.

However, this approach has a clear boundary: in dense, low-trash contexts where most content is potentially relevant, query-agnostic compaction loses 46–77% of facts at 3–10% retention. The compactor still outperforms naive truncation in this regime, but the absolute recall is insufficient for reliable operation.

These results support a layered architecture for long-context management:

1. **Deterministic pre-pass**: exact deduplication, boilerplate/log/stacktrace suppression, structured-fact pinning, and recency fallback. This benchmark demonstrates that such a pass is safe and effective when trash is structurally obvious.
2. **Explicit pin/keep markers**: adding structured markers to agent memory and run-note formats makes extreme compaction safe, as shown by the marked-versus-natural fact comparison.
3. **Query-aware retrieval or semantic summarization**: for dense contexts where the deterministic pass cannot guarantee preservation, downstream semantic methods are necessary.

The primary open questions are whether these synthetic results transfer to real agent transcripts, and whether preserved facts remain useful for downstream LLM answer generation after compaction. Both require validation on real data with end-to-end quality measurement before claiming production readiness.
