## Abstract

We investigate whether a small, locally executed multiple-choice probe can separate three distinct capabilities in instruction-tuned language models: parametric factual recall, in-context counterfactual override following, and synthetic reasoning over invented symbol systems. Using a `llama.cpp`-based harness at temperature zero, we evaluate two quantized instruct models—Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct-Q4_K_M and Phi-4-mini-instruct-Q4_K_M—across 72 items each (24 per probe family). Both models achieve ceiling accuracy on direct factual recall (24/24, 1.000) and on simple counterfactual override following (24/24, 1.000), with zero memorization-lure errors. Both models drop to 16/24 (0.667, Wilson 95% CI [0.467, 0.820]) on synthetic reasoning items involving invented token orderings and function composition. The probe successfully separates factual/override competence from the selected synthetic reasoning workload, but the simple counterfactual condition fails to elicit memorization-interference failures, leaving the memory-lure question unresolved under these conditions. Sample sizes (n = 24 per family per model) are sufficient for a smoke-test diagnostic but not for publication-grade inference.

## Introduction

A central question in understanding large language model behavior is whether model outputs reflect parametric memorization, faithful in-context reasoning, or some mixture of the two. Prior work has examined tension between memorized facts and contextual instructions, but reproducible, lightweight diagnostics that can run on local hardware without API dependencies remain scarce. This study asks: **Can a small local multiple-choice probe separate parametric factual recall from in-context override following and synthetic reasoning?**

We design three probe families intended to tax different capabilities:

1. **Direct fact** — questions about real-world capitals, answerable from parametric knowledge alone.
2. **Counterfactual override** — prompts that state a fictional capital conflicting with the real one, requiring the model to follow the in-context override rather than its parametric knowledge.
3. **Synthetic reasoning** — prompts involving invented symbol orderings or function composition rules that have no memorizable answer, requiring on-the-fly reasoning.

If the probe split is informative, we expect high accuracy on direct facts (parametric recall is well-established in instruct models), high accuracy on counterfactual overrides (instruction-following should dominate when the override is explicit), and lower accuracy on synthetic reasoning (which cannot be solved by recall). A secondary question is whether counterfactual prompts will elicit *memorization-lure* errors, where the model reverts to its parametric answer despite an explicit override.

## Method

### Harness

The evaluation harness is implemented in `scripts/mem_reason_probe.py`, which uses `llama_cpp.Llama` for inference with temperature set to 0 (deterministic decoding). Each item is presented as a multiple-choice question with labeled options (A/B/C/D). The model's generated text is parsed to extract the chosen letter; items where parsing fails are recorded as unparsed but not counted as correct.

A separate script, `scripts/summarize_results.py`, aggregates raw JSONL outputs into per-model summaries and a combined metrics table.

This constitutes a `llama.cpp` hook-prototype harness: inference is performed via the `llama-cpp-python` binding on local GGUF files, not through a production serving pipeline. Results should be interpreted as prototype evidence rather than final production validation.

### Probe Families

- **Direct fact (`direct_fact`):** Capital-city questions for real countries. The correct answer is the real capital.
- **Counterfactual override (`counterfactual_override`):** A prompt states that a given country has a fictional capital (e.g., "In this scenario, the capital of France is New Tokyo"), then asks which city is the capital. The correct answer is the fictional capital stated in-context, not the real one. A *memory-lure* error is recorded when the model selects the real capital despite the explicit override.
- **Synthetic reasoning (`synthetic_reasoning`):** Prompts define an invented ordering over tokens or a simple function composition rule, then ask the model to apply the rule. No memorizable answer exists; the model must reason from the provided definitions.

### Key Observations

1. **Factual recall is at ceiling.** Both models answer all 24 direct-fact capital items correctly, consistent with well-established parametric knowledge in instruct-tuned models.

2. **Simple counterfactual override is at ceiling.** Both models follow the explicit fictional-capital instruction in all 24 counterfactual items, producing zero memorization-lure errors. The memory-lure rate is 0.000 for both models.

3. **Synthetic reasoning accuracy drops identically.** Both models score 16/24 (0.667) on synthetic reasoning. The Wilson 95% confidence interval [0.467, 0.820] is wide due to the small sample size, but the drop from ceiling is clear.

4. **No unparsed responses.** All 144 generated responses (72 per model) were successfully parsed; the harness experienced no format failures.

5. **Model size does not differentiate.** Despite differing parameter counts (~7B vs. ~3.8B), the two models produce identical accuracy profiles across all three probe families. Whether this reflects a ceiling effect on the first two families and a shared difficulty on the third, or a genuine equivalence, cannot be determined at this sample size.

### Negative Result: No Memorization-Lure Failures

The counterfactual override condition was designed to test whether models would revert to parametric knowledge when it conflicts with in-context instructions. Under the simple, explicit counterfactual prompts used here, neither model exhibited any such reversion. This is a negative result: the probe condition was insufficiently challenging to elicit memorization-interference errors. This does not demonstrate that such interference is absent in general—only that the present prompt design was too easy to detect it.

## Limitations

1. **Small sample size.** With n = 24 per probe family per model, confidence intervals are wide. The Wilson 95% CI on the 0.667 synthetic reasoning accuracy spans [0.467, 0.820], indicating substantial uncertainty about the true rate. These results constitute smoke-test evidence, not publication-grade inference.

2. **Possible option-position bias.** Errors on synthetic reasoning items showed some pattern consistent with letter/position bias in multiple-choice generation. Without balanced choice-order replicates or logit scoring over option letters, it is unclear whether errors reflect reasoning failures or generation-format artifacts.

3. **Counterfactual prompts were too easy.** The explicit "In this scenario, the capital of X is Y" framing made the override unambiguous. Real memorization–context conflicts often involve subtler cues—buried counterfactuals, paraphrase traps, or familiar-quote completion conflicts. The zero memory-lure rate may reflect prompt simplicity rather than robust instruction-following.

4. **Capital facts may be too easy.** World capitals are high-frequency, unambiguous factual items. Harder factual domains might yield different recall accuracy and different interference patterns.

5. **CPU-only inference path.** Despite GPU availability, all inference ran on the CPU path. While this does not affect logical correctness, CPU-path and GPU-path inference can produce different token sequences due to floating-point accumulation differences, so results may not transfer directly to GPU deployments of the same models.

6. **Single quantization level.** Both models were evaluated at Q4_K_M only. Results may differ at other quantization levels.

7. **Two models only.** Generalization to other model families, sizes, or training regimes is unknown.

8. **Claim audit status.** The structured claim ledger for this artifact recorded no formal claims and was flagged as `blocked_empty_claims`. The results reported here are drawn directly from the run notes and decision JSON; they have not passed a structured claim/evidence audit.

## Conclusion

A lightweight local multiple-choice probe successfully separates three capability levels in two quantized instruct models: ceiling factual recall, ceiling simple counterfactual override following, and sub-ceiling synthetic reasoning (0.667 for both models). The probe split is viable as a quick diagnostic. However, the simple counterfactual condition failed to elicit any memorization-lure errors, leaving the central question of memorization–context interference unresolved under these conditions. The identical accuracy profiles of the two models—despite different parameter counts—suggest either that the probe families are not sensitive enough to differentiate model capability at this sample size, or that both models genuinely share the same competence profile on these tasks.

To move from smoke test to rigorous evidence, future work should: (a) increase sample sizes substantially; (b) add balanced choice-order replicates and logit scoring to eliminate position-bias confounds; (c) design harder memorization-pressure conditions with buried counterfactuals, paraphrase traps, and familiar-quote completion conflicts; and (d) evaluate a broader range of models, quantization levels, and factual domains.
