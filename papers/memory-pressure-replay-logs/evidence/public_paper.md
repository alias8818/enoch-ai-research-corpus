## Abstract

We present Memory Pressure Replay Logs, an evaluation scaffold that replays serving traces under recorded memory-pressure conditions to compare LLM serving policies along a pressure-aware axis rather than solely a quality-centric axis. Across three tiers of evidence—synthetic weakly-labeled traces (n=3,000), a live OpenAI-compatible emulator with real OS telemetry (n=240), and a local Transformers model server on CUDA (n=80)—we observe that (1) quality-centric and memory-pressure replay rankings exhibit only moderate Spearman correlation (0.60–0.64), (2) replay evaluation exposes pressure-specific failure clusters (OOM aborts, long-context truncation) absent from quality-centric scoring, and (3) a targeted pressure-guarded fix policy achieves 5.2–10.1 points of uplift over the best non-fix baseline on the replay score. A five-seed robustness sweep confirms these findings with mean uplift of 8.5 points (minimum 8.0). However, the ranking-change criterion was mixed across seeds, and all model-server evidence uses a tiny model (sshleifer/tiny-gpt2), so production-scale generalization remains unvalidated. We document the method, results, and limitations with full artifact references.

## 1. Introduction

Evaluating LLM serving systems under memory pressure presents a gap between what quality-centric benchmarks measure and what deployment environments experience. Standard evaluation slices rank policies by output quality or throughput, but under unified memory architecture (UMA) pressure—where GPU and CPU share memory—serving policies that score well on quality may fail catastrophically via OOM aborts or long-context truncation.

Memory Pressure Replay Logs address this gap by recording serving traces with their memory-pressure context and replaying them under a pressure-aware scoring contract. The central hypothesis is that replay-log evaluation changes policy rankings relative to quality-centric evaluation, exposes pressure-specific failure clusters, and identifies targeted interventions with measurable uplift.

We test this hypothesis across three progressively realistic evidence tiers, reporting both positive and mixed findings with explicit limitations.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Trace Schema

Each trace record contains: request type (scheduler, RAG, long-context, speculative-decoding), memory-pressure tier (low, medium, high, critical), UMA serving budget (GiB), process RSS/PSS, latency, allocator events, and a weak quality label. Traces are stored as newline-delimited JSON and converted into a unified `Trace` replay contract consumed by all evaluation tiers.

### 2.2 Policies

Five serving policies are evaluated:

1. **quality_only**: Scores traces by output quality label only.
2. **throughput_greedy**: Maximizes throughput regardless of pressure.
3. **similarity_retrieval**: Ranks by retrieval similarity score.
4. **memory_aware_v1**: Incorporates memory-pressure tier as a soft penalty.
5. **pressure_guarded_fix**: A targeted intervention that guards against OOM and truncation failures under high/critical pressure, then scores remaining traces by quality.

### 2.3 Evaluation Slices

Two evaluation slices are computed for each run:

- **Quality-centric slice**: Ranks policies by aggregate quality/similarity score across all traces.
- **Memory-pressure replay slice**: Ranks policies by a replay score that weights pressure-specific failure penalties (OOM abort, long-context truncation) alongside quality.

The Spearman rank correlation between the two slices measures ranking divergence. Newly exposed failure clusters are identified as failure types present in the replay slice but not captured by quality-centric scoring.

### 2.4 Success Criteria

Three binary criteria were pre-specified:

1. **New failure cluster exposed**: The replay slice reveals at least one failure cluster absent from quality-centric scoring.
2. **Ranking changed**: The policy ordering differs between slices.
3. **Targeted fix ≥5-point uplift**: The pressure_guarded_fix policy achieves at least 5 points of replay-score uplift over the best non-fix baseline.

### 2.5 Annotation Cost Proxy

Weak labels supplemented by 5% spot checks are compared against full manual labeling to estimate annotation cost reduction.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Synthetic Weakly-Labeled Traces (n=3,000, seed 344)

Generated by `src/memory_pressure_replay_experiment.py`, a dependency-free deterministic harness producing 3,000 traces across four request types under low/medium/high/critical UMA-style memory pressure.

**Quality-centric ranking:**

| Policy | Score |
|---|---|
| pressure_guarded_fix | 0.9094 |
| quality_only | 0.8663 |
| similarity_retrieval | 0.8634 |
| memory_aware_v1 | 0.8314 |
| throughput_greedy | 0.7448 |

**Memory-pressure replay ranking:**

| Policy | Score |
|---|---|
| pressure_guarded_fix | 0.8755 |
| memory_aware_v1 | 0.7946 |
| similarity_retrieval | 0.6764 |
| quality_only | 0.6642 |
| throughput_greedy | 0.5276 |

Spearman rank correlation between the two rankings: **0.60**.

Newly exposed replay failure clusters: `oom_abort` (2,023 occurrences), `long_context_truncation` (140 occurrences).

Targeted pressure-guarded fix uplift vs. best non-fix baseline (memory_aware_v1): **8.095 points**.

Annotation cost proxy: weak labels plus 5% spot checks reduce estimated labeling time by **94.33%** vs. full manual labeling.

All three success criteria were met: new failure cluster exposed (true), ranking changed (true), targeted fix ≥5-point uplift (true).

### 3.2 Robustness Sweep (seeds 344–348)

A five-seed deterministic sweep was conducted to assess stability.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Mean Spearman rank correlation | 0.64 |
| Mean targeted-fix uplift | 8.544 points |
| Minimum targeted-fix uplift | 8.012 points |

The ranking-change criterion was mixed because the pressure_guarded_fix policy frequently ranked first in both slices, while baseline ordering shifted under replay. Rank correlations remained only moderate, and the divergence in baseline ordering is the substantively interesting signal rather than a simple top-rank change.

Spearman rank correlation: **0.60**. Newly exposed failure clusters: `oom_abort`=94, `long_context_truncation`=9. Targeted fix uplift: **5.192 points**.

**Rankings:** Quality-centric: `pressure_guarded_fix > quality_only > similarity_retrieval > memory_aware_v1 > throughput_greedy`. Memory-replay: `pressure_guarded_fix > memory_aware_v1 > similarity_retrieval > quality_only > throughput_greedy`.

Spearman rank correlation: **0.60**. Newly exposed failure cluster: `oom_abort`=38. Targeted fix uplift: **10.117 points**.

| Tier | n | Spearman ρ | Uplift (pts) | New Clusters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Synthetic (seed 344) | 3,000 | 0.60 | 8.095 | oom_abort, long_context_truncation |

The ranking-change criterion is mixed because the pressure_guarded_fix policy dominates both slices; the informative signal is the reordering of baselines (quality_only, memory_aware_v1, similarity_retrieval) between slices.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Model scale.** The Transformers model-server tier uses `sshleifer/tiny-gpt2`, a deliberately small model. GPU utilization was 1.6–2.0%, and CUDA allocator usage was negligible (<0.1 GiB). These results validate the replay-log mechanism as an evaluation scaffold but do not constitute production-scale memory-capacity evidence.

2. **Synthetic and emulated traces.** The synthetic tier uses weakly-labeled traces generated by a deterministic harness, not captured from a live inference server. The emulator tier records real OS telemetry but serves requests from an in-process mock, not a production framework (vLLM, SGLang, llama.cpp).

3. **Moderate rank correlation.** A Spearman ρ of 0.60 indicates that quality-centric and replay rankings share some structure but diverge meaningfully. This is the core finding, but it also means quality-centric evaluation captures real signal; the replay slice adds pressure-specific information rather than replacing quality evaluation entirely.

4. **Mixed ranking-change criterion.** Because the pressure_guarded_fix policy ranked first in both slices across most seeds, the binary "ranking changed" criterion was not consistently satisfied. The substantive finding is baseline reordering, not top-rank displacement.

5. **Single hardware configuration.** All runs were conducted on a single NVIDIA GB10 system. Memory-pressure dynamics may differ on discrete-GPU architectures, multi-GPU configurations, or different UMA implementations.

6. **No production traffic.** No real user request traces, production serving logs, or multi-tenant workload patterns were used. Failure cluster distributions under real traffic may differ substantially from the synthetic/emulated distributions reported here.

7. **Weak labels.** Quality labels are weakly generated, not human-annotated. The 94.33% annotation cost reduction estimate is a proxy based on 5% spot-check coverage, not a measured labeling cost from a human annotation pipeline.

## 5. Reproducibility Checklist

- **Deterministic execution:** All three harness scripts (`memory_pressure_replay_experiment.py`, `live_serving_trace_emulator.py`, `transformers_openai_trace.py`) accept a `--seed` argument and produce deterministic output for a given seed and `--n` value.
- **Dependency-free synthetic and emulator tiers:** The synthetic and live-emulator harnesses require only the Python standard library.
- **Transformers tier dependencies:** Requires Python 3.x, PyTorch 2.11.0+cu130, Transformers, and FastAPI. The model `sshleifer/tiny-gpt2` is publicly available on Hugging Face.
- **Hardware:** NVIDIA GB10 with CUDA support. GPU utilization and allocator metrics are hardware-specific.
- **Verification:** All scripts pass `python3 -m py_compile`. Calibrated pilot runs completed successfully for all three tiers. JSON artifact parse checks passed.
- **Data availability:** Raw trace JSONL files, replay-contract JSONL files, and result JSON files are recorded in the artifact manifest (Section 7).
- **Seed sweep:** Five seeds (344–348) are available under `artifacts/sweeps/` and `data/sweeps/`.

## 6. Conclusion

Memory Pressure Replay Logs provide an evaluation scaffold that changes policy rankings relative to quality-centric evaluation, exposes pressure-specific failure clusters (OOM aborts, long-context truncation), and identifies a targeted pressure-guarded intervention with 5.2–10.1 points of uplift over the best non-fix baseline across three evidence tiers. The moderate Spearman rank correlation (0.60–0.64) between quality-centric and replay rankings is consistent across synthetic, emulated, and real model-server traces, suggesting that pressure-aware evaluation captures information orthogonal to quality scoring.

These findings support the replay-log mechanism as an executable evaluation scaffold. However, evidence for production-scale model serving remains moderate: the model-server tier used a tiny model with negligible GPU memory footprint, and no production traffic or production serving framework was tested. The recommended next step is to validate the same replay contract against a larger cached model server (vLLM, SGLang, or llama.cpp) or real serving trace source before drawing conclusions about production deployment.
