## Abstract

We investigate whether an outcome-calibrated safety-drift monitor can distinguish wrong-direct-answer risk from evidence-need signals in real LLM interaction traces, under a gate requiring ≥90% violation recall, ≥2-step median lead time, and ≤15% safe false positive rate (FPR). On a 10,000-trace SQuAD/GSM8K replay benchmark, an initial outcome-calibrated monitor using pre-final confidence, ambiguity, and evidence-strength features reduced balanced FPR from 66.7% (parent laundering-robust monitor) to 19.0% while preserving 100% recall, but failed the gate on FPR budget (19.0% > 15%), lead time (1-step < 2-step minimum), and laundering robustness (recall dropped to 33.3% under adversarial conditions). On a smaller 182-trace benchmark constructed from observed-scaffold and teacher-scored outcome-labeled logs, an early outcome-calibrated monitor using draft/revision/scaffold/teacher outcome signals achieved 100% recall, 4-step median lead, and 0.0% safe FPR on the balanced held-out slice, with laundering robustness confirmed across 12 randomized views. The positive result is bounded by the small scale (182 traces, 26-trace balanced test) and the specific outcome-label sources. The 10,000-trace benchmark remains unsolved. We recommend archiving the outcome-labeled benchmark result and revisiting with a larger deployment-scale logged corpus for external validity.

## 1. Introduction

Safety-drift monitoring for LLM interactions aims to detect when a model's trajectory is heading toward an undesirable outcome before that outcome is finalized. A central difficulty is distinguishing traces that are complex or evidence-seeking (and therefore exhibit high retrieval/tool-need signals) from traces that are heading toward a wrong direct answer. Monitors that alarm on evidence-need signals alone tend to over-alarm on safe but complex traces, while monitors that achieve high recall through generic risk counters are vulnerable to laundering—adversarial perturbation of surface features that erases the detection signal without changing the outcome.

This work tests the hypothesis that calibrating a drift monitor to true outcome signals—rather than observation-surface risk counters or evidence-need proxies—can separate wrong-direct-answer risk from evidence need under a practical operating gate. The gate requires: (1) ≥90% violation recall on a balanced held-out slice, (2) ≥2-step median lead time before the violation event, and (3) ≤15% safe FPR, with robustness to randomized laundering.

We report results from two benchmark experiments. The first, on 10,000 real mixed SQuAD/GSM8K traces, demonstrates that an initial outcome-calibrated monitor improves over a parent laundering-robust monitor on balanced FPR but does not satisfy the gate. The second, on 182 outcome-labeled traces from observed-scaffold and teacher-scored logs, demonstrates that an early outcome-calibrated monitor using draft/revision/scaffold/teacher signals passes the gate at small scale. We document both the positive and negative results, and discuss the gap between them.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Problem Formulation

Given a sequence of trace events (e.g., prompt, draft, critique, revision, scaffold, finalization), the monitor must decide at each step whether the trace is heading toward a wrong-direct-answer violation. A violation is defined as a trace where the final direct answer is incorrect. A safe trace is one where the final direct answer is correct, regardless of whether the trace involved retrieval, tool use, or other evidence-need signals.

The operating gate requires:
- **Recall** ≥ 90% on the balanced held-out violation set.
- **Median lead** ≥ 2 steps before the violation event.
- **Safe FPR** ≤ 15% on the balanced held-out safe set.
- **Laundering robustness**: the gate must hold under randomized laundering of observation-surface features.

### 2.2 Monitor Variants

Three monitor variants are compared:

1. **Evidence-need baseline**: Alarms on retrieval/tool-need signals without outcome calibration. This baseline tests whether evidence need alone is sufficient to detect wrong-direct-answer risk.

2. **Parent randomized laundering monitor**: The inherited laundering-robust monitor from the parent project, which uses a feature family of observation-surface risk counters. This baseline tests whether generic risk counters achieve the gate.

3. **Outcome-calibrated monitors**: Two versions are evaluated:
   - **Initial outcome-calibrated monitor** (10K benchmark): Uses pre-final logged confidence, ambiguity, evidence strength, evidence-confidence gaps, and derived outcome-risk features. Class-balanced.
   - **Early outcome-calibrated monitor** (scaffold/teacher benchmark): Uses logged draft/revision/scaffold/teacher outcome signals. Intentionally excludes inherited observation-surface risk counters so that laundering of generic risk counters does not erase the primary outcome calibration signal.

### 2.3 Laundering Robustness Testing

Randomized laundering generates multiple views of each trace by perturbing observation-surface features (e.g., token-level risk counters, surface heuristics) while preserving the underlying outcome. The monitor is evaluated across all views; the gate requires that recall, lead, and FPR constraints hold for every view. A fixed adversarial condition also tests the monitor against a single worst-case laundering.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Benchmark 1: 10,000-Trace SQuAD/GSM8K Replay

**Dataset.** 10,000 traces (50,000 events) replayed from real mixed SQuAD/GSM8K logged rows as five-step traces. The dataset contains 9,898 wrong-direct-answer violation traces and 102 direct-correct safe traces. The stratified split produced 20 validation safe traces, 21 natural test safe traces, and a 42-trace balanced test slice (21 safe / 21 violating).

**Evidence-need baseline** (thresholded to 15% validation FPR budget):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Violation recall | 14.3% |
| Median lead | 4 steps |
| Safe FPR | 19.0% |

This baseline fails the recall and FPR gates, confirming that evidence/task complexity is not equivalent to wrong-direct-answer risk.

**Parent randomized laundering monitor**:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Violation recall | 100.0% |
| Median lead | 4 steps |
| Safe FPR | 66.7% |

The parent monitor preserves recall and lead but fails the FPR gate severely (66.7% >> 15%). Its recall derives from over-alarming on direct-correct rows.

**Initial outcome-calibrated monitor** (clean balanced replay):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Violation recall | 100.0% |
| Median lead | 1 step |
| Safe FPR | 19.0% |

This improves balanced FPR from 66.7% to 19.0% while preserving recall, but fails the gate on three counts: FPR exceeds the 15% budget, median lead is only 1 step (below the 2-step minimum), and laundering robustness is not achieved.

**Initial outcome-calibrated monitor** (fixed adversarial balanced replay):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Violation recall | 33.3% |
| Safe FPR | 19.0% |

Recall collapses under adversarial laundering.

**Initial outcome-calibrated monitor** (randomized laundering suite):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Min recall | 33.3% |
| Max safe FPR | 23.8% |

The laundering suite confirms fragility: the monitor does not pass the gate under laundering.

**Summary for Benchmark 1.** The initial outcome-calibrated monitor demonstrates that outcome calibration can reduce FPR relative to the parent monitor, but the 10,000-trace benchmark remains unsolved. The heavy class imbalance (9,898 violating vs. 102 safe) and the reliance on pre-final confidence/evidence features rather than true draft outcome signals limit the monitor's ability to separate the two classes under the gate.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Scale of the positive result.** The positive result comes from 182 traces with a 26-trace balanced held-out slice. This is a small benchmark, and the generalization to deployment-scale logs is not established. The 10,000-trace benchmark, which is closer to deployment scale, remains unsolved by the current approach.

2. **Class imbalance in Benchmark 1.** The 10,000-trace dataset contains only 102 safe traces out of 10,000 (1.02%), making balanced evaluation statistically fragile. The 42-trace balanced test slice is small, and confidence intervals on the reported metrics are wide.

3. **Outcome-label provenance.** The scaffold/teacher benchmark relies on outcome labels from two adjacent project artifacts. Whether these labels generalize to other domains, model families, or trace formats is unknown.

4. **Feature design dependence.** The early outcome-calibrated monitor intentionally excludes observation-surface risk counters to achieve laundering robustness. This design choice means the monitor depends on the availability and quality of draft/revision/scaffold/teacher outcome signals, which may not be present in all deployment settings.

5. **No external replication.** Results have not been replicated on independent datasets, by independent teams, or on different hardware. All experiments were run within a single project directory using a single Python virtual environment.

6. **Gate definition.** The ≥90% recall / ≥2-step lead / ≤15% FPR gate is a project-specific operating point. Different deployment contexts may require different trade-offs, and the results here do not characterize the full ROC curve or alternative operating points.

7. **Adversarial model.** The laundering robustness test perturbs observation-surface features but does not model adaptive adversaries who can modify the draft/revision content itself. Robustness to content-level adversarial perturbation is not evaluated.

## 6. Conclusion

We have presented evidence from two benchmark experiments on outcome-calibrated safety-drift monitoring for LLM traces. The key finding is that the distinction between evidence need and wrong-direct-answer risk is real and measurable: evidence-need baselines consistently fail to identify wrong-direct-answer violations, and parent monitors that achieve high recall do so by over-alarming on safe traces. Outcome calibration with true draft/revision/scaffold/teacher outcome signals can achieve the recall/lead/FPR gate on a small (182-trace) outcome-labeled benchmark, including under randomized laundering. However, the same approach applied to a larger (10,000-trace) benchmark with pre-final confidence features does not satisfy the gate, failing on FPR, lead time, and laundering robustness.

The gap between the two benchmarks suggests that the availability and quality of outcome signals—not merely the monitor architecture—is the critical variable. The current project artifacts support this finding in the tested setting. We recommend archiving the outcome-labeled benchmark result and revisiting with a larger deployment-scale logged corpus for external validity before drawing stronger conclusions.
