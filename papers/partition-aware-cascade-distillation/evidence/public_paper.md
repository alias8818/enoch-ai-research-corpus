## Abstract

We investigate whether a cascade system that distills a teacher model into partition-specific cheap students, with per-partition fallback thresholds, can outperform a single globally distilled student with a global fallback gate at matched teacher-call budgets. Using sklearn proxy models (RandomForestClassifier teacher, logistic-regression students) on four benchmark tasks, we find that partition-aware cascade distillation (PACD) yields substantial accuracy gains when partitions expose genuine conditional heterogeneity (+13.7 to +20.3 percentage points on a synthetic partition-shift task), moderate gains on a digit-classification task with digit-band partitions (+1.3 to +2.8 points), and negligible or zero gains on tasks where partitions do not meaningfully differentiate local decision rules. Per-partition routing incurs a throughput reduction of 0.33–0.51× relative to a single global student. Individual random seeds on low-heterogeneity tasks occasionally show small negative deltas (minimum −0.0088), confirming that PACD is not universally beneficial. These results are limited to sklearn-scale proxy experiments and do not yet validate the approach in LLM cascade or GPU inference settings.

## Introduction

Model cascades reduce inference cost by routing easy inputs to cheap student models and escalating hard inputs to expensive teacher models. A standard cascade trains a single global student and applies a single global confidence threshold to decide when to escalate. However, when the input distribution is heterogeneous—containing subpopulations with different local decision boundaries—a single global student may be a poor fit for all partitions simultaneously, and a single threshold may be suboptimal for each partition's difficulty profile.

Partition-Aware Cascade Distillation (PACD) addresses this by training one cheap student per partition and calibrating one escalation threshold per partition on a held-out validation set. The core research question is: at a matched teacher-call budget, does this partition-aware design outperform the global baseline?

This paper reports results from controlled sklearn-scale experiments across four tasks with varying degrees of partition heterogeneity. We report both positive and null results, as the viability of PACD depends critically on whether partitions carry meaningful conditional structure. The experiments use sklearn RandomForestClassifier teachers and logistic-regression students as proxies; they do not involve LLM cascades, GPU inference, or production-scale workloads.

## Method

### Models

- **Teacher:** sklearn `RandomForestClassifier` trained on true labels.
- **Global student:** A single sklearn logistic regression trained on teacher hard labels across all partitions.
- **Partition students:** One sklearn logistic regression per observed partition, each trained on teacher hard labels restricted to that partition.
- **Cascade logic:** The student predicts locally; if prediction confidence falls below a threshold, the example escalates to the teacher. The global cascade uses one threshold for all inputs; PACD uses one threshold per partition, calibrated on a held-out validation set.

### Datasets and Partitions

Four tasks were used, ordered by expected degree of partition heterogeneity:

1. **partition_shift:** A deliberately heterogeneous synthetic task designed so that different partitions have different conditional distributions. This represents the best-case scenario for PACD.
2. **digits:** sklearn digits dataset, binarized into high/low digit classes, with digit-band partitions.
3. **breast:** sklearn breast-cancer dataset, with radius-quantile partitions.
4. **generic:** A generic synthetic quantile-partition sanity check.

### Experimental Protocol

- Five full-run seeds per configuration.
- Three intended teacher-call budgets: 10%, 20%, 40%.
- Held-out train/calibration/test splits.
- Metrics: accuracy, actual teacher-call rate, throughput (samples/s).
- Platform: Linux 6.17.0 on NVIDIA GB10 (aarch64), Python 3.12.3, glibc 2.39.
- Full run elapsed time: 7.89 s.
- GPU telemetry: NVIDIA GB10 reported 0% utilization throughout, consistent with CPU-only sklearn execution.
- Memory: ~121 GB available; negligible consumption observed (start 121,521,092 kB, end 121,481,308 kB). Swap disabled (0 kB total).

### Throughput

Per-partition routing over multiple logistic-regression models incurs measurable overhead:

| Task            | Global Student (samples/s) | Partition Students (samples/s) | Ratio |
|-----------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|-------|
| partition_shift | 20,516,063                | 6,748,256                     | 0.33× |
| digits          | 3,269,451                 | 1,675,196                     | 0.51× |
| breast          | 1,477,241                 | 655,591                       | 0.44× |
| generic         | 10,585,581                | 4,232,028                     | 0.40× |

Partition-aware inference is 2–3× slower per sample than a single global student. However, absolute throughput remains in the hundreds of thousands to millions of samples per second for these sklearn models, so the overhead is unlikely to be the binding constraint in this proxy setting. The throughput penalty may become more significant in LLM inference where per-request latency dominates.

### Teacher-Call Efficiency

On the breast-cancer task, PACD achieved comparable or slightly better accuracy while using fewer teacher calls (e.g., 0.077 vs. 0.114 at 10% budget; 0.344 vs. 0.437 at 40% budget), yielding teacher-call deltas of −0.0368 and −0.0930 respectively. On digits and partition_shift, actual call rates were closely matched between methods (teacher-call deltas within ±0.007). On the generic task, PACD used slightly fewer calls at 10% and 20% budgets (deltas −0.0036, −0.0054) but slightly more at 40% (+0.0042). This suggests that per-partition thresholds can sometimes allocate the teacher budget more efficiently, though this effect is task-dependent and not consistent.

## Limitations

1. **Proxy models, not LLMs.** All experiments use sklearn RandomForest and logistic-regression models. The distillation uses teacher hard labels only; soft-logit distillation, which is standard for LLM/token-level tasks, has not been tested. The results do not directly predict PACD behavior in LLM cascade settings with real latency and memory constraints.

2. **Predefined partitions.** Partitions are defined by supplied metadata or quantile splits, not learned by a router. In production, partition boundaries would need to be discovered or learned, introducing additional complexity and potential error.

3. **Small-scale benchmarks.** The four tasks are small sklearn datasets. Generalization to high-dimensional, large-scale, or distributionally shifted production workloads is untested.

4. **No GPU utilization.** Despite running on an NVIDIA GB10 platform, all execution was CPU-only (0% GPU throughout). The experiments do not profile GPU memory, kernel launch overhead, or batched inference behavior that would be relevant for LLM deployment.

5. **Negative seed-level results.** On the breast-cancer and generic tasks, individual seeds showed small negative deltas (minimum −0.0088 on breast at 10% budget, −0.0020 on generic at 10% and 20% budgets). PACD is not guaranteed to help when partitions lack meaningful conditional heterogeneity.

6. **Throughput cost.** Partition-aware inference is 2–3× slower per sample than a single global student in this setting. The tradeoff between accuracy gains and throughput cost must be evaluated per application.

7. **No calibration stability analysis.** Per-partition thresholds were calibrated once on a held-out validation set. Sensitivity to validation-set size, distribution shift over time, and threshold drift has not been characterized.

8. **Limited seed count.** Five seeds per configuration provide a preliminary estimate of variance but may be insufficient for robust statistical claims, particularly on tasks where mean deltas are small (breast, generic).

## Conclusion

Partition-Aware Cascade Distillation shows clear accuracy gains when input partitions carry genuine conditional heterogeneity: +13.7 to +20.3 percentage points on a synthetic partition-shift task and +1.3 to +2.8 points on a digit-classification task, at matched teacher-call budgets, across all five random seeds. On tasks where partitions do not meaningfully differentiate local decision rules (breast-cancer, generic synthetic), gains are negligible or zero, and individual seeds can show small negative deltas (as large as −0.0088). The per-partition routing overhead reduces throughput by a factor of 2–3× relative to a single global student, though absolute throughput remains high in this sklearn-scale setting.

These results establish PACD as a viable direction for partitioned workloads but do not yet validate the approach in its intended LLM cascade setting. The central finding is that the benefit of partition-aware distillation is contingent on the degree of conditional heterogeneity across partitions—when partitions expose different local decision rules, PACD provides substantial gains; when they do not, the overhead of maintaining partition-specific models and thresholds yields no return. Key next steps include: (1) testing with real language-model cascades where teacher latency and memory are binding constraints; (2) comparing learned partition routers against fixed metadata partitions; and (3) reporting quality-cost Pareto curves with full GPU inference telemetry on production-scale workloads.
