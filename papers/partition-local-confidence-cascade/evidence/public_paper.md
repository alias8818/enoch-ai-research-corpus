## Abstract

Two-stage inference cascades reduce computation by routing high-confidence predictions through a cheap model and falling back to a full model otherwise. Standard cascades apply a single global confidence threshold on the cheap model. This work investigates whether replacing that global threshold with partition-local thresholds—calibrated separately within subgroups defined by the cheap model's predicted class and confidence bin—can increase the fraction of examples accepted by the cheap stage while preserving full-model accuracy. We evaluate on five datasets (one synthetic, four real) with five random seeds each, using logistic regression as the cheap model and random forest as the full model under a 1-percentage-point accuracy tolerance. On a synthetic dataset designed to exhibit partition-wise reliability heterogeneity, partition-local thresholds increased cheap-stage acceptance by 7.1 percentage points with a slight accuracy improvement (+0.23 pp). However, across all 25 runs, the mean acceptance delta was −0.171, and partition-local thresholds won on acceptance in only 36% of runs. On real datasets with small or sparse partitions, local thresholds became overly conservative, routing most examples to the full model. A budgeted calibration variant achieved a higher acceptance win rate (48%) but exhibited calibration overfitting. We conclude that partition-local confidence cascades are conditionally viable: they offer throughput gains when partition-wise reliability heterogeneity is present and calibration data per partition is sufficient, but they do not constitute a universal improvement over global thresholds.

## Introduction

Inference cascades reduce computational cost by deploying a cheap model for "easy" predictions and invoking an expensive model only when the cheap model's confidence is insufficient. The standard design applies a single global confidence threshold: if the cheap model's maximum class probability exceeds the threshold, its prediction is accepted; otherwise, the example is routed to the full model.

A global threshold implicitly assumes that the cheap model's reliability is uniform across its output space. In practice, this assumption often fails. A logistic regression model may be highly reliable when it predicts class A with 90% confidence but unreliable when it predicts class B at the same confidence level, because different classes rely on different feature subsets with varying signal strength. If the cheap model is systematically more reliable in some partitions of its output space than others, a global threshold must be set conservatively enough for the worst-case partition, unnecessarily rejecting cheap predictions from more reliable partitions.

This work tests a straightforward extension: replace the single global threshold with partition-local thresholds, each calibrated to the cheap model's error rate within a subgroup defined by the cheap model's predicted class and confidence bin. The hypothesis is that when cheap-model reliability varies across partitions, local thresholds can accept more examples from reliable partitions without violating an overall accuracy constraint.

The related WILLUMP system constructs ML inference cascades from computationally independent feature groups and accepts approximate predictions above a confidence threshold. Our experiment tests a narrower extension within that framework: whether partition-local thresholds outperform a single global threshold under a controlled accuracy budget.

Our contributions are:

1. An empirical comparison of global vs. partition-local confidence thresholds across five datasets and 25 experimental runs, with negative and mixed results reported in full.
2. Evidence that partition-local thresholds improve throughput in the intended heterogeneous setting but degrade throughput on real datasets with sparse partitions.
3. Evidence that direct calibration-budget optimization of per-partition thresholds increases acceptance rates but risks overfitting, motivating statistical guards.

## Method

### Cascade Architecture

The cascade consists of two stages:

- **Full model:** A `RandomForestClassifier` trained on all features.
- **Cheap model:** A multinomial or binary logistic regression (standardized) trained on a subset of features selected by feature-count proxy cost (fewer features = cheaper inference).

At inference time, each example is first evaluated by the cheap model. If the cheap model's confidence (maximum class probability) exceeds the applicable threshold, the cheap prediction is accepted. Otherwise, the example is routed to the full model.

### Threshold Selection

**Global baseline.** A single confidence threshold is selected on calibration data to maximize the fraction of examples accepted by the cheap model, subject to the constraint that the cascade's calibration accuracy is at least (full model calibration accuracy − tolerance), where tolerance = 0.01 (1 percentage point).

**Partition-local (conservative).** Partitions are defined by the cheap model's predicted class crossed with confidence bin (for real datasets) or by a synthetic heterogeneity marker crossed with predicted class (for the synthetic dataset). For each partition, a local threshold is selected to maximize cheap acceptance within that partition, subject to a per-partition cheap-error cap. Partitions with fewer than 20 calibration examples are routed entirely to the full model (no local threshold is computed). This is the primary variant reported.

**Partition-local (budgeted).** Per-partition thresholds are selected by a calibration loss-budget knapsack: the total allowable accuracy loss is distributed across partitions to maximize overall cheap acceptance. This variant is reported as secondary and risk evidence because it exhibited greater calibration overfitting.

### Datasets and Partitions

| Dataset | Type | Classes | Partition definition |
|---|---|---|---|
| `synthetic_local_hetero` | Synthetic with injected partition-wise reliability | 3 | Marker × predicted class |
| `classification_50f` | Synthetic, 50 features | — | Predicted class × confidence bin |
| `breast_cancer` | Real, scikit-learn | 2 | Predicted class × confidence bin |
| `digits` | Real, scikit-learn | 10 | Predicted class × confidence bin |
| `wine` | Real, scikit-learn | 3 | Predicted class × confidence bin |

Each dataset was evaluated over 5 random seeds. The calibration tolerance was fixed at 0.01 accuracy below the full model.

### Evaluation Metrics

- **Acceptance rate:** Fraction of test examples accepted by the cheap model (higher = more throughput savings).
- **Accuracy:** Cascade accuracy on the test set.
- **Acceptance delta:** Partition-local acceptance rate minus global acceptance rate (positive = partition-local accepts more).
- **Accuracy delta:** Partition-local accuracy minus global accuracy.

### Environment

Experiments ran on Linux (`gx10-efe8`, aarch64) with Python 3.12.3, using numpy, scipy, pandas, and scikit-learn. Host memory was 121 GiB with 116 GiB available and no swap. These were CPU-only tabular experiments; no GPU was involved. The experiments are toy-scale simulations using tabular proxy costs rather than a production inference pipeline.

## Results

### Primary Results: Conservative Partition-Local Thresholds

Mean results over 5 seeds per dataset (from `full_results_min20.json`):

| Dataset | Acceptance Δ (local − global) | Accuracy Δ (local − global) | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| `synthetic_local_hetero` | +0.0705 | +0.0023 | Partition-local accepted ~7.1 pp more examples with a slight accuracy improvement |
| `classification_50f` | +0.0054 | −0.0021 | Marginal acceptance gain, small accuracy loss |
| `breast_cancer` | −0.0867 | +0.0112 | Local thresholds more conservative; improved accuracy at cost of throughput |
| `digits` | −0.0591 | +0.0018 | Local thresholds more conservative; slight accuracy gain |
| `wine` | −0.7867 | +0.0356 | Small partitions routed to full model; near-total loss of cheap acceptance |

Aggregated across all 25 runs:

- **Overall acceptance delta:** −0.1713
- **Overall accuracy delta:** +0.0098
- **Acceptance win rate:** 36% of runs (partition-local accepted more than global)

The synthetic local heterogeneity dataset—designed to exhibit partition-wise reliability variation—showed the intended effect: local thresholds exploited the heterogeneity to accept substantially more examples while maintaining accuracy. On real datasets, the results were predominantly negative for throughput. The `wine` dataset, with only 3 classes and small calibration partitions, illustrates the failure mode: most partitions fell below the minimum-20-examples threshold and were hard-routed to the full model, effectively disabling the cheap stage.

### Secondary Results: Budgeted Partition-Local Thresholds

The budgeted variant (`full_results_budgeted.json`) distributed the accuracy budget across partitions via a knapsack optimization. This increased the acceptance win rate to 48% of runs and produced larger acceptance gains on `synthetic_local_hetero` and `classification_50f`. However, on those acceptance-winning cases, the budgeted variant generalized worse in accuracy compared to the conservative variant, indicating calibration overfitting. The budgeted selector directly optimizes against calibration data without statistical guards, making it vulnerable to partition-specific overfitting when calibration samples are sparse.

### Summary of Mixed Evidence

The results do not support a universal claim that partition-local thresholds improve cascade throughput. The mechanism works as intended when (a) partition-wise reliability heterogeneity exists and (b) sufficient calibration data is available per partition. When these conditions are unmet—which was the common case in our real-dataset experiments—local thresholds either match or underperform the global baseline on acceptance, typically trading throughput for safety.

## Limitations

1. **Proxy cost model.** Experiments use feature count as a proxy for inference cost. Actual inference cascades in production LLM or retrieval-augmented systems incur costs from KV-cache lookups, operator dispatch, and memory bandwidth that are not captured by feature count. Scientific closure for a production inference claim requires real target workload traces and measured operator latencies.

2. **Tabular models only.** The cheap model is logistic regression and the full model is a random forest, both operating on tabular features. Whether partition-local thresholds transfer to neural network cascades, embedding-based cheap models, or LLM token-level routing is untested.

3. **Simple partition design.** Partitions are defined by predicted class × confidence bin. This scheme produces many sparse partitions on small datasets. More sophisticated partitioning (e.g., clustering in representation space, hierarchical partitions) might yield different results.

4. **Single tolerance value.** All experiments use a 1-percentage-point accuracy tolerance. The trade-off surface across tolerances is unexplored; a Pareto curve over tolerance values would better characterize the method.

5. **Minimum-partition-size heuristic.** The conservative variant routes partitions with fewer than 20 calibration examples to the full model. This threshold was not systematically tuned and may be suboptimal. The `wine` dataset result (−0.79 acceptance delta) is largely an artifact of this heuristic.

6. **No statistical significance testing.** Results are reported as means over 5 seeds without confidence intervals or hypothesis tests. The sample size is small, and the reported deltas should be interpreted as directional evidence rather than statistically confirmed effects.

7. **Calibration overfitting in budgeted variant.** The budgeted partition-local selector optimizes directly against calibration data. Without cross-validation, conformal bounds, or hierarchical shrinkage, it is expected to overfit, and the empirical results confirm this.

8. **Toy-scale simulation.** These experiments are toy simulations on small tabular datasets with proxy costs. They are not CUDA copy calibrations, llama.cpp hook prototypes, or production validation results. Extrapolation to production inference workloads is not justified by the current evidence.

## Conclusion

Partition-local confidence thresholds for two-stage inference cascades are conditionally viable but not universally beneficial. On a synthetic dataset with injected partition-wise reliability heterogeneity, local thresholds increased cheap-stage acceptance by 7.1 percentage points while slightly improving accuracy, confirming the mechanism works in its intended setting. Across five datasets and 25 runs, however, the mean acceptance delta was −0.171, and partition-local thresholds won on acceptance in only 36% of runs. On real datasets with small or sparse partitions, local thresholds became overly conservative, routing most examples to the full model and negating the throughput benefit.

The budgeted calibration variant increased the acceptance win rate to 48% but exhibited calibration overfitting, underscoring the need for statistical guards when optimizing per-partition thresholds.

These results support a narrow, conditional claim: partition-local confidence thresholds should be considered when (a) there is measurable partition-wise reliability heterogeneity in the cheap model and (b) sufficient calibration data exists per partition. In the absence of these conditions, a global threshold remains the safer default. Future work should address adaptive partition merging for sparse subgroups, conformal or upper-confidence-bound calibration for per-partition error rates, and evaluation on real inference workloads with measured operator costs and Pareto curves over accuracy tolerances.
