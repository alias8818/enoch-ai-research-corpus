## Abstract

We investigate whether pruning weak experts from a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model and then upcycling the surviving experts back to the original expert count can yield better recovery-after-compression than prune-only baselines. Using a synthetic regression task with a softmax-routed 12-expert MoE, we compare four recovery paths across 48 random seeds: prune-only recovery (6 experts), prune-then-upcycle (saliency-selected 6 duplicated and jittered back to 12), random-survivor upcycle, and vanilla unpruned expert diversification. Prune-then-upcycle clearly outperforms prune-only recovery (37/48 wins, sign-test *p* = 0.0002) and random-survivor upcycle (41/48 wins, *p* = 6.2 × 10⁻⁷). However, it only weakly and inconsistently outperforms vanilla unpruned diversification (28/48 wins, *p* = 0.31), with the mean advantage sensitive to a single outlier seed. These results establish that the prune-then-upcycle mechanism is viable in a controlled synthetic setting against two of three baselines, but do not constitute scientific closure. Validation on real MoE checkpoints with continued pretraining budgets is required.

## Introduction

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures achieve model capacity through conditional sparsity, but their expert count creates deployment and serving costs. Compression via expert pruning has emerged as a practical strategy: methods such as REAP (Router-weighted Expert Activation Pruning) use router gate values combined with expert activation norms as saliency signals to identify and remove low-importance experts in a single pass, reporting that pruning can outperform expert merging for generative MoE compression.

Separately, upcycling methods expand MoE capacity by initializing new experts from existing ones, with partial reinitialization strategies like Drop-Upcycling studying how best to diversify duplicated experts during continued training. A natural question arises: can these two operations compose productively? If pruning pressure identifies stronger expert seeds than the original full set, then upcycling from those selected survivors might recover quality more efficiently than either keeping the pruned model small or diversifying from the unpruned checkpoint.

We define the **prune-then-upcycle (PTU)** loop as: (1) compute saliency scores for all experts, (2) prune low-saliency experts, (3) duplicate surviving experts with small perturbation ("jitter") back to the original expert count, and (4) recover via continued training. The core hypothesis is that saliency-guided survivor selection provides better initialization seeds for upcycling than either random selection or the full unpruned expert set.

This paper reports results from a synthetic feasibility study designed as an evidence gate before committing to real MoE checkpoint experiments. We do not claim these results generalize to production-scale MoE models; rather, we document what the synthetic proxy suggests and what it does not, in order to inform the design of subsequent validation experiments.

## Method

### Task and Model

We construct a synthetic regression task. A latent function generates data from task-specific linear experts with imbalanced cluster frequencies, ensuring that some experts are more important than others—a prerequisite for pruning to be meaningful. The model is a softmax-routed MoE with 12 experts, each a linear module, trained via gradient descent on mean squared error (MSE).

### Saliency Computation

Following the REAP approach, we compute a saliency score for each expert as:

$$s_i = \text{mean}\bigl(\text{router\_probability}_i \times |\text{expert\_activation}_i|\bigr)$$

Experts are ranked by saliency, and the bottom 6 are pruned.

### Recovery Paths

After pruning to 6 experts, we compare four recovery strategies under an identical recovery step budget:

1. **Prune-only recovery.** Continue training the 6-expert pruned model. No upcycling.
2. **Prune-then-upcycle (PTU).** Duplicate each of the 6 saliency-selected survivors, apply small random perturbation ("jitter") to the copies, yielding 12 experts. Continue training.
3. **Random-survivor upcycle.** Randomly select 6 of the original 12 experts as survivors, duplicate and jitter them to 12. Continue training.
4. **Vanilla unpruned diversification.** Keep all 12 original experts, apply jitter/diversification perturbation, continue training.

All four paths receive the same number of recovery training steps (450) after the same pretraining schedule (1800 steps on 12,000 training samples), evaluated on 4,000 held-out test samples.

### Experimental Protocol

We run 48 independent seeds. For each seed, all four recovery paths share the same pretrained checkpoint, ensuring paired comparisons. We report MSE on the held-out test set after recovery.

Statistical analysis uses paired sign tests and bootstrap confidence intervals for mean differences (negative differences favor PTU). We also report a sensitivity analysis removing the largest outlier seed.

### Implementation

The experiment is implemented in pure NumPy (version 2.4.4) without PyTorch or GPU acceleration. This is a **toy simulation**: the experts are linear modules, the router is a softmax gate, and the data is synthetically generated. The implementation does not operate on real language model checkpoints or real data. It is not a llama.cpp hook prototype, CUDA copy calibration, or production validation; it is a synthetic proxy designed to test whether the PTU mechanism is coherent before investing in real-MoE experiments.

## Results

### Aggregate MSE

| Recovery Path | Mean MSE | Median MSE |
|---|---|---|
| Checkpoint (before recovery) | 0.3546 | 0.1482 |
| Prune-only (6 experts) | 0.3650 | 0.1596 |
| Prune-then-upcycle (PTU) | 0.1682 | 0.1322 |
| Random-survivor upcycle | 0.3436 | 0.2323 |
| Vanilla unpruned diversification | 0.3063 | 0.1178 |

### Paired Comparisons

**PTU vs. prune-only.** PTU wins in 37 of 48 seeds. Mean difference: −0.1968 (favoring PTU). Sign-test *p* = 0.0002. Bootstrap 95% CI for mean difference: [−0.4496, −0.1861, −0.0494]. This is a clear and statistically significant advantage.

**PTU vs. random-survivor upcycle.** PTU wins in 41 of 48 seeds. Mean difference: −0.1754. Sign-test *p* = 6.2 × 10⁻⁷. Bootstrap 95% CI: [−0.2624, −0.1732, −0.1020]. Saliency-selected survivors are substantially better upcycling seeds than randomly selected survivors.

**PTU vs. vanilla unpruned diversification.** PTU wins in 28 of 48 seeds. Mean difference: −0.1380. Sign-test *p* = 0.31 (not significant). Bootstrap 95% CI: [−0.3951, −0.1271, +0.0101]. The interval crosses zero. The favorable mean is heavily influenced by a single catastrophic vanilla failure in seed 38; removing that seed changes the mean difference to −0.0246 with 27/47 wins. The median difference is −0.0062, indicating a marginal and inconsistent PTU advantage.

### Resource Usage

The 48-seed main run completed in 10.22 seconds wall time (19.58 seconds user CPU, 191% CPU utilization), with maximum RSS of 39,788 KB and zero swap operations. The experiment was well within available memory and compute.

## Limitations

1. **Synthetic proxy only.** The entire experiment operates on a toy linear-regression MoE with synthetic data. The experts are linear modules, not transformer feed-forward networks. The router is a simple softmax gate. No real language model checkpoints, tokenized corpora, or downstream task evaluations are involved. Generalization to production MoE architectures is unknown.

2. **Inconclusive against the strongest baseline.** PTU does not decisively beat vanilla unpruned diversification. The sign test is non-significant (*p* = 0.31), the bootstrap CI includes zero, and the advantage is sensitive to a single outlier seed. This is the comparison that matters most for the practical question of whether pruning adds value before upcycling.

3. **Jitter magnitude and recovery budget are unexplored hyperparameters.** The perturbation scale applied to duplicated experts and the ratio of recovery steps to pretraining steps were set to fixed values. Sensitivity to these choices is not characterized.

4. **Single pruning ratio.** Only a 50% pruning ratio (12 → 6) is tested. Other ratios may yield different conclusions.

5. **No continued pretraining on real data.** Recovery in this experiment is on the same synthetic distribution as pretraining. Real MoE recovery involves distribution shifts, tokenization artifacts, and data ordering effects that are absent here.

6. **No evaluation of routing dynamics.** We do not analyze whether the upcycled experts develop differentiated routing behavior or collapse into redundant copies, which is a known failure mode in MoE upcycling.

7. **Model-authored draft.** The claim ledger for this paper contains no audit-approved claims. Human claim audit is required before any results are cited as established findings.

## Conclusion

In a synthetic 12-expert linear MoE regression task across 48 seeds, the prune-then-upcycle recovery loop clearly outperforms both prune-only recovery and random-survivor upcycling, establishing that saliency-selected survivors are better seeds for upcycling than either a reduced expert set or randomly chosen survivors. However, PTU does not decisively outperform vanilla unpruned expert diversification—the comparison most relevant to whether pruning adds value before upcycling—with a non-significant sign test (*p* = 0.31) and a bootstrap confidence interval that includes zero.

These results are sufficient to pass the synthetic evidence gate: the mechanism is viable and does not hit the kill condition (failure to recover pruned quality within the recovery budget). But they do not constitute scientific closure. The prune-then-upcycle loop should advance to validation on a real small MoE checkpoint with a fixed short continued-pretraining budget, comparing REAP-prune-only recovery, PTU from REAP survivors, random-survivor upcycle, and vanilla unpruned diversification on held-out perplexity and downstream task evaluations. Only such an experiment can determine whether the synthetic signal survives contact with real transformer experts, real data distributions, and real routing dynamics.
