## Abstract

We investigate whether a quantization-aware saliency (QAS) score can identify parameters whose protection from low-bit quantization reduces post-quantization error more effectively than standard quantization-agnostic saliency methods. The proposed QAS score approximates per-coefficient post-quantization loss contribution as $H_{ii}(w_i - q_i)^2$, where $H_{ii} = \mathbb{E}[x_i^2]$ is the diagonal Hessian element and $(w_i - q_i)$ is the quantization error. In a controlled NumPy synthetic linear-regression testbed with correlated features and grouped symmetric quantization, we compare two QAS variants—a naive diagonal score (`qas_diag`) and a greedy structural score (`qas_greedy_exact`)—against magnitude, Hessian/pruning saliency, quantization-error-only, random, and oracle baselines across 30 seeds, three bit widths (2, 3, 4), three protection fractions (0.05, 0.10, 0.20), and three weight distributions. The naive diagonal QAS is decisively outperformed by magnitude ranking (64 wins vs. 746 losses in pairwise comparisons; mean excess-ratio delta $+0.2183$). The greedy structural QAS, which models the quantizer intervention and requantizes affected groups after each protection step, shows a conditional advantage in the heavy-tailed weight regime (best in 9/9 aggregate cells; mean excess-ratio delta $-0.00905$ relative to magnitude) but is slightly worse than magnitude overall (385 wins vs. 413 losses; mean delta $+0.0104$). These results indicate that quantization-aware saliency is viable only when the saliency computation explicitly models the quantization intervention; naive one-shot diagonal approximations are not viable and are actively harmful relative to magnitude ranking.

## Introduction

Low-bit quantization of neural network weights introduces error that is unevenly distributed across parameters. Mixed-precision schemes address this by protecting a subset of parameters at higher precision, typically selected by saliency scores such as weight magnitude or Hessian-based pruning importance. However, these saliency scores were developed for the pruning problem and do not account for the structure of quantization error: quantization error depends on the quantizer's scale, group assignment, and rounding behavior, and protecting one parameter can change the quantization scale for other parameters in the same group.

We hypothesize that a saliency score incorporating the expected quantization error—rather than relying solely on weight magnitude or second-order gradient information—should more effectively identify parameters whose protection yields the greatest reduction in post-quantization loss. Specifically, for a trained linear model with MSE loss, the post-quantization loss contribution from coefficient $i$ is approximately $H_{ii}(w_i - q_i)^2$, where $H_{ii} = \mathbb{E}[x_i^2]$ and $q_i$ is the quantized value. This decomposition suggests that the optimal protection set depends on both the input statistics and the quantizer's behavior, not on weight magnitude alone.

We evaluate this hypothesis in a controlled synthetic setting and report mixed results. The core finding is conditional: quantization-aware saliency helps only when it faithfully models the quantizer intervention, including group-scale recomputation after protection. A naive diagonal approximation that ignores this structure performs substantially worse than magnitude ranking. We regard the naive diagonal result as a negative finding and the greedy structural result as a conditional positive, limited to the heavy-tailed weight regime in this testbed.

## Method

### Problem Setup

We consider a linear regression model $\hat{y} = \mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{w}$ with ridge-fitted weights $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^d$. The model is quantized using symmetric grouped quantization at bit widths $b \in \{2, 3, 4\}$. A fraction $p \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$ of coefficients may be protected in a full-precision side table, exempt from quantization. When a coefficient is protected, the remaining coefficients in its quantization group are requantized with a recomputed group scale.

### Saliency Methods Compared

We evaluate the following methods for ranking coefficients by protection priority:

1. **Magnitude**: $|w_i|$, the standard baseline for mixed-precision selection.
2. **Hessian/pruning saliency**: $H_{ii} \cdot w_i^2$, the diagonal Optimal Brain Surgeon-style score.
3. **Quantization-error-only**: $(w_i - q_i)^2$, ignoring input statistics.
4. **Random**: uniform random ranking.
5. **Oracle**: single-coordinate gain ranking (greedily selects the coefficient whose protection yields the largest MSE reduction, computed exactly).
6. **`qas_diag` (naive diagonal QAS)**: $H_{ii} \cdot (w_i - q_i)^2$, computed in a single forward pass without modeling the effect of protection on group quantization scales.
7. **`qas_greedy_exact` (greedy structural QAS)**: iteratively selects the coefficient whose protection yields the largest MSE reduction, recomputing group quantization scales after each protection step.

The distinction between `qas_diag` and `qas_greedy_exact` is the central comparison of this study. Both incorporate quantization error and Hessian information, but only `qas_greedy_exact` accounts for the structural dependency between protection decisions and group quantization scales.

### Synthetic Data Generation

Experiments use synthetic correlated linear regression problems. Features are drawn from a multivariate distribution with controlled correlation structure. Weight distributions span three regimes:

- **Heavy-tailed**: weights drawn from a distribution with high kurtosis, producing outliers analogous to the outlier channels observed in transformer weight matrices.
- **Dense-log**: weights with a log-uniform distribution, representing a regime with moderate variation.
- **Gaussian**: standard normal weights, serving as a baseline without outliers.

### Evaluation Protocol

For each configuration (seed, bit width, protection fraction, weight mode), we apply each saliency method to select the top-$k$ coefficients for protection, quantize the remaining coefficients, and measure the resulting MSE. The primary metric is the **excess ratio**: the ratio of a method's MSE to the oracle MSE, where lower values indicate better performance.

We run 30 random seeds per configuration, yielding 5,670 total measurement rows. Aggregate comparisons are reported both by configuration cell (the best method per unique combination of bit width, protection fraction, and weight mode, yielding 27 cells) and by individual pairwise comparison (wins/ties/losses against magnitude across all seeds and configurations).

### Implementation

The experiment is implemented in pure NumPy with no dependency on PyTorch or scikit-learn. All computation is CPU-only. Source code, tests, and analysis scripts are listed in the Referenced Artifacts section.

## Results

### Aggregate Cell Comparison

Across 27 configuration cells (3 bit widths × 3 protection fractions × 3 weight modes), `qas_greedy_exact` achieves the best MSE in 14 cells, while magnitude achieves the best in 13 cells. This near-tie at the aggregate level masks substantial heterogeneity across weight regimes.

### Individual Pairwise Comparisons: Greedy Structural QAS vs. Magnitude

In seed-level pairwise comparisons of `qas_greedy_exact` vs. magnitude across all configurations:

| Comparison | Count |
|---|---|
| `qas_greedy_exact` wins | 385 |
| Ties | 12 |
| `qas_greedy_exact` losses | 413 |

Magnitude wins more individual comparisons than `qas_greedy_exact`. The mean excess-ratio delta of `qas_greedy_exact` relative to magnitude is $+0.0104$, indicating a small overall disadvantage. This result is negative in the aggregate: the greedy structural QAS does not reliably outperform magnitude across all configurations.

### Heavy-Tailed Weight Regime

In the heavy-tailed weight regime, `qas_greedy_exact` achieves the best MSE in all 9/9 aggregate cells (3 bit widths × 3 protection fractions). The mean excess-ratio delta relative to magnitude is $-0.00905$, indicating a consistent advantage. This is the strongest positive finding for QAS in this study, though the magnitude of the improvement is modest (approximately 0.9 percentage points in excess ratio).

### Naive Diagonal QAS: A Negative Result

The naive `qas_diag` method is decisively outperformed by magnitude:

| Comparison | Count |
|---|---|
| `qas_diag` wins | 64 |
| Ties | 0 |
| `qas_diag` losses | 746 |

The mean excess-ratio delta is $+0.2183$, indicating that `qas_diag` performs substantially worse than simple magnitude ranking. This negative result is the clearest finding of the study: merely multiplying Hessian diagonal elements by per-coefficient quantization error, without accounting for how protection changes group quantization scales, produces a saliency score that is actively misleading. The `qas_diag` score is worse than random protection in many configurations.

### Computational Cost

The full sweep of 5,670 measurement rows completed in 73.6 seconds wall time with maximum RSS of 47,756 KB on a CPU-only system with no swap usage. The greedy structural QAS method is more expensive than one-shot methods due to its iterative requantization, but remains tractable in this synthetic setting. Scaling to large models would require approximation.

## Limitations

1. **Synthetic testbed only.** All experiments use synthetic linear regression with ridge-fitted weights. The results may not transfer to deep neural networks, where activation distributions, layer interactions, and non-linearities introduce complexities absent from this setting. This is a toy simulation, not a production validation.

2. **Linear model assumption.** The QAS derivation assumes that the loss can be locally approximated by a quadratic form. This is exact for linear regression with MSE loss but is an approximation for general neural networks, and the quality of that approximation is not evaluated here.

3. **Grouped symmetric quantization only.** The quantization scheme is symmetric and grouped. Asymmetric quantization, per-channel scales, and floating-point quantization formats (FP8, FP4) are not evaluated.

4. **No activation calibration.** The Hessian diagonal elements are computed from input feature statistics in the synthetic setting. In practice, these would need to be estimated from calibration data, introducing additional error that is not modeled here.

5. **Small overall advantage even in the favorable regime.** In the heavy-tailed regime where `qas_greedy_exact` is strongest, the mean excess-ratio improvement over magnitude is approximately 0.9 percentage points. Whether this margin is practically significant depends on the application context.

6. **Greedy structural QAS cost.** The iterative requantization in `qas_greedy_exact` scales with the number of protected parameters. For large models with many outlier channels, this cost may become prohibitive without approximation strategies not evaluated here.

7. **No comparison to existing mixed-precision baselines.** Methods such as AWQ or SmoothQuant are not evaluated in this testbed. The comparison is limited to simple saliency ranking methods, and no claim is made about relative performance against production mixed-precision schemes.

8. **Overall negative aggregate result for greedy structural QAS.** Despite the conditional advantage in the heavy-tailed regime, `qas_greedy_exact` loses to magnitude in the overall pairwise comparison (413 losses vs. 385 wins). The conditional positive finding should not be overstated.

## Conclusion

We evaluated quantization-aware saliency (QAS) as a method for selecting parameters to protect in mixed-precision quantization of linear models. The results are conditional and mixed:

- **Naive diagonal QAS (`qas_diag`)**, which computes $H_{ii}(w_i - q_i)^2$ without modeling the quantizer intervention, is not viable. It is substantially outperformed by magnitude ranking (64 wins vs. 746 losses), with a mean excess-ratio penalty of $+0.2183$. This is the study's clearest finding and a negative result.

- **Greedy structural QAS (`qas_greedy_exact`)**, which models the quantizer intervention and requantizes affected groups at each protection step, shows a conditional advantage in the heavy-tailed weight regime (best in 9/9 aggregate cells, mean delta $-0.00905$ vs. magnitude). However, it is slightly worse than magnitude overall (385 wins vs. 413 losses, mean delta $+0.0104$). This is a conditional positive, not a general advantage.

The central finding is that quantization-aware saliency is viable only when the saliency computation faithfully models the quantization intervention, including the recomputation of group quantization scales after protection. A score that incorporates quantization error but ignores this structural dependency is actively harmful. Whether the conditional advantage of greedy structural QAS in outlier-heavy regimes transfers to real transformer weights and activation-calibrated Hessians remains an open question. If continuing this line of work, the recommended next step is to evaluate greedy structural QAS on real transformer layer weights and activation-calibrated Hessian/importance estimates, against AWQ/SmoothQuant-style baselines.
