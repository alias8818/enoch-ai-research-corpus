## Abstract

We investigate whether supervised compact "recap" tokens—short, information-dense summaries of earlier context—can make model behavior degrade more gracefully when the available context budget is reduced. In a controlled synthetic key/value report task with sklearn classifiers, we compare plain full-context baselines against conditions where oracle recap tokens replace raw context under tight budgets. Across five random seeds, faithful recap tokens at the minimum budget of 10 tokens yield 100.0% accuracy versus 56.6% for the best plain baseline (+43.4 percentage points), and at 14 tokens yield 100.0% versus 78.1% (+21.9 points). However, the same mechanism exhibits a sharp failure mode under recap corruption: at 25% noise injection, budget-14 recap accuracy (74.9%) falls below the plain baseline (78.1%), and expected calibration error rises substantially (0.095 at 10% noise versus 0.003 at full fidelity). These results establish an upper bound on the mechanism's viability in a narrow setting and identify semantic drift in recap tokens as the primary risk. The experiment uses sklearn classifiers and synthetic data; it does not test whether a language model can *generate* faithful recaps, only whether faithful recaps can be *consumed* effectively. Closure on the scientific question requires generative experiments with non-oracle recaps on naturalistic data.

## Introduction

When language models operate under constrained context budgets—due to inference cost, hardware limits, or streaming constraints—early context is typically truncated or dropped. This creates a degradation pattern where information required for a downstream query may be lost, and accuracy declines sharply as the budget shrinks.

One proposed mitigation is to insert compact "recap" tokens that summarize earlier context into a shorter representation. If recap tokens faithfully preserve task-relevant information, they could flatten the degradation curve: the model retains access to critical facts even when raw context is pruned.

The central question is: **Can supervised compact recap tokens make behavior degrade more gracefully than plain training when the available context budget is reduced?**

This paper reports a minimal viable experiment designed as an upper-bound mechanism check. We use a synthetic key/value report task where oracle recap tokens are provided by construction, isolating the question of whether recap tokens can be *consumed* effectively from the harder question of whether they can be *generated* faithfully. We also inject controlled noise into recap tokens to characterize the failure mode. The experiment is deliberately narrow: it establishes what is possible under ideal recap fidelity, not what is achievable in practice.

## Method

### Task Design

We designed a synthetic key/value report task as a controlled information-availability test:

- Each synthetic report contains 8 keys, each assigned one of 8 possible values.
- A late query asks for the value of one key.
- **Full raw context** costs 18 tokens: 16 raw fact tokens (8 × `key value` pairs) plus 2 query tokens.
- **Recap context** costs 10 tokens: 8 canonical recap value tokens plus 2 query tokens, achieving a 2× compression of raw facts.

This design ensures that the information content is fully known and the recap representation is unambiguous, eliminating confounds from natural-language ambiguity.

### Conditions

We compare three conditions across multiple budget levels:

1. **Plain baseline.** The model sees only the raw fact tokens that fit within the budget, reading from the end (suffix). Early facts are simply dropped.
2. **Faithful recap.** The model sees oracle recap tokens (correct canonical values) plus the query, within the budget.
3. **Noisy recap.** Recap tokens are corrupted at controlled rates (10% and 25% of values replaced with incorrect values) to simulate semantic drift.

### Model

Because PyTorch was not available in the experiment environment, the MVP uses sklearn classifiers rather than a neural language model. An attended-value feature extractor isolates the central question—information retained by recap tokens—from representation-learning noise that would arise in a tiny end-to-end model. This choice limits generalizability but provides a clean test of the information-retention mechanism. The classifier uses an attended-value feature to isolate the information-retention question from representation-learning confounds.

### Budget Levels

We evaluate at three token budgets:

- **10 tokens:** Only recap+query fits; plain baseline sees just 2 raw facts (suffix) plus query.
- **14 tokens:** Recap+query fits; plain baseline sees 4 raw facts plus query.
- **18 tokens:** Full context; both conditions see all information.

### Evaluation Metrics

- **Accuracy:** Proportion of correct value predictions.
- **Negative log-likelihood (NLL):** Confidence-weighted loss.
- **Expected calibration error (ECE):** Absolute difference between predicted confidence and observed accuracy.

### Experimental Protocol

We run each condition over 5 random seeds and report means. The full experiment elapsed approximately 4 seconds on the test machine (Linux aarch64, kernel 6.17.0-1014-nvidia). Memory usage was negligible (~115 GiB available, unchanged before and after; swap disabled). No GPU was required.

## Results

### Accuracy by Budget and Condition

| Budget (tokens) | Best Plain Accuracy | Faithful Recap Accuracy | Gain (pts) | 10% Noisy Recap Acc. | 25% Noisy Recap Acc. |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 10 | 56.6% | 100.0% | +43.4 | 90.0% | 74.7% |
| 14 | 78.1% | 100.0% | +21.9 | 90.3% | 74.9% |
| 18 | 100.0% | 100.0% | 0.0 | 89.7% | 75.2% |

At the tightest budget (10 tokens), faithful recap tokens eliminate the degradation entirely, achieving 100.0% accuracy where the plain baseline retains only 56.6%. At 14 tokens, the gain remains substantial at +21.9 points. At full budget (18 tokens), both conditions achieve ceiling performance, as expected.

### Calibration Effects

Faithful recap at budget 10 exhibited very low NLL (0.0033) and ECE (0.0033), indicating well-calibrated confidence. However, noise injection degraded calibration substantially:

- At 10% recap noise, budget-10 accuracy remained high (90.0%) but ECE rose to 0.095, showing that semantic drift hurts calibration even when accuracy is partially preserved.
- At 25% recap noise, budget-14 recap accuracy (74.9%) fell below the best plain baseline (78.1%), confirming that unfaithful recap tokens can be *worse* than plain context when sufficient raw context remains.

### Noise Sensitivity and Crossover

The 25% noise condition reveals an important crossover: at budget 14, the noisy recap path underperforms the plain baseline by 3.2 points. This crossover does not appear at budget 10 because the plain baseline is already severely degraded (56.6%), and even 75% faithful recap information outperforms having only 2 raw facts. The pattern indicates that recap corruption is most damaging at intermediate budgets where plain context still carries substantial information. At full budget (18 tokens), the noisy recap conditions (89.7% and 75.2%) substantially underperform both the plain and faithful-recap ceilings (100.0%), confirming that recap noise imposes a cost even when raw context is fully available—though this condition is artificial, since at full budget one would simply use raw context.

## Limitations

1. **Oracle recap assumption.** The experiment supplies recap tokens by construction. It does not test whether a language model can generate faithful recap tokens from arbitrary text. This is the most important limitation: the positive results represent an upper bound on the mechanism's performance, not a demonstration that the mechanism works in practice.

2. **Synthetic task.** The key/value report task has unambiguous, discrete information content. Naturalistic long-context tasks (multi-turn dialogue, document QA, code generation) involve far more complex information structures, and recap compression may lose critical nuance even without explicit noise.

3. **Non-neural model.** The use of sklearn classifiers rather than a neural language model limits the ecological validity of the results. A transformer-based model may interact differently with recap token representations, for better or worse.

4. **Noise model.** Recap corruption is modeled as uniform random value replacement, which may not reflect the distribution of errors a generative model would produce. Real recap errors may be correlated, systematic, or adversarial in ways this test does not capture.

5. **Single task structure.** The 8-key report with late query is one specific information-retrieval pattern. The mechanism may behave differently for tasks requiring reasoning over the recap, multi-hop inference, or tasks where the recap must preserve relational structure rather than atomic facts.

6. **No training dynamics.** The experiment does not test whether auxiliary recap-supervision losses introduce training instability, mode collapse, or other optimization pathologies in a generative model.

7. **Small seed count.** Five seeds provide a preliminary estimate of variance but may not capture the full distribution of outcomes, especially for the noisy conditions where accuracy is far from ceiling.

8. **No latency or memory overhead measurement.** In a production system, recap token generation and supervision would impose compute and memory costs not captured by this toy experiment.

## Conclusion

In a controlled synthetic setting, supervised compact recap tokens materially flatten budget degradation when they faithfully preserve task-relevant early context. At the minimum recap budget of 10 tokens, faithful recaps achieve 100.0% accuracy versus 56.6% for the best plain baseline (+43.4 points), exceeding the pre-registered ≥5-point gain criterion. At 14 tokens, the gain remains +21.9 points.

However, the same mechanism exhibits a sharp failure mode under recap corruption. At 25% noise, recap-conditioned accuracy at budget 14 (74.9%) falls below the plain baseline (78.1%), and calibration degrades measurably even at 10% noise (ECE 0.095 vs. 0.003). This confirms that semantic drift in recap tokens is the primary risk: unfaithful recaps can be worse than having no recaps at all, particularly at intermediate budgets where plain context still carries substantial information.

These results establish an upper bound on the mechanism's viability. They do not demonstrate that the mechanism works in practice, because the experiment uses oracle recap tokens rather than model-generated recaps, synthetic rather than naturalistic data, and sklearn classifiers rather than a neural language model. Scientific closure requires a generative experiment in which a sequence model is trained to emit recap tokens with auxiliary supervision, and downstream answers are conditioned on generated (not oracle) recaps under raw-context pruning. We recommend that any follow-up experiment directly test recap generation fidelity alongside answer accuracy, calibration, and abstention behavior, and that continuation be gated on recap fidelity metrics rather than answer accuracy alone.
