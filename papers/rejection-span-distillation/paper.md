## Abstract

Speculative decoding accelerates autoregressive inference by proposing multiple draft tokens and verifying them against a target model, discarding any suffix beyond the first rejection. We investigate whether the contexts associated with these rejected spans provide a useful distillation curriculum—*rejection-span distillation* (RSD)—for aligning a draft model to its target. In a controlled synthetic experiment using exact Markov target and draft distributions with intentional domain-shift mismatches, we compare RSD against baseline (no distillation), first-reject-only distillation, uniform on-policy knowledge distillation, and uniform teacher-sampled distillation. RSD raises one-step acceptance from 0.803 to 0.992 and a verifier-call speedup proxy from 4.01 to 6.83, while using fewer context updates than uniform on-policy distillation. At constrained training budgets (80–320 steps), RSD achieves higher speedup proxy than uniform on-policy distillation; however, at a large 900-step budget, uniform on-policy distillation slightly exceeds RSD (speedup proxy 6.91 vs. 6.83). These results are limited to synthetic Markov models on CPU; no real transformer fine-tuning or wall-clock timing was performed. The findings support the viability of rejection-derived contexts as a targeted curriculum but do not establish superiority over broad distillation when labeling budgets are unconstrained.

## 1. Introduction

Speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023) uses a small draft model to propose short token sequences that a larger target model verifies in parallel, accepting a prefix and discarding the suffix beyond the first mismatch. The method preserves the target distribution exactly while potentially producing multiple tokens per target-model call. Its efficiency depends critically on the acceptance rate: the closer the draft distribution is to the target, the longer the accepted prefix and the fewer the target-model calls.

DistillSpec (2024) demonstrated that knowledge distillation from target to draft improves speculative-decoding speedups, using on-policy draft-generated data and decoding-specific divergence objectives. The present work narrows this idea: rather than distilling on all on-policy draft data, we ask whether the *rejected spans*—the contexts where speculative decoding actually fails—provide a particularly efficient training signal.

The intuition is that accepted tokens already indicate sufficient draft-target alignment, while rejected tokens mark the precise contexts where alignment breaks down. A curriculum that prioritizes these failure contexts might achieve comparable or better alignment with fewer gradient updates.

We test this hypothesis in a controlled setting: exact synthetic Markov language models where the target distribution $p$ is known analytically, and the draft distribution $q$ is initially close in normal contexts but intentionally shifted on sparse hard contexts. This permits measurement of acceptance rates and KL divergence without judge noise, sampling variance from private APIs, or approximation error. The trade-off is that these are toy models, and the results do not directly transfer to transformer-based LLMs.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Setup

We define a synthetic Markov target distribution $p(x \mid c)$ and a draft distribution $q(x \mid c)$ over a finite vocabulary. The draft is constructed to be close to the target on most contexts but systematically shifted on a sparse set of "hard" contexts, simulating domain-shift mismatches observed in real draft-target pairs.

### 2.2 Speculative Decoding

Speculative decoding proposes $K = 6$ draft tokens from $q$, then verifies against $p$ using exact rejection sampling. The accepted prefix is the longest prefix where every draft token passes verification. The *rejection span* consists of the first rejected context (the context at the point of rejection) plus the discarded suffix of the proposed block.

### 2.3 Distillation Curricula

We compare five conditions:

1. **Baseline**: No distillation. The draft model retains its initial mismatched distribution.
2. **First-reject**: Distill $q(\cdot \mid c)$ toward $p(\cdot \mid c)$ only on the context $c$ of the first rejected token in each speculative block.
3. **Rejection-span (RSD)**: Distill on all contexts in the rejection span—the first rejected context plus the discarded suffix contexts.
4. **Uniform on-policy**: Distill on contexts sampled uniformly from the draft model's on-policy trajectories, regardless of acceptance.
5. **Uniform teacher**: Distill on contexts sampled uniformly from the target model's trajectories.

In all distillation conditions, the update is a cross-entropy gradient step: $q(\cdot \mid c)$ is pushed toward $p(\cdot \mid c)$.

### 2.4 Metrics

- **One-step acceptance**: $\sum_x \min(p(x \mid c),\, q(x \mid c))$, averaged over contexts $c$.
- **Speedup proxy**: $1 / (\text{target calls per token})$. This is an algorithmic verifier-call proxy that *ignores* draft compute and batching overhead; it is not a wall-clock speedup.
- **KL divergence**: $\mathrm{KL}(p \,\|\, q)$, computed exactly.
- **Hard-context acceptance**: One-step acceptance restricted to the intentionally shifted contexts where targeted distillation should help most.
- **Context updates**: Total number of gradient updates performed (a proxy for labeling/compute cost).

### 2.5 Experimental Procedure

Two experiments were run:

**Full run.** 12 seeds, 900 training steps, batch of 128 spans per step, 80,000 evaluation tokens. This tests saturation behavior.

**Budget sweep.** 6 seeds, training steps in {20, 40, 80, 160, 320}, 20,000 evaluation tokens. This tests efficiency at constrained budgets.

All experiments ran on CPU (Python 3.12.3, NumPy 2.4.4). Maximum resident set size was approximately 36.7 MiB. An NVIDIA GB10 was visible on the host but GPU utilization remained 0% throughout; the experiment was CPU-only.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Full Run (900 Steps)

| Method | One-step acceptance | Speedup proxy | KL(p‖q) | Context updates | Hard-context acceptance |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Baseline | 0.8034 ± 0.0044 | 4.0070 ± 0.0503 | 0.3606 | 0 | 0.4256 |
| First-reject | 0.9821 ± 0.0002 | 6.6372 ± 0.0089 | 0.0031 | 16,684 | 0.9748 |
| Rejection-span | 0.9919 ± 0.0001 | 6.8341 ± 0.0063 | 0.0011 | 29,994 | 0.9851 |
| Uniform on-policy | 0.9954 ± 0.0002 | 6.9070 ± 0.0063 | 0.0006 | 57,599 | 0.9884 |
| Uniform teacher | 0.9954 ± 0.0002 | 6.9082 ± 0.0066 | 0.0007 | 49,640 | 0.9883 |

RSD improves one-step acceptance by +0.1885 absolute over baseline and the speedup proxy by +2.83. Hard-context acceptance rises from 0.426 to 0.985, confirming that rejection-derived contexts effectively target the draft's known weaknesses.

However, uniform on-policy distillation achieves slightly higher acceptance (0.9954 vs. 0.9919) and speedup proxy (6.907 vs. 6.834) after 900 steps, while using nearly twice as many context updates (57,599 vs. 29,994). The KL divergence under RSD (0.0011) is higher than under uniform on-policy (0.0006), indicating that RSD leaves more residual mismatch in contexts it does not prioritize.

First-reject distillation is surprisingly strong given its minimal context budget (16,684 updates), reaching acceptance 0.9821 and speedup proxy 6.6372, though it plateaus below RSD.

At low budgets (20–40 steps), first-reject distillation achieves the highest speedup proxy, suggesting the first rejected token is a high-precision training signal. At mid budgets (80–320 steps), RSD overtakes both first-reject and uniform methods. At 320 steps, RSD reaches speedup proxy 6.5604 versus uniform on-policy's 6.5411, while using fewer context updates (approximately 14,300 vs. 20,500).

At the 900-step budget (Section 3.1), uniform on-policy distillation catches up and slightly exceeds RSD. This crossover weakens any claim that RSD is universally superior as a distillation objective; its advantage appears to be budget-dependent.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Synthetic models only.** The target and draft are exact Markov distributions, not transformer networks. There is no real fine-tuning, no LoRA adaptation, and no wall-clock training or inference measurement. Whether rejection spans provide the same curriculum advantage for high-dimensional transformer parameter spaces is unknown.

2. **Speedup proxy, not wall-clock speed.** The speedup proxy counts target-model calls per token and ignores draft-model compute, verification batching overhead, and memory hierarchy effects. Real speculative-decoding speedups depend on wall-clock time, which this experiment does not measure.

3. **Known hard contexts.** The synthetic draft was constructed with intentional domain-shift mismatches on sparse contexts. This design favors methods that target those contexts. Real draft-target mismatches may be more diffuse, and the advantage of rejection-span prioritization may be smaller.

4. **No quality evaluation.** We measure acceptance rate and KL divergence but not generation quality, perplexity on held-out text, or downstream task performance. In a real LLM setting, distillation that over-aligns the draft to the target on rejection contexts could potentially degrade the draft's own capabilities.

5. **Single draft-target gap.** Only one magnitude and structure of draft-target mismatch was tested. The relative advantage of RSD may vary with the severity and distribution of mismatches.

6. **No hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.** The speculative block size $K = 6$ was fixed. The effect of $K$ on rejection-span composition and distillation efficiency was not studied.

7. **Claim audit status.** The structured claim ledger for this artifact is in a blocked state with no extracted claims, meaning no formal claim-evidence audit has been completed. The numerical results reported here are taken directly from the run logs and metric files and have not undergone independent verification.

## 6. Conclusion

Rejection-span distillation is a targeted curriculum that uses the failure contexts of speculative decoding to prioritize distillation updates. In synthetic Markov models with intentional draft-target mismatches, RSD substantially improves acceptance rates and verifier-call efficiency over an undistilled baseline, and outperforms uniform on-policy distillation at constrained training budgets (80–320 steps). At larger budgets (900 steps), uniform on-policy distillation slightly exceeds RSD, indicating that the advantage of rejection-span prioritization is budget-dependent rather than absolute.

The core mechanism appears viable: rejection-derived contexts are not noise; they consistently improve hard-context alignment and are competitive with broader distillation under update budgets. However, when target distributions are cheap enough to label everywhere, full uniform or on-policy distillation can match or slightly exceed RSD.

These results do not constitute validation on real language models. The critical next step is to test RSD on an actual draft-target transformer pair (e.g., via LoRA fine-tuning) with measured acceptance rates, wall-clock tokens per second, and generation quality, across multiple speculative block sizes $K \in \{4, 6, 8\}$.
