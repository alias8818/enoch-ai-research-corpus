## Abstract

Retrieval-augmented generation systems face a tension between presenting clean, high-confidence evidence and defending against noisy or adversarial retrieval results through negative-evidence packing. We introduce a retrieval-aware arbitration mechanism that gates between vanilla top-k context assembly and guarded negative-evidence packing based on retrieval confidence features—specifically, the top retrieval score and the score margin between the top and runner-up citations. On a bounded benchmark of 48 SciFact cases evaluated with Qwen2.5-7B via llama.cpp, the arbiter routes all high-confidence reranked queries to vanilla top-k (preserving answer+evidence accuracy of 0.667 at budget 240) and all low-confidence hard-decoy queries to guarded packing (preserving answer+evidence accuracy of 0.167 at budget 240, where vanilla collapses to 0.000). A threshold sensitivity sweep over 90 parameter pairs confirms that the margin threshold is the primary control variable in this score geometry, with the default gate (top score ≥ 0.95, margin ≥ 0.08) tying the best swept mean accuracy. These results are limited to a single dataset, a single model, and synthetic retrieval score distributions; generalization to less structured retrieval scenarios remains unvalidated.

## 1. Introduction

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems assemble context from retrieved documents to ground model outputs in external evidence. Two competing strategies for context assembly present a design tension:

1. **Vanilla top-k packing** simply includes the top-k retrieved citations. This works well when the retriever confidently surfaces the gold citation, but it is fragile under noisy or adversarial retrieval where irrelevant or misleading documents dominate the top ranks.

2. **Guarded negative-evidence packing** explicitly structures context to include negative evidence and keep-alive signals, making the reader more robust to decoy documents. However, this strategy introduces overhead and can regress performance when retrieval is already high-confidence, because the additional structural tokens displace evidence content within a fixed budget.

The core observation motivating this work is that these two strategies have complementary failure modes: vanilla top-k fails under adversarial retrieval, while guarded packing can regress under high-confidence retrieval. A simple arbitration mechanism that selects the appropriate strategy based on retrieval confidence signals could, in principle, capture the benefits of both.

We implement and evaluate such a mechanism—a retrieval-confidence gate that inspects the top retrieval score and the score margin to the runner-up, routing to vanilla top-k when confidence is high and to guarded negative-evidence packing when confidence is low. We evaluate on the SciFact dataset under two retrieval conditions (reranked and hard-decoy) using Qwen2.5-7B served via llama.cpp, at token budgets of 240 and 480.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Retrieval Confidence Features

Given a ranked list of retrieved citations with associated scores, we extract two features:

- **Top score** ($s_1$): the retrieval score of the highest-ranked citation.
- **Score margin** ($m$): the difference $s_1 - s_2$ between the top-ranked and runner-up citation scores.

These features are computed by the function `retrieval_confidence_features()`.

### 2.2 Confidence-Gated Arbitration

The arbitration rule is:

$$
\text{route} = \begin{cases}
\text{vanilla\_topk} & \text{if } s_1 \geq 0.95 \text{ and } m \geq 0.08 \\
\text{guarded\_negative\_evidence} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

When the top retrieval score is high and well-separated from the runner-up, the retriever is judged to have confidently surfaced the gold citation, and vanilla top-k packing is used. Otherwise, guarded negative-evidence packing is selected to defend against potential decoys.

This gate is implemented in `retrieval_aware_evidence_arbitration()` in `src/negative_evidence_packer.py`.

### 2.3 Baseline Systems

Three context-assembly strategies are compared:

- **vanilla_topk**: Standard top-k context assembly without negative-evidence structuring.
- **negative_keepalive_guarded**: The parent guarded negative-evidence packer, which structures context with explicit negative-evidence and keep-alive signals regardless of retrieval confidence.
- **retrieval_aware_arbitration**: The proposed confidence-gated arbiter.

### 2.4 Threshold Sensitivity Analysis

After the primary benchmark, we perform an offline replay sweep over 90 combinations of top-score and margin thresholds, replaying the arbiter's routing decision between recorded vanilla and guarded outcomes for each threshold pair. This allows us to assess the sensitivity of the arbitration strategy to threshold choice without re-running the full reader benchmark.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Pilot Benchmark (24 SciFact Cases)

An initial pilot with 24 SciFact cases established the basic routing behavior:

| Condition | Budget | System | Exact | Ans+Evid | Evid Recall | Route |
|-----------|--------|--------|-------|----------|-------------|-------|
| Reranked | 240 | Vanilla | 0.792 | 0.792 | 0.958 | — |
| Reranked | 240 | Guarded | 0.250 | 0.250 | 0.833 | — |
| Reranked | 240 | Arbitration | 0.792 | 0.792 | 0.958 | vanilla_high_confidence: 24 |
| Hard-decoy | 240 | Vanilla | 0.000 | 0.000 | 0.000 | — |
| Hard-decoy | 240 | Guarded | 0.167 | 0.167 | 0.833 | — |
| Hard-decoy | 240 | Arbitration | 0.167 | 0.167 | 0.833 | guarded_low_confidence: 24 |

At budget 480, the pattern held: arbitration matched vanilla under reranked conditions (answer+evidence 0.792 vs. guarded 0.292) and matched guarded under hard-decoy conditions (answer+evidence 0.250 vs. vanilla 0.083). The arbiter also used fewer prompt tokens under hard-decoy at budget 480 (242.6 vs. 422.8 for vanilla).

### 3.3 Threshold Sensitivity

The offline replay sweep over 90 top-score/margin threshold pairs yielded:

- The default gate (top ≥ 0.95, margin ≥ 0.08) tied the best swept mean answer+evidence score of 0.427 across the four condition/budget cells.
- The margin threshold is the primary control: any margin threshold in the separating band above the hard-decoy margin (~0.03) and at or below the reranked margin (~0.11) preserves the desired route split.
- The best individual sweep row was top ≥ 0.70, margin ≥ 0.04, though this reflects the specific score geometry of the synthetic retrieval conditions rather than a universally optimal setting.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Single dataset.** All results are on SciFact, a scientific fact-verification benchmark with 48 evaluated cases. Performance on other datasets, domains, or query types is unknown.

2. **Single model.** Only Qwen2.5-7B was evaluated, served via llama.cpp. Behavior with other model families, sizes, or serving frameworks may differ.

3. **Synthetic retrieval score distributions.** The reranked and hard-decoy conditions produce score distributions with a clear separation (hard-decoy margins ~0.03, reranked margins ~0.11). Real retrieval systems may produce overlapping or less structured score distributions, making threshold selection less clean.

4. **Small evaluation slice.** The 48-case evaluation, while sufficient to observe the routing behavior, provides limited statistical power. Confidence intervals are not reported and would be wide at this sample size.

5. **Separate sampling for baselines.** The vanilla and guarded baselines at budget 480 were sampled separately from the arbitration runs. The slight arbitration-over-vanilla advantage under reranked 480 (0.667 vs. 0.646) may reflect sampling variance rather than a genuine effect.

6. **Threshold calibration.** The gate thresholds were calibrated to the score geometry of this specific benchmark. The sensitivity sweep shows robustness within the separating band, but the existence of such a clean band is an artifact of the synthetic retrieval conditions.

7. **No out-of-distribution test.** The arbiter was not evaluated on retrieval conditions that fall between the reranked and hard-decoy extremes, where the routing decision is most consequential and most uncertain.

8. **Absolute accuracy levels.** Under hard-decoy conditions, even the best system (guarded packing or arbitration) achieves only 0.167–0.250 answer+evidence accuracy. The arbitration mechanism preserves relative gains but does not solve the underlying difficulty of adversarial retrieval.

## 6. Conclusion

We have presented a retrieval-aware arbitration mechanism that gates between vanilla top-k and guarded negative-evidence packing based on retrieval confidence features. On a bounded 48-case SciFact benchmark with Qwen2.5-7B, the arbiter successfully preserves vanilla top-k quality under high-confidence reranked retrieval (answer+evidence 0.667 at budget 240, matching vanilla) and preserves guarded-packer gains under adversarial hard-decoy retrieval (answer+evidence 0.167 at budget 240, where vanilla collapses to 0.000). A threshold sensitivity sweep confirms that the margin threshold is the primary control variable and that the default gate parameters are within the optimal band for this score geometry.

These findings are bounded: they hold on one dataset, one model, and synthetic retrieval conditions with a clean score-margin separation. The arbitration mechanism is a candidate for integration, but validation on less structured retrieval score distributions and broader evaluation coverage is necessary before drawing stronger conclusions. The project decision is to finalize this branch as positive and defer out-of-distribution validation to future work.
