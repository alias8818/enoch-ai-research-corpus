## Abstract

Knowledge distillation conventionally transfers knowledge via soft logits or intermediate feature maps. We investigate whether teacher-provided saliency (attribution) maps can serve as useful auxiliary distillation targets when a student model has insufficient labels to reliably identify the correct input features. In a controlled synthetic setting—a sparse linear teacher with 6 salient features among 64 dimensions, plus correlated distractors—we compare label-only, logit KD, saliency KD, and combined logit-plus-saliency distillation across five training-set sizes (16–256 samples) with 20 random seeds each. Saliency KD consistently recovered the teacher's true feature support (top-6 precision = 1.0 at all training sizes) and improved accuracy over label-only baselines by +23.5 to +9.1 percentage points, with the largest gains under severe label scarcity. Combined logit-plus-saliency distillation achieved the highest or tied-highest accuracy in all conditions. However, these results are constrained to a synthetic linear teacher–student setting where saliency targets are exact and cheap to compute. Whether the mechanism survives noisy, expensive saliency extraction from real neural networks remains an open question.

## Introduction

Knowledge distillation (KD) transfers learned representations from a teacher model to a student, most commonly by matching the teacher's soft logits. Extensions have explored matching intermediate features, attention maps, or other internal representations. A separate line of work has examined saliency and attribution methods—such as input gradients, Grad-CAM, and attention rollout—as tools for explaining model predictions.

The intersection of these two directions raises a practical question: when training labels are scarce, a student model may latch onto spurious correlations rather than the features a teacher has already learned to rely on. If the teacher can provide not just output predictions but also *where it looks*—i.e., which input dimensions matter—could that attribution information guide the student toward the correct feature support more efficiently than labels or logits alone?

Prior work has explored related ideas. Jin et al. (EMNLP Findings 2021) introduced saliency-weighted auxiliary distillation for multimodal understanding tasks, reporting improvements over ordinary KD. Attention-transfer approaches have framed attention maps as inference-process knowledge to be matched between teacher and student (Nature Scientific Reports, 2023; "Show, Attend and Distill," arXiv 2021). Surrogate explanation networks have been trained to produce saliency maps more cheaply than iterative attribution methods (Sensors, 2021). However, these works have not isolated the mechanism question that motivates the present study: does attribution matching specifically help students recover the correct feature support under label scarcity, and how does it compare to logit-only distillation in a setting where the ground-truth salient features are known?

We address this question with a minimal synthetic experiment designed to isolate the mechanism. We construct a sparse linear teacher where the ground-truth salient features are known, inject correlated distractors that make label-only learning unreliable, and measure whether saliency distillation targets help students recover the correct features and achieve higher accuracy. We report results across 20 seeds and five training-set sizes.

## Method

### Problem Setting

We define a binary classification task with $d = 64$ input dimensions. A linear teacher model uses only $k = 6$ truly salient dimensions (selected uniformly at random) with weights drawn from $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. The remaining 58 dimensions are noise. To create a challenging learning environment, we inject correlated distractor dimensions: for each salient dimension $j$, we add a correlated copy dimension with correlation $\rho = 0.8$ to the salient signal but zero independent predictive power. This means a label-only student can achieve non-trivial accuracy by latching onto distractors rather than the true features.

### Saliency Target

For a linear model with logits $z = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b$, the input-gradient-times-input attribution for dimension $j$ is $a_j = x_j \cdot w_j$. The teacher's attribution vector $\mathbf{a}^{(T)}$ is computed exactly. Saliency distillation minimizes the mean squared error between the student's attribution vector and the teacher's attribution vector over the training batch:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{saliency}} = \frac{1}{d} \|\mathbf{a}^{(S)} - \mathbf{a}^{(T)}\|^2$$

This is feasible in the linear setting because the attribution is exact and deterministic. In real neural models, this target would require an approximate attribution method (e.g., Grad-CAM, integrated gradients, attention rollout) and would introduce noise and computational cost that are not modeled here.

### Training Regimes

We compare four student training regimes:

1. **Label-only**: Standard cross-entropy loss on ground-truth labels.
2. **Logit KD**: Cross-entropy loss plus KL divergence to the teacher's soft logits (temperature $T = 2$).
3. **Saliency KD**: Cross-entropy loss plus saliency attribution MSE loss.
4. **Combined**: Cross-entropy loss plus both logit KD and saliency KD terms.

All students are linear models trained with gradient descent on the same training data.

### Evaluation Protocol

We sweep training-set sizes $n \in \{16, 32, 64, 128, 256\}$. For each size, we run 20 independent seeds, each with a freshly sampled teacher, training set, and test set of 5,000 examples. We report:

- **Classification accuracy** on the held-out test set.
- **AUC-ROC** on the test set.
- **Top-6 precision**: Of the 6 dimensions with largest absolute student weight, how many are truly salient? This measures feature support recovery.
- **Saliency cosine similarity**: Cosine similarity between the student's and teacher's attribution vectors on the test set.

### Implementation

The experiment is implemented in NumPy only (no deep learning framework) to minimize dependency overhead. The script `scripts/saliency_distillation_experiment.py` performs all data generation, training, and evaluation. Full calibration runs completed in 12.4 seconds wall time on CPU with peak RSS of 44,808 KB. No GPU was used despite one being present on the machine.

## Results

### Classification Accuracy

| Train size | Label-only | Logit KD | Saliency KD | Combined |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 16 | 0.616 | 0.660 | 0.851 | 0.868 |
| 32 | 0.650 | 0.707 | 0.867 | 0.877 |
| 64 | 0.690 | 0.749 | 0.872 | 0.878 |
| 128 | 0.739 | 0.793 | 0.880 | 0.882 |
| 256 | 0.791 | 0.839 | 0.881 | 0.883 |

Saliency KD substantially outperformed both label-only and logit KD baselines at all training sizes. The accuracy gain over label-only was largest under severe scarcity (+23.5 pp at $n = 16$) and diminished as labels became abundant (+9.1 pp at $n = 256$), consistent with the hypothesis that attribution targets are most valuable when labels alone are insufficient for feature discovery.

Logit KD provided a meaningful but smaller improvement over label-only (+4.4 to +4.8 pp), and did not close the gap to saliency KD at any training size. Combined logit-plus-saliency distillation achieved the highest accuracy in all conditions, though the marginal gain over saliency KD alone was modest (1.2–1.7 pp).

### Feature Support Recovery

| Train size | Label-only top-6 prec. | Saliency KD top-6 prec. |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 16 | 0.267 | 1.000 |
| 32 | 0.283 | 1.000 |
| 64 | 0.500 | 1.000 |
| 128 | 0.775 | 1.000 |
| 256 | 0.950 | 1.000 |

Saliency KD achieved perfect top-6 precision (1.0) at every training size, meaning the student always identified exactly the 6 truly salient dimensions. Label-only students, by contrast, frequently selected distractor dimensions, especially at small training sizes. At $n = 16$, label-only top-6 precision was only 0.267, barely above the $\approx 0.103$ expected by random selection from 64 dimensions.

### Saliency Alignment

| Train size | Label-only cosine | Saliency KD cosine |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 16 | 0.345 | 0.990 |
| 32 | 0.413 | 0.992 |
| 64 | 0.536 | 0.995 |
| 128 | 0.694 | 0.997 |
| 256 | 0.835 | 0.998 |

Saliency cosine similarity between student and teacher attribution vectors was near-perfect (0.990–0.998) under saliency KD, compared to 0.345–0.835 for label-only training. This confirms that the attribution matching loss successfully aligned the student's feature weighting with the teacher's.

### AUC-ROC

| Train size | Label-only AUC | Saliency KD AUC |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 16 | 0.660 | 0.953 |
| 32 | 0.710 | 0.954 |
| 64 | 0.762 | 0.955 |
| 128 | 0.821 | 0.957 |
| 256 | 0.879 | 0.957 |

The AUC gap mirrors the accuracy gap, with saliency KD achieving substantially higher discrimination at all training sizes.

### Mixed and Negative Observations

Several aspects of the results temper an unqualifiedly positive interpretation:

- **Diminishing marginal returns.** The accuracy advantage of saliency KD over label-only shrinks monotonically from +23.5 pp at $n = 16$ to +9.1 pp at $n = 256$. At some (untested) larger training size, the advantage may vanish entirely, as label-only students eventually accumulate enough data to overcome the distractor correlation.

- **Saliency KD alone does not always match combined.** While saliency KD alone was close to the combined regime, the combined logit-plus-saliency condition consistently achieved the highest accuracy. The saliency term does not fully subsume the information in the logit term, suggesting the two provide complementary signals.

- **No variance reporting.** The tables report means over 20 seeds but not standard deviations. The magnitude of seed-to-seed variability is not characterized in the available metrics, which limits the strength of the comparative claims.

- **Logit KD top-6 precision not reported.** The run notes report top-6 precision for label-only and saliency KD but not for logit KD alone, making it impossible to assess whether logit KD partially recovers feature support or improves accuracy through a different mechanism.

## Limitations

This study has several important limitations that constrain the generality of its conclusions:

1. **Synthetic linear setting.** The teacher and student are both linear models. Real neural networks have nonlinear interactions, and the effectiveness of attribution matching may differ substantially when features interact multiplicatively or hierarchically. This experiment establishes mechanism viability in a toy setting, not production effectiveness.

2. **Exact saliency targets.** Because the teacher is linear, the attribution map $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{x} \odot \mathbf{w}$ is exact and deterministic. In practice, saliency targets from real neural networks would be produced by approximate methods (Grad-CAM, integrated gradients, attention rollout) that introduce noise, may be inconsistent across inputs, and carry non-trivial computational cost. The impact of target noise and extraction cost on distillation quality is unmeasured here.

3. **Known ground-truth features.** The evaluation of feature support recovery (top-6 precision) relies on knowing which dimensions are truly salient. In real applications, this ground truth is generally unavailable, making it harder to assess whether the student has recovered the correct features.

4. **Single task architecture.** Only binary classification with a fixed input dimensionality and sparsity level was tested. The results may not generalize to multi-class settings, different sparsity levels, or different correlation structures among distractors.

5. **No comparison to feature/attention KD.** The experiment compares saliency KD to label-only and logit KD baselines but does not include intermediate feature matching or attention-transfer baselines, which are closer relatives in the KD literature and may provide similar feature-localization benefits.

6. **No extraction cost analysis.** The computational overhead of generating saliency targets from a real teacher (which may require backpropagation through the teacher per input) was not modeled or measured.

7. **Claim ledger audit status.** The structured claim ledger for this artifact was flagged as `blocked_empty_claims` at the time of draft generation, meaning no formal claims passed the evidence-audit pipeline. The results reported here should be read as preliminary pilot observations rather than audit-approved claims.

## Conclusion

In a controlled synthetic setting with a sparse linear teacher and correlated distractors, saliency distillation targets substantially improved student accuracy and perfectly recovered the teacher's feature support, with the largest gains under severe label scarcity. The mechanism signal is clear and consistent: when a student cannot reliably identify the correct features from labels alone, teacher-provided attribution maps can guide it toward the right feature support.

However, this result is a positive mechanism pilot, not a broad empirical claim. The gap between exact linear attribution and noisy neural attribution is significant. The diminishing returns as label count grows, the absence of variance statistics, and the lack of feature/attention KD baselines all constrain the strength of the conclusions. Whether saliency distillation survives the noise, cost, and approximation error of real attribution methods in real neural networks remains the critical open question. A follow-on benchmark with trained neural teachers, noisy saliency extraction, and downstream task evaluation is needed before the mechanism can be considered validated for practical use.
