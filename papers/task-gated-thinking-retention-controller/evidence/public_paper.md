## Abstract

We evaluate a task-gated thinking retention controller that scores agent reasoning records by open downstream task gates, retaining facts required by still-open gates while evicting closed-gate and off-task records under a fixed token budget. In a deterministic synthetic benchmark simulating LangGraph-style agent episodes with intentionally high-salience noise, the task-gated policy achieved a mean downstream query accuracy of 0.738 across 12 parameter regimes (4 token budgets × 3 noise levels, 1,000 episodes per regime), compared to 0.116 for FIFO and 0.048 for global salience. A summary variant matched this accuracy (0.739) while consuming 6.0% fewer mean tokens. Global salience performed worst because high-salience off-task records displaced lower-salience but task-critical constraints. These results support the viability of gate-aware retention as a prototype direction, but the evaluation is confined to a synthetic harness with explicit future-gate metadata; no real LLM traces or production agent outcomes were tested.

## 1. Introduction

Agents that reason over multiple steps accumulate intermediate thinking records that compete for limited context window capacity. When the budget is exhausted, a retention policy must decide which records to evict. Common heuristics—first-in-first-out (FIFO) eviction or retention by local salience—ignore whether a given record will be needed by a downstream task that has not yet closed. This mismatch can cause task-critical constraints (often low in local salience) to be displaced by off-task records that score highly on generic importance measures.

We investigate a simple alternative: score each thought record by the set of downstream task gates that remain open at eviction time. Records needed by open gates receive a retention boost; records associated only with closed gates or no gates are demoted. This approach requires that the agent's task structure expose gate metadata—either explicitly or through inference.

The central question is whether such gate-aware scoring preserves downstream-useful facts more reliably than ungated policies under the same token budget, particularly when off-task thoughts carry high local salience designed to fool global-salience methods.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Benchmark Design

We implemented a deterministic synthetic benchmark (`scripts/evaluate_retention_controller.py`) that generates LangGraph-style agent episodes without external LLM dependencies. Each episode contains:

- **Root constraints**: facts generated at the outset that are required later by integration or verification steps.
- **Active and inactive branches**: one branch with open downstream gates and one with closed gates, distinguishing task-relevant from task-irrelevant reasoning.
- **High-salience off-task noise**: records intentionally assigned high local salience scores but no downstream utility, designed to exploit global-salience policies.
- **Downstream queries**: questions that require exact retained facts from earlier thought records.
- **Fixed token budget and gate closure schedule**: the budget is consumed as records accumulate; gates close according to a predetermined schedule.

### 2.2 Retention Policies

Four policies were compared:

1. **FIFO**: Oldest records are evicted first, regardless of content or task relevance.
2. **Global salience**: Records with the highest local salience score are retained; no task-gate information is used.
3. **Task-gated**: Records needed by still-open downstream gates receive a retention boost; records associated only with closed gates or off-task noise are demoted.
4. **Task-gated summary**: Identical scoring to task-gated, but future-required facts are stored as compact summaries rather than full records, reducing token consumption.

### 2.3 Metrics

- **Primary**: Downstream query accuracy—the fraction of required facts still available in retained context when queried.
- **Secondary**: Mean retained tokens, useful-record precision (fraction of retained records that are task-relevant), and eviction count.

### 2.4 Experimental Matrix

We swept 12 regimes: 4 token budgets (450, 600, 750, 1000) × 3 noise multipliers (1, 2, 4), with 1,000 episodes per regime (12,000 episodes total). A calibration run of 200 episodes at budget 750 and noise multiplier 2 preceded the full matrix. A 5-episode smoke test verified harness correctness before calibration.

### 2.5 Execution Environment

All runs executed on `gx10-efe8`, Linux 6.17.0-1014-nvidia, Python 3.12.3, aarch64. Swap was disabled; available memory remained approximately 121.6 GB throughout smoke, calibration, and final runs, indicating no memory pressure.

### 3.3 Key Observations

**Task-gated policies dominate across all regimes.** Even under the most adverse condition tested (noise multiplier 4, budget 450), task-gated retention achieved 0.642 accuracy versus 0.000 for both baselines.

**Global salience is counterproductive in this setting.** Its mean accuracy (0.048) fell below FIFO (0.116) because the benchmark's high-salience noise records were designed to be irrelevant to downstream tasks. Global salience retained these noise records at the expense of task-critical constraints, yielding a useful-record precision of only 0.0013.

**The summary variant trades precision for token efficiency.** Task-gated summary matched or slightly exceeded task-gated accuracy (0.7391 vs. 0.7375) while consuming 5.97% fewer mean tokens. However, its useful-record precision was lower (0.4780 vs. 0.6652), suggesting that summaries introduce some irrelevant or redundant content alongside the preserved facts.

**Accuracy degrades with noise and tight budgets for all policies.** Task-gated accuracy dropped from 0.824 (noise 1, budget 450) to 0.642 (noise 4, budget 450), a 22% relative decline. Baseline policies were near zero across high-noise regimes regardless of budget.

**FIFO benefits modestly from larger budgets.** At noise 1, FIFO accuracy rose from 0.104 (budget 450) to 0.314 (budget 1000), but remained far below task-gated performance even at the largest budget.

**Eviction counts diverge sharply.** Task-gated policies averaged 11.7 evictions per episode, compared to 38.5 for FIFO and 39.1 for global salience, reflecting that task-gated retention preserves relevant records rather than cycling through them. The summary variant averaged 34.4 evictions, higher than task-gated, consistent with its strategy of compressing and then evicting the original records.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Synthetic benchmark only.** The harness tests control-plane retention logic in a controlled setting with procedurally generated episodes. No real LLM hidden states, actual LangGraph traces, or production agent outcomes were evaluated. The results demonstrate algorithmic viability in a toy simulation, not production impact.

2. **Explicit future-gate metadata.** The controller relies on knowing which downstream gates remain open at eviction time. In production, this metadata must either be exposed by the agent framework or inferred. The benchmark supplies it explicitly; gate inference accuracy is untested.

3. **Accuracy measures fact availability, not answer quality.** The primary metric records whether a required fact is still present in retained context when queried. It does not measure whether an LLM correctly uses that fact, avoids hallucination, or produces a correct final answer.

4. **Adversarial noise design.** The benchmark intentionally constructs high-salience off-task records to stress global salience. This design choice favors the task-gated approach. Real agent traces may exhibit different salience distributions, potentially narrowing the observed gap.

5. **Single benchmark implementation.** All results derive from one harness. Replication on independent implementations or real trace data is necessary before drawing general conclusions.

6. **No latency or cost measurement.** The benchmark measures retention accuracy and token counts but does not profile wall-clock latency, compute cost, or integration overhead that a production controller would impose.

7. **Mixed result on summary precision.** While the task-gated summary variant achieved equivalent accuracy with fewer tokens, its useful-record precision was substantially lower (0.478 vs. 0.665). The implications of this precision loss for downstream answer quality in real LLM contexts remain unknown.

## 6. Conclusion

In a deterministic synthetic benchmark simulating budgeted agent episodes with high-salience off-task noise, a task-gated thinking retention controller substantially outperformed FIFO and global-salience baselines on downstream query accuracy (+61.6 pp and +68.9 pp respectively). A summary variant achieved equivalent accuracy while reducing mean token consumption by approximately 6%. Global salience performed worst overall, confirming that locally important but task-irrelevant records can displace critical low-salience facts when gate structure is ignored.

These results constitute positive synthetic evidence for the viability of gate-aware retention as a prototype direction. They do not constitute a production claim. The large accuracy gap observed here may narrow substantially when evaluated against real agent traces where salience distributions are less adversarial and gate metadata must be inferred rather than supplied explicitly. Scientific closure on real-world impact requires integration with actual agent traces, measurement of end-to-end task success, and evaluation of gate inference when explicit future-gate metadata is unavailable.
