## Abstract

Split-conformal prediction provides finite-sample marginal coverage guarantees but can exhibit substantial conditional undercoverage in heterogeneous or shifted data regions. We investigate a post-hoc Uncertainty-Coverage Co-Estimator (UCCE) that uses a gradient-boosted local quantile regressor to predict per-example nonconformity thresholds from classifier features, then applies a residual split-conformal correction on a held-out calibration split. In a synthetic 3-class heteroskedastic classification task replicated across 10 seeds at 12,000 samples each, UCCE consistently reallocates the uncertainty budget toward hard examples: under distribution shift, marginal coverage improved from 0.8721 to 0.8891 and coverage error roughly halved (0.0279 → 0.0146). Hard-region coverage improved by +0.041 (iid) and +0.038 (shifted), and worst-bin undercoverage decreased correspondingly. However, these gains came at the cost of increased mean prediction-set size (+0.083 iid, +0.166 shifted), and on iid data UCCE slightly underperformed the global threshold on marginal coverage (0.8978 vs. 0.9010). A separate singleton-correctness co-estimator did not robustly outperform raw maximum probability. These results position UCCE as a conditional-coverage repair mechanism rather than an efficiency improvement, and validation on real tasks with meaningful prediction sets remains necessary.

## 1. Introduction

Split-conformal prediction produces prediction sets with finite-sample marginal coverage guarantees under exchangeability. The standard approach computes a single global nonconformity threshold from a calibration set. While this guarantees marginal coverage at the target level, the guarantee is distributional: subpopulations with intrinsically higher difficulty or noise can experience systematic undercoverage, while easier subpopulations are over-covered.

This mismatch motivates the goal of conditional coverage—approximately uniform coverage across subgroups or feature regions. Exact conditional coverage is impossible without strong distributional assumptions, but practical mechanisms can reduce conditional coverage error. Prior work has explored adaptive prediction sets and Mondrian conformal approaches that partition the feature space.

We investigate a complementary approach: a post-hoc **Uncertainty-Coverage Co-Estimator (UCCE)** that learns a local nonconformity quantile function from calibration features, then applies a residual conformal correction to preserve finite-sample coverage. The key idea is that if the local quantile estimator captures a substantial fraction of the heterogeneity in nonconformity scores, the residual conformal correction can be small, yielding prediction sets that are locally adaptive while retaining coverage guarantees.

This paper reports results from a controlled synthetic experiment designed to test whether UCCE can improve conditional coverage in settings with known heteroskedastic structure and distribution shift, and characterizes the efficiency tradeoffs involved.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Task and Data

We constructed a synthetic 3-class nonlinear classification task with heteroskedastic label noise. The data-generating process places higher label noise in a center region (hard examples) and lower noise in outer clusters (easy examples). This design creates a known conditional-coverage challenge: a global conformal threshold that achieves marginal coverage will systematically undercover the hard region.

Two test regimes were evaluated:

- **iid**: drawn from the same generator as calibration.
- **shifted_harder**: increased mass in the hard/ambiguous region plus mild label-mechanism drift, simulating distribution shift that exacerbates the coverage problem.

### 2.2 Baseline: Global Split-Conformal

The baseline method (`global_split_conformal`) computes a single nonconformity threshold from a calibration split using the score $s_i = 1 - p_{\text{true}}(x_i)$, where $p_{\text{true}}$ is the classifier's estimated probability for the true class. The threshold is set at the $\lceil (n+1)(1-\alpha) \rceil / n$ quantile of calibration scores, yielding prediction sets with marginal coverage at level $1 - \alpha$ under exchangeability.

### 2.3 UCCE: Conformalized Local Quantile

UCCE (`ucce_conformalized_local_quantile`) proceeds in two stages:

1. **Local quantile estimation.** A gradient-boosted quantile regressor is trained on a calibration split to predict the per-example nonconformity threshold from a feature vector comprising: input features $x$, class probabilities, maximum probability, probability margin, entropy, and k-nearest-neighbor distance in feature space.

2. **Residual conformal correction.** A second held-out calibration split is used to compute residuals between the true nonconformity scores and the local quantile predictions. A split-conformal correction is applied to these residuals, ensuring that the final locally-adapted thresholds retain finite-sample coverage guarantees.

This two-stage design preserves the exchangeability-based coverage guarantee while allowing the local quantile estimator to capture systematic variation in difficulty across the feature space.

### 2.4 Auxiliary Singleton Correctness Co-Estimator

As a secondary investigation, a random-forest regressor was trained to predict whether the top-1 prediction is correct (singleton correctness) from the same feature substrate. This co-estimator was evaluated against raw maximum probability using Brier score and AUROC, to assess whether a learned correctness probability adds value beyond the classifier's own confidence.

### 2.5 Evaluation Protocol

The nominal coverage target was $\alpha = 0.10$ (90% coverage). Each experiment was replicated across 10 random seeds with $n = 12{,}000$ samples per seed. Metrics reported include:

- **Marginal coverage**: fraction of test examples where the true label falls in the prediction set.
- **Coverage error**: $|\text{coverage} - 0.90|$.
- **Mean set size**: average number of labels per prediction set (efficiency).
- **Conditional coverage MAE**: mean absolute deviation of coverage across difficulty-stratified bins from the target.
- **Worst-bin undercoverage**: maximum shortfall of any bin's coverage below the target.
- **Hard/easy coverage**: coverage in the hard (center) and easy (outer) regions respectively.

Paired deltas (UCCE minus global) were computed per seed to control for seed-level variation.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Marginal and Conditional Coverage

Table 1 summarizes coverage and efficiency metrics across both test regimes.

**Table 1.** Coverage and efficiency metrics (mean ± sd over 10 seeds). Nominal coverage target: 0.900.

| Split | Method | Coverage (mean ± sd) | Coverage Error | Mean Set Size | Cond. Coverage MAE | Worst-Bin Undercoverage | Hard Coverage | Easy Coverage |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| iid | Global | 0.9010 ± 0.0050 | 0.0044 | 1.2822 | 0.0452 | 0.0678 | 0.8322 | 0.9284 |
| iid | UCCE | 0.8978 ± 0.0087 | 0.0075 | 1.3652 | 0.0392 | 0.0323 | 0.8729 | 0.9076 |
| shifted_harder | Global | 0.8721 ± 0.0074 | 0.0279 | 1.3819 | 0.0527 | 0.0620 | 0.8380 | 0.9372 |
| shifted_harder | UCCE | 0.8891 ± 0.0118 | 0.0146 | 1.5475 | 0.0366 | 0.0347 | 0.8764 | 0.9132 |

Under iid conditions, the global threshold achieves marginal coverage slightly above target (0.9010), while UCCE falls marginally below (0.8978). However, UCCE substantially improves hard-region coverage (+0.0407) and reduces worst-bin undercoverage (0.0323 vs. 0.0678) and conditional coverage MAE (0.0392 vs. 0.0452).

Under distribution shift, the global threshold fails to maintain marginal coverage (0.8721, a shortfall of 0.0279 from target). UCCE recovers marginal coverage to 0.8891, cutting coverage error approximately in half (0.0146 vs. 0.0279). Hard-region coverage improves by +0.0384, and worst-bin undercoverage decreases from 0.0620 to 0.0347.

### 3.3 Auxiliary Singleton Correctness Co-Estimator

The separate singleton-correctness regressor did not robustly outperform raw maximum probability:

- **iid**: Brier score improved marginally (Δ = −0.00057), but AUROC decreased (Δ = −0.00369).
- **shifted_harder**: Brier score worsened slightly (Δ = +0.00064), and AUROC decreased (Δ = −0.00372).

These differences are small in magnitude and inconsistent in direction, providing no evidence that a learned correctness probability adds value beyond the classifier's own confidence signal in this setting.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Synthetic benchmark only.** All results derive from a synthetic 3-class heteroskedastic classification task. No real model, LLM calibration trace, or production dataset was used. The heteroskedastic structure was designed to favor local adaptation; performance on real tasks with different difficulty structures is unknown.

2. **Limited baseline comparison.** UCCE was compared only against a global split-conformal threshold. Adaptive conformal methods and Mondrian conformal approaches that partition the feature space were not evaluated. The relative merits of UCCE versus these established methods remain untested.

3. **Efficiency cost.** UCCE is not a free efficiency improvement. Mean prediction-set size increased in all conditions, particularly under distribution shift (+0.166 labels per example). Whether this cost is acceptable depends on a downstream utility function that was not modeled.

4. **Auxiliary co-estimator showed no benefit.** The singleton-correctness regressor did not robustly improve over raw maximum probability. The positive signal in this work is specific to conformalized local thresholding, not to generic learned correctness probabilities.

5. **Marginal coverage under iid conditions.** On iid data, UCCE's marginal coverage (0.8978) fell slightly below the global method (0.9010) and the nominal target (0.900). While the conditional coverage improvement may justify this trade in some applications, it represents a real cost in settings where marginal coverage is the primary objective.

6. **Feature substrate dependence.** The local quantile estimator uses features including k-nearest-neighbor distance, which may not scale efficiently to high-dimensional or streaming settings. The sensitivity of UCCE to feature choice was not systematically explored.

## 6. Conclusion

A conformalized local nonconformity-quantile co-estimator (UCCE) was evaluated on a synthetic heteroskedastic classification task across 10 seeds and two distribution regimes. The central finding is that UCCE consistently reallocates the uncertainty budget toward hard examples, improving conditional coverage at the cost of larger prediction sets.

Under distribution shift, UCCE improved marginal coverage from 0.872 to 0.889 and halved coverage error. In both regimes, hard-region coverage improved by approximately +0.04 and worst-bin undercoverage decreased by 0.028–0.036. These are meaningful improvements in the uniformity of coverage across difficulty strata.

However, UCCE is not an efficiency improvement: mean set sizes increased by 0.08–0.17 labels per example, and on iid data the global threshold achieved marginally better marginal coverage. The separate singleton-correctness co-estimator provided no robust benefit over raw maximum probability.

These results support a constrained claim: UCCE is viable as a **conditional-coverage repair mechanism** when the goal is to reduce systematic undercoverage in heterogeneous or shifted regions, provided the downstream application can tolerate larger prediction sets on hard examples. It is not viable as a general efficiency improvement.

The critical next step is validation on real tasks with meaningful prediction sets—such as QA answer candidates, classification labels, retrieval candidates, or LLM selective prediction—where the coverage/efficiency tradeoff has concrete utility consequences. Comparison against adaptive and Mondrian conformal baselines is necessary to establish whether UCCE's local quantile approach offers advantages over existing conditional-coverage methods.
