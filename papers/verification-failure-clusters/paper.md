## Abstract

Standard evaluation of verifier-guarded systems reports aggregate accuracy, which can obscure structurally coherent failure modes that affect specific subpopulations. We investigate whether clustering verifier rejection reasons into interpretable failure families, then re-weighting evaluation across those clusters, changes system rankings and exposes actionable weaknesses. In a synthetic pilot with 3,000 weakly labeled examples across four domains and three guard systems, cluster-balanced evaluation reversed the top-ranked system (aggregate winner: `balanced_guard`; cluster-balanced winner: `reasoning_guard`). A simulated targeted fix on the dominant failure cluster (`syntax_guard` on `long_range_dependency`) yielded a 5.2-point cluster-balanced uplift. In a real-log validation with 1,000 examples, 18 systems, and 18,000 prediction rows from existing verifier and model-output logs, cluster balancing again shifted at least one pairwise ranking: `tail_512` rose above `boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256` despite lower aggregate accuracy (0.600 vs. 0.700), because `tail_512` was perfect on near/mid/long-range dependency clusters while `boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256` was substantially weaker on long-range dependency. Bootstrap Kendall tau stability was high for both evaluation modes (mean τ ≥ 0.81 in the synthetic pilot; mean τ ≥ 0.97 in the real-log validation), though the 5th-percentile τ dropped to 0.33 in the synthetic pilot, indicating meaningful ranking instability under resampling for that setting. These results support the hypothesis that cluster-balanced evaluation reveals ranking changes and failure surfaces that aggregate scoring partly hides, but the evidence is bounded to the tested domains and does not constitute universal validation.

## 1. Introduction

When verifier-guarded systems are evaluated by aggregate accuracy over a mixed dataset, performance on large subpopulations can dominate the score and mask systematic failures on smaller but structurally coherent groups. If two systems have similar aggregate accuracy but different failure profiles, a practitioner choosing between them may miss an actionable weakness in one system that the other avoids.

We propose and test a simple mechanism: (1) assign each verification rejection to an interpretable failure cluster based on the rejection reason, (2) compute per-cluster accuracy for each candidate system, and (3) re-weight evaluation so that each cluster contributes equally to the final score. If cluster-balanced ranking differs from aggregate ranking, the difference identifies failure surfaces that aggregate scoring obscures.

This report presents two passes of evidence. The first is a synthetic minimum-viable-product (MVP) pilot with 3,000 weakly labeled examples and deterministic verifiers. The second is a real-log validation that reuses existing verifier and model-output logs with 1,000 examples and 18 systems. Both passes show a ranking shift under cluster balancing and expose an interpretable long-range dependency weakness. We report these findings with their limitations honestly: the synthetic pilot uses weak labels, the real-log validation covers only a long-context/evidence-position benchmark, and neither constitutes production validation.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Synthetic MVP Pilot

The synthetic pilot (`src/verification_failure_clusters.py`) generates 3,000 weakly labeled examples across four domains:

| Domain | Count |
|---|---|
| `structured_extraction` | 1,268 |
| `speculative_decoding` | 844 |
| `abstention_calibration` | 465 |
| `code_navigation` | 423 |

Deterministic verifiers emit rejection reasons for each example. Rejection reasons are mapped to seven challenge clusters: `syntax_or_format` (838), `semantic_value` (575), `schema_contract` (545), `scope_resolution` (302), `abstention_policy` (283), `long_range_dependency` (275), and `confidence_calibration` (182).

Three guard systems are evaluated: `balanced_guard`, `reasoning_guard`, and `syntax_guard`. For each system, we compute:

- **Aggregate accuracy**: fraction of all examples accepted (correctly passed or correctly rejected).
- **Cluster-balanced accuracy**: mean of per-cluster accuracies, weighting each cluster equally regardless of size.

Rankings under the two schemes are compared. Bootstrap resampling (1,000 iterations) estimates Kendall tau stability for each ranking.

A targeted fix simulation selects the worst system–cluster cell from the cluster report, repairs a fraction of rejected examples in that cell, and measures the resulting uplift in both aggregate and cluster-balanced accuracy.

### 2.2 Real-Log Validation

The real-log validation (`src/real_log_cluster_analysis.py`) ingests existing prediction logs from a separate project:

- Baseline/retrieval verifier log: `baseline_natural_pilot_1000_summary_predictions.jsonl`
- Real Qwen 2.5 3B output log: `qwen25_3b_full_1000_summary_predictions.jsonl`

These are normalized into 18,000 prediction rows spanning 1,000 examples and 18 systems/methods. Challenge clusters are defined by evidence position: `in_window_evidence` (400), `long_range_dependency` (200), `mid_range_dependency` (200), and `near_boundary_dependency` (200).

Two oracle/control methods (`full_context_oracle`, `final_keyword_retriever_top1`) are excluded from ranking comparisons. The same aggregate-vs-cluster-balanced comparison and bootstrap stability analysis are applied.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Synthetic MVP Pilot

**Ranking comparison.** Aggregate accuracy ranked the systems as `balanced_guard` (0.827) > `reasoning_guard` (0.818) > `syntax_guard` (0.780). Cluster-balanced accuracy reversed the top two: `reasoning_guard` (0.824) > `balanced_guard` (0.816) > `syntax_guard` (0.718). The ranking changed between the two evaluation modes.

**Bootstrap stability.** Mean Kendall tau was 0.900 for aggregate ranking and 0.813 for cluster-balanced ranking. The 5th-percentile Kendall tau was 0.333 for both, indicating that under some resamples, ranking order is unstable for both evaluation modes in this small-sample setting.

**Failure cluster coverage.** All three guard systems produced rejections in all seven clusters (coverage = 7/7 for each).

**Targeted fix simulation.** The worst cell was `syntax_guard` on `long_range_dependency`, with pre-fix cluster accuracy of 0.5345. At a repair rate of 0.7, 122 of 160 eligible rejected examples were repaired. This produced a cluster-balanced uplift of 5.2 points and an aggregate uplift of 4.07 points.

### 3.2 Real-Log Validation

**Dataset.** 1,000 examples, 18 systems, 18,000 prediction rows. Rejection counts by cluster: `in_window_evidence` (2,120), `long_range_dependency` (1,299), `mid_range_dependency` (1,066), `near_boundary_dependency` (1,078).

**Ranking comparison (top 12, excluding oracle/control methods).**

| Rank | Aggregate | Cluster-Balanced |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `tail_1024` | `tail_1024` |
| 2 | `tail_2048` | `tail_2048` |
| 3 | `tail_4096` | `tail_4096` |
| 4 | `qwen25_3b_instruct_real_output` | `qwen25_3b_instruct_real_output` |
| 5 | `boundary_probe_4096_lookahead256` | `boundary_probe_4096_lookahead256` |
| 6 | `lexical_retriever_top1` | `lexical_retriever_top1` |
| 7 | `truncate_4096` | `truncate_4096` |
| 8 | `sklearn_tfidf_char_wb_top1` | `sklearn_tfidf_char_wb_top1` |
| 9 | `boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256` | **`tail_512`** |
| 10 | `tail_512` | **`boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256`** |
| 11 | `truncate_2048` | `truncate_2048` |
| 12 | `boundary_probe_1024_lookahead256` | `boundary_probe_1024_lookahead256` |

The ranking changed: positions 9 and 10 swapped. `tail_512` rose above `boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256` under cluster balancing despite lower aggregate accuracy (0.600 vs. 0.700). Under cluster-balanced evaluation, `tail_512` scored 0.750 vs. 0.688 for `boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256`.

**Interpretability of the shift.** `tail_512` achieved perfect accuracy on near/mid/long-range dependency clusters but failed on in-window evidence. `boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256` was smoother across clusters but substantially weaker on long-range dependency. Cluster balancing revealed that the long-range dependency weakness is large enough to reverse the pairwise ranking.

**Worst non-control cluster cell.** `boundary_probe_512_lookahead256` on `long_range_dependency` achieved 0.0 accuracy — a complete failure on that cluster that aggregate scoring partly conceals.

**Bootstrap stability.** Mean Kendall tau was 0.978 for aggregate ranking and 0.976 for cluster-balanced ranking. The 5th-percentile Kendall tau was 0.948 for aggregate and 0.961 for cluster-balanced, indicating high ranking stability in this setting for both evaluation modes.

## 4. Limitations

1. **Synthetic/weak labels.** The MVP pilot uses 3,000 synthetic or weakly labeled examples with deterministic verifiers. The relationship between these verifiers and production verification logic is not established.

2. **Domain coverage.** The real-log validation covers only a long-context/evidence-position benchmark. The original proposal spanned four domains (structured extraction, code navigation, abstention calibration, speculative decoding), but the real-log pass tested only evidence-position clusters. Whether the ranking-shift mechanism generalizes to other task domains remains unknown.

3. **Ranking shift magnitude.** In the real-log validation, only one pairwise ranking position changed (positions 9–10 of 12). The top 8 positions were identical under both evaluation modes. The ranking shift is real but modest in this setting.

4. **Bootstrap instability in the synthetic pilot.** The 5th-percentile Kendall tau of 0.333 in the synthetic pilot indicates that both aggregate and cluster-balanced rankings are unstable under resampling at that sample size. The pilot's ranking reversal should be interpreted cautiously.

5. **Targeted fix is simulated, not implemented.** The 5.2-point uplift assumes a 70% repair rate on rejected examples, which is an idealized assumption. Actual repair rates in production may differ substantially.

6. **No external replication.** All results come from a single project's artifacts. No independent replication on different datasets, models, or hardware has been performed.

7. **Cluster definitions are hand-specified.** The mapping from rejection reasons to clusters, and the evidence-position cluster definitions in the real-log pass, were designed by the experimenter. Different cluster definitions could yield different rankings.

8. **No production validation.** Neither pass constitutes final production validation. The results are from a prototype harness and existing log files.

## 6. Conclusion

Two evaluation passes — a synthetic MVP pilot and a real-log validation — support the hypothesis that cluster-balanced evaluation changes system rankings and exposes actionable failure surfaces that aggregate scoring partly hides. In the synthetic pilot, the top-ranked system reversed under cluster balancing, and a simulated targeted fix on the dominant failure cluster produced a 5.2-point cluster-balanced uplift. In the real-log validation, cluster balancing elevated `tail_512` above `boundary_probe_2048_lookahead256` by revealing a long-range dependency weakness, and identified a system (`boundary_probe_512_lookahead256`) with zero accuracy on that cluster.

These findings are bounded. The ranking shift in the real-log validation affected only one pairwise comparison among the lower-ranked systems. The synthetic pilot showed high ranking instability under bootstrap resampling. The real-log validation covered only one of four originally proposed task domains. The targeted fix was simulated rather than implemented.

The current project artifacts support the finding in the tested setting. Whether cluster-balanced evaluation produces meaningful ranking changes across broader task domains, larger model populations, and production verification pipelines remains an open question. The project decision recommends packaging the synthetic and real-log harnesses as a reusable cluster-balanced evaluator and applying them to the next real verifier-log dataset with broader task domains.
